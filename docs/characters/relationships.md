## The Gladstone–McAteer Seychelles Connection

### The Family Geography

**The McAteer family home: Glacis, Mahé**
Bill McAteer and Juliette retired to the Seychelles and lived in Glacis — a small community in northern Mahé, close to Beau Vallon beach. This was their permanent retirement home. Edmond (Juliette's brother) lives on Cerf Island; McAteer's private study is preserved there. The family therefore had two Seychelles locations: the Glacis house (main home, family gatherings) and Cerf Island (Edmond's family seat, McAteer's study).

**Annual winter holidays**
From early childhood, Aimee and Chloe travelled to the Seychelles every Australian winter (June–August) for up to six weeks. They stayed at the Glacis house with Bill and Juliette. They went to Beau Vallon beach. They explored the islands — often by boat. Adam came sometimes; other times he remained in Australia for work. These annual visits gave Aimee and Chloe a childhood geography of Mahé that is more extensive and more deeply embedded than a single year at school would produce.

**Significance:**
- Beau Vallon is not a strange beach to Aimee — she has been going there since she could walk
- The family knew Amit and Sea Shell from these regular visits
- The Glacis house is in northern Mahé, close to Bel Ombre — the girls may have walked that stretch of coastline as children without knowing what the granite held
- **Aimee and Adam at Bel Ombre:** As a child, Aimee and Adam slipped through the security fencing and scrambled the granite rocks at Bel Ombre together, looking for pirate treasure. This childhood adventure is the novel's most important structural echo: in Act 3 they return to that exact coastline, now knowing what is beneath the granite. [This is CANON — structurally essential]
- When Aimee hears the rhyme fragment at Beau Vallon in Chapter 10, it is a homecoming in every possible sense

**The 2008 year**
The year-long stay (Juliette's cancer diagnosis + GFC) was different from the holidays — the girls attended local school, lived as residents rather than visitors. This is when they moved from knowing the Seychelles to having it. The regular holidays gave them familiarity; the 2008 year gave them rootedness.
