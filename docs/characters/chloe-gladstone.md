# CHARACTER BIBLE — CHLOE GLADSTONE
## The Eden Cipher / The Gladstone Files Series
*Canonical reference document. Details marked [CANON] are established fact. Details marked [EDITORIAL] are craft suggestions, non-canonical until confirmed.*

---

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Chloe Juliette Gladstone is the eldest daughter of Adam and Brigitte and the emotional anchor of the Gladstone family.

Where Adam is the visionary, Brigitte the organiser, and Aimee the adventurer, Chloe is the calm head that people instinctively turn to when life becomes difficult. She is thoughtful, emotionally intelligent, and deeply caring. Although naturally cautious — she evaluates risks carefully and prefers informed decisions over instinct — she possesses quiet courage and remarkable resilience under pressure.

Her career as a paediatric nurse has sharpened her into something that looks, from the outside, simply like kindness, but is in fact a highly trained capacity: to observe acutely, to remember everything, and to remain functional when others cannot.

Her most specific gift — and the one that will matter to the investigation — is her exceptional memory for people. She remembers names, faces, the small details of conversations, emotional textures, the thing someone said once in passing. As a nurse this is craft. In the Seychelles it will become something more.

She begins the novel believing that safety comes from avoiding unnecessary risk. By the end she understands that courage is not the absence of fear but the willingness to act despite it.

## 2. PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION

**[CANON]**
- Tall
- Athletic build, broad shoulders from years of swimming
- Fair blonde hair
- Blue eyes
- Natural healthy appearance
- Casual but neat dresser — more organised than Aimee
- **No tattoos** — would never get one, which she occasionally mentions when the subject of Aimee's gecko comes up

**[EDITORIAL]**
Where Aimee's presence is energy-in-motion, Chloe's is contained and deliberate. She moves like someone who has learned to stay calm in rooms where calm is required. Her hands are nurse's hands — still when they need to be, precise when they act. The no-tattoo point matters as characterisation: she commits to things slowly and carefully. The gecko on Aimee's skin represents a kind of impulsive certainty that Chloe admires and slightly envies.

## 3. BIOGRAPHY

**[CANON]**
- **Full name:** Chloe Juliette Gladstone
- **Middle name:** Juliette — named after her grandmother Juliette McAteer
- **Born:** 1 January 2000, Manly Hospital, Sydney, Australia
  - Born during Sydney's Millennium celebrations. Adam and Brigitte watched the New Year's Eve fireworks over Sydney Harbour through the hospital ward windows. She arrived into the first hours of the new century.
- **Raised:** Sydney's Northern Beaches — family home close to the beach
- Eldest daughter of Adam and Brigitte Gladstone
- Older sister of Aimee Gladstone (born 9 July 2002 — two years and six months younger)

**[CANON — Annual Seychelles Holidays]**
From infancy, Chloe travelled to the Seychelles approximately every year with Brigitte and Aimee — staying at the McAteer family home in Glacis, northern Mahé. Adam joined when work permitted; sometimes he stayed in Australia. These trips lasted up to six weeks during the Australian winter. She grew up treating the Seychelles not as a holiday destination but as a second home.

**[CANON — The 2008–2009 Seychelles Year]**
During the Global Financial Crisis, the family moved to the Seychelles for a full year. Chloe was nine. Aimee was seven. They attended the Independent School in Victoria, Mahé, where teaching was in English. Chloe learned conversational Creole from classmates, teachers, and her grandmother Juliette. She and Aimee were the only blonde-haired girls at school and quickly became well known. She loved living in Seychelles and developed lifelong friendships and a deep affection for the islands.

**[CANON — Bel Ombre]**
She visited Bel Ombre with Adam and Aimee during childhood. She was less captivated by the treasure story itself than by the simple pleasure of being somewhere difficult and beautiful with her family. She remembers the visit differently from Aimee — as a family afternoon rather than as a moment of revelation. This distinction matters in Act 3.

## 4. PSYCHOLOGICAL PROFILE

**[CANON]**

**Greatest fear:** Making the wrong decision when someone depends on her. She worries about letting people down — her parents especially. This fear is not weakness; it is the pressure she applies to herself to be good enough for the trust others place in her.

**Core belief:** That caring for people is the most important thing a person can do. She does not say this. She demonstrates it continuously.

**Internal conflict:** She constantly worries about making the wrong decision. She carries responsibility willingly — it is her natural shape — but sometimes forgets to care for herself. She wants to protect everyone she loves and occasionally treats this as a job with no off-shift.

**External conflict:** She arrives in the Seychelles on what is meant to be a family holiday. The investigation finds her, not the other way around. Once she is inside it, her skills — medical calm, exceptional memory, the ability to read people under stress — become essential in ways she did not anticipate.

**False belief:** That safety comes from avoiding unnecessary risk. By the end of the novel she has had to act without a plan, and discovered that she is capable of it.

**Relationship with her own Creole knowledge:** She has good conversational Creole and uses it confidently. She knows *Ti chat ki pe dòmi* — she learned it at school, sang it in the playground. She can recite every word. She does not know, until the moment at the second door, that knowing the words and having the sequence in your body are different things. When she sees what Aimee does, she understands immediately — with the speed and completeness of someone watching a diagnosis confirm itself.

## 5. PERSONALITY ANALYSIS

**[CANON]**

Kind. Compassionate. Responsible. Thoughtful. Patient. Emotionally intelligent. Independent. Determined. Loyal. Protective. Gentle. Calm under pressure.

She is naturally cautious and likes to think things through before acting. She evaluates risks carefully and prefers making informed decisions. She is quietly confident rather than loud or attention-seeking. She is proud to be a Gladstone and values family above almost everything else.

She is not the boldest person in any room — but she is often the most reliable. When panic begins to move through a group, Chloe is the counterforce. She does not perform composure; she is composed.

**[CANON — Weaknesses]**
- Overthinks decisions
- Worries about making mistakes
- Can be overly cautious
- Places enormous pressure on herself
- Finds it difficult to disappoint people
- Sometimes struggles to relax because she feels responsible for everyone else

## 6. CHARACTER VOICE

**[CANON]**
Warm, measured, and precise. She chooses her words carefully — a habit from nursing, where the wrong phrase at the wrong moment can undo minutes of careful calm. She does not speak in Adam's register (analytical, building arguments) or Aimee's (sensory, associative) but in her own: clear, human, and focused on the person in front of her rather than the problem.

Her humour is quiet and dry — noticed by those paying attention, easy to miss if you're not. She does not use humour to deflect; she uses it to say true things gently.

When she is worried she becomes quieter, not louder. She asks questions — specific, small questions that seem incidental — instead of saying what she is actually thinking.

**[EDITORIAL — Dialogue style]**
Chloe speaks in shorter sentences than Adam and complete ones, unlike Aimee's associative tangents. She tends to answer a question before asking one of her own. She uses people's names — a nursing habit, a humanising gesture. She says *"I think"* before an opinion and means it literally.

**[EDITORIAL — Signature tendency]**
When something worries her and she has not yet named it, she can be found sitting quietly, separating individual hairs and checking for split ends. The family all recognise this. Nobody mentions it to her directly. It is her version of Adam's golf putting — a body signal that the mind is elsewhere.

**[EDITORIAL — Sample lines]**
- *"I'm not saying don't. I'm saying tell me what we do if it goes wrong."*
- *"He said that to you? When?"* — and then she remembers exactly when.
- To Aimee, after the second door: *"You knew, didn't you. Since Beau Vallon."*
- *"I'm fine."* (She is not fine. Everyone knows this.)
- *"What do you need right now?"* — asked in any crisis, to any person. The nurse's reflex.

## 7. HABITS AND MANNERISMS

**[CANON]**
- **Split ends:** Sits absent-mindedly pulling individual hairs apart looking for split ends. The habit annoys the rest of the family. It is most visible when she is anxious or thinking through something she hasn't said aloud.
- **Asks small specific questions** when she is actually processing something larger
- Cooks when she needs to decompress — methodical, generous cooking, not performative
- Loves quiet evenings watching films or television after busy shifts — a blanket, a film, the weight of a day put down
- Exceptionally tidy compared to Aimee, which is not a high bar

**[CANON — Unique Ability: Exceptional Memory for People]**
Chloe possesses an exceptional memory for people. She remembers names, faces, conversations, tiny personal details, family stories, and emotional moments. As a paediatric nurse she has spent years training this capacity — because a child who mentioned something last Tuesday matters today, and the parent who said something worried three months ago may be saying the same thing again, if you're listening.

This ability becomes valuable in the investigation in ways that are not immediately obvious. She is the one who remembers a face from the marina. She is the one who recalls what Edmond said on Tuesday that was actually about Wednesday. She is the one who connects the name of Viktor's associate to a conversation held before anyone knew it mattered.

## 8. RELATIONSHIPS

**[CANON]**

**Adam Gladstone (father):**
She deeply admires his intelligence and kindness. She also worries about him — his physical fearlessness has flipped since the water incident into something that costs him, and she sees it. She wishes he would slow down and look after himself occasionally. Adam, for his part, admires Chloe enormously and wishes she would take more risks. He cannot say no to her and never has been able to. She is aware of this and tries not to use it.

**Brigitte Gladstone (mother):**
Brigitte is Chloe's role model. She has inherited much of Brigitte's sense of responsibility, her compassion, and her organisational instincts. When difficult decisions need to be made, Chloe naturally looks to Brigitte first — not for permission but for orientation. Brigitte and Chloe share a register that is different from Brigitte and Aimee's: practical, direct, occasionally combative, always honest.

**Aimee Gladstone (sister):**
The sisters are extremely close. Their personalities are very different — Chloe is cautious, Aimee is adventurous — and this has been the texture of their relationship since childhood. There is constant good-natured sibling rivalry. Aimee frequently borrows Chloe's clothes without asking, which irritates Chloe enormously. Chloe wishes Aimee would show a little more respect for her advice as the older sister. Aimee is aware of this and is strategic about when she shows she has listened. Despite all of this, Chloe would do absolutely anything to protect Aimee. The scene at the second door — Chloe standing beside her younger sister, knowing every word of the rhyme, unable to do what Aimee does — is the moment she understands something she has circled her whole life without naming.

**Callum (partner):**
Chloe is in a committed long-term relationship with Callum, a teacher. Their relationship is stable, loving, and supportive. Marriage is a likely future step. Callum is not in the Seychelles for the events of this novel; he exists as an anchor at home — someone she calls, someone she has to explain things to that she cannot fully explain. His absence is part of what makes her decisions her own.

**Edmond Beaumont:**
She respects him immediately — she is good with older people who carry things in the way he carries things, and her nursing experience has taught her to hear what is being said obliquely. She is sometimes ahead of Adam in understanding what Edmond is communicating, though she tends to let Adam arrive at it himself.

**Raymonde:**
She reads Raymonde's eccentricity as competence in disguise — the same way she might read a difficult patient whose apparent chaotic presentation is actually very clear if you know how to look. She is fond of Raymonde quickly and trusts her.

**Viktor Volkov:**
She notices him with the specificity of someone who has spent years reading people in controlled and high-stakes environments. She cannot name what she distrusts — but she files it. When Chloe goes quiet about someone, that silence is worth attending to.

## 11. INTERNAL CONFLICT

**[CANON]**
She carries responsibility as a natural shape — it is how she moves through the world — but she has built safety from it too. Risk means the possibility of being wrong; being wrong means letting someone down. The investigation in the Seychelles asks her repeatedly to act without certainty, and each time she does, she discovers that the person she has protected most carefully is herself. The arc resolves not in a dramatic moment but in a series of small choices to move forward before she is ready.

---

## 12. EXTERNAL CONFLICT

**[CANON]**
She arrives in the Seychelles on an annual family holiday. The investigation arrives around her. Her medical skills are immediately practical — she is the person equipped to manage physical crisis, to assess injury, to stay useful when the situation deteriorates. Her exceptional memory becomes investigatively critical in ways the reader pieces together alongside the characters. Her relationship with Aimee reaches its defining point at the second door.

## 13. CHARACTER ARC

**[CANON]**

**Opening:** She is the family's emotional anchor — dependable, loving, careful. She is the one making sure everyone is all right. This is her natural role and she is excellent at it, though it means she is often watching other people's moments rather than having her own.

**During:** The investigation makes increasing demands on her that are outside her prepared categories. She has to act on incomplete information. She has to trust her instincts about people rather than observed data. She has to stand beside Aimee at the second door and discover, in real time, the difference between knowing and having.

**The second door:** This is Chloe's most important scene. She stands beside Aimee knowing every word of *Ti chat ki pe dòmi*. She has known them since she was nine. She cannot perform the sequence. Watching Aimee's body do what Chloe's cannot, she understands something that no amount of thinking could have produced. She does not say what she understands. She does not need to.

Her line — *"You knew, didn't you. Since Beau Vallon."* — is the first complete thought she says after.

**Ending:** She goes back to her life: Callum, the ward, the work she loves. But she goes back having understood something she hadn't before — about what her sister carries, about the difference between knowing and having, about the particular kind of courage required to act when you are not certain. She is still the emotional anchor. She is also, now, something more.

## 17. EDITORIAL SUGGESTIONS

*[NON-CANONICAL UNTIL AUTHOR CONFIRMS]*

**[EDITORIAL 1 — Dialogue style in full]**
Chloe speaks in shorter, complete sentences. She answers before asking. She uses names — a nursing habit, a humanising gesture. She says *"I think"* before an opinion because she means it literally. In a crisis she becomes more precise, not less — the opposite of panic. Her speech under pressure is quieter, not louder, and more specific.

**[EDITORIAL 2 — Signature phrases]**
- *"What do you need right now?"* — the nurse's reflex; said in any crisis, to any person
- *"I'm not saying don't. I'm saying tell me what we do if it goes wrong."*
- *"I remember him."* — said at the right moment, about someone nobody else has connected
- *"She's all right."* — Chloe's assessment carries more weight than anyone else's, because she is trained to know

**[EDITORIAL 3 — Humour]**
Dry and quiet. She notices absurdity and names it once, briefly, then moves on. Her best lines are the ones people realise were funny three beats after she's said them. She never repeats a joke. She does not perform warmth — the warmth is always already there.

**[EDITORIAL 4 — Emotional scenes]**
Chloe is at her most emotionally revealing not in speech but in what she does. When she is frightened she moves closer to the people she is protecting. When she is proud she goes quiet. When she is hurt she asks a practical question to avoid the real one. The writer should use action and silence more than dialogue to convey her inner state.

**[EDITORIAL 5 — Nursing experience in the investigation]**
Her nursing skills create specific practical moments:
- She is the person who notices that someone is more injured than they are admitting
- She assesses physical and psychological stress in the group accurately and adjusts accordingly
- In a situation of physical danger, she is functionally calm when trained responses take over — she may be frightened, but the hands know what to do
- Her memory for people surfaces at key moments: a face at the marina connected to a boat she noticed; a name said in passing by Edmond that she places three chapters later; a detail from a conversation with Raymonde that connects something Adam has been looking at from the wrong angle

**[EDITORIAL 6 — The split ends habit]**
This habit is best deployed at moments where the scene's surface calm is in tension with what Chloe is actually processing. The family recognises it; when one of them sees her doing it, the reader understands she is worried before she says so. It is the physical tell she cannot control and does not know she has.

**[EDITORIAL 7 — The second door scene]**
Chloe does not try to perform the sequence. She stands beside Aimee and watches. She has known the words for twenty years. The moment is not painful in the way failure is painful — it is clarifying. She sees the difference between what she has and what Aimee has, and she understands, in the way she understands medical diagnoses, that it is not a judgement. It is a fact. She hands the moment to Aimee without cost. This is her most generous act in the novel and it is silent.
