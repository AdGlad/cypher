## 1. VERIFIED FACTS

*(Full research: `/Users/adglad/git/cypher/research/la-buse-levasseur-research.md`)*

**Biography**
- Born: Calais, France — one French source cites 5 November 1695 from a surviving birth record
- Privateer during the War of the Spanish Succession; turned pirate after Treaty of Utrecht (1714)
- Eye patch: from a war wound (documented), not affectation
- Caribbean 1716–1718: alliance with Hornigold, Bellamy; captured ~50 ships
- West Africa 1718–1720: alliance with Cocklyn and Davis; attack on Portuguese fort at Ouidah
- Shipwrecked in Mozambique Channel; rescued by Edward England — origin of the England-Levasseur alliance
- Operated from Île Sainte-Marie (Nosy Boraha), Madagascar, from 1720

**The Name**
- **La Buse** = "The Buzzard" (French) — correct, primary, used by French maritime historians
- **La Buze**: McAteer's spelling; attested in French colonial documents — historically defensible
- **La Bouche** = "The Mouth" — some sources; probably mis-transcription
- **O Falcão** = "The Falcon" in Portuguese — the Viceroy's own report uses this
- **Novel decision:** Use "La Buze" throughout — consistent with McAteer (historically defensible)

**The Nossa Senhora do Cabo Heist (April 1721)**
- Full ship name: *Nossa Senhora do Cabo e São Pedro de Alcântara* — 72-gun Dutch-built Portuguese flagship
- Three pirate captains: Levasseur (*Victory*), Taylor (*Cassandra/Fancy*), Jasper Seagar — confirmed in the Viceroy's own account
- The ship had jettisoned all 72 cannons in a storm; was completely defenceless when attacked at Réunion
- Viceroy (Count of Ericeira) ransomed for 2,000 rix-dollars; returned to Portugal in disgrace, banished from court for 10 years
- Value: £800,000–£1,000,000 in 1721; approximately $138 million in 2025 terms
- Each crew member reportedly received £50,000 + 42 diamonds
- The Flaming Cross of Goa (Fiery Cross): studded with diamonds, rubies, emeralds — reportedly so large three men were needed to lift it
- Robert Louis Stevenson used this heist: Long John Silver references "the Viceroy of the East Indies" in *Treasure Island*

**Settlement in the Seychelles**
- Levasseur settled secretly in the Seychelles circa 1724–1728 — historically documented
- He rejected the 1724 amnesty rather than return the treasure
- Four years on the islands — time enough to build something permanent

**Arrest and Execution**
- Captured while working as a harbour pilot at Antongil Bay, Madagascar (c.1729)
- Hanged at **Saint-Paul** on Réunion (NOT Saint-Denis — a common error in secondary sources)
- Date: **7 July 1730, at 5 PM** — precisely documented
- Last words (reported, no contemporary source): "Find my treasure, he who can understand it" — or variant
- The necklace-throwing story (throwing an encoded message to the crowd): **no contemporary source**; first appears in La Roncière's 1934 book

**The Fake Grave**
- The supposed "grave" in Saint-Paul cemetery is fake
- The tombstone actually belongs to **Delphine Hélod**, a freed enslaved woman who died in 1836
- Discovered as fake in 2010

## 2. THE CIPHER — CRITICAL FOR THE NOVEL

**Origin and Authenticity**
- The 17-line cryptogram was **first published in 1934** by historian Charles de La Roncière — *Les Secrets de la "Bouche" forban* — published as a novel
- **No contemporary attestation exists** — no 18th-century source refers to any cipher
- The execution speech and necklace-throwing: **no contemporary source** — first appears in La Roncière 1934
- La Roncière's book was published as fiction, blurring the research/fiction boundary
- The decoded text produces **jumbled French resembling folk recipes** — not a treasure map
- A second 22-line cryptogram: a **proven modern fake**, likely fabricated by photographer Yannick Benaben in the late 20th century
- **Academic consensus (2025):** the cipher is probably a 20th-century literary invention, possibly built on a fragment of genuine Réunion oral tradition

**The "bad copy" problem**
- Reginald Cruise-Wilkins worked from a poor copy of the cipher for decades at Bel Ombre
- This provides in-world reason why Viktor's AURORA can only produce a 60% solution — she is working from the same degraded source material

**Novel implication (fundamental)**
The novel can treat the cipher as in-world genuine while the scholarly uncertainty deepens the data-vs-understanding theme: Viktor's AI produces a 60% solution of a document that may itself be fabricated. The unreliable document is perfectly suited to an investigation about the limits of analytical tools.

**The fictional cipher in the novel**
The cipher in the novel is in-world a genuine document — Levasseur's actual navigation tool for the interior of the complex. Its in-world authenticity is not in question within the plot. Its historical authenticity (in the real world) provides the sophisticated reader with an additional layer of irony: Viktor is investing enormous resources in a document that is probably fake, to navigate an interior whose entrance he cannot find, toward an archive whose existence he cannot confirm. His confidence is the wrong kind of confidence.

## 3. THE 2025 ARCHAEOLOGICAL DISCOVERY

**Critical for the novel's contemporary setting**

In **July 2025**, the *Nossa Senhora do Cabo* wreck was confirmed at Nosy Boraha (Île Sainte-Marie), Madagascar:
- 3,300+ artifacts recovered: gold coins, Chinese porcelain, religious objects, ship's bell
- The Flaming Cross of Goa was **NOT found** — consistent with Levasseur personally removing it before the ship was taken as a prize
- Discovery made by Barry Clifford's team (the same maritime archaeologist who found the *Whydah* in 1984)

**Novel implications:**
- The novel is set in 2026 — this discovery happened one year before the story begins
- Viktor's AURORA will have ingested it. Adam will have read about it. Père Henri will have seen it on the news.
- The discovery provides contemporary academic validation for the entire search
- The missing Cross is the strongest available argument that Levasseur retained and hid something
- SYBIL can cite the discovery and its implications (the absent Cross) with full confidence

## 4. CONNECTIONS AND CULTURAL LEGACY

**Robert Louis Stevenson:** *Treasure Island*'s Long John Silver explicitly references "the Viceroy of the East Indies" — Stevenson knew this story. This is the most famous treasure novel ever written, drawing directly on Levasseur's heist. Viktor will know this. Adam will know this.

**One Piece (Eiichiro Oda):** Oda confirmed that Levasseur's execution — the "dying declaration to find the treasure" — inspired Gol D. Roger's death and the launch of the Grand Age of Pirates. This opens the manga's entire story. Aimee might know this; Adam won't; a small character beat available in Chapter 9 or 15.

**Reginald Cruise-Wilkins:** Began excavating Bel Ombre in the late 1940s. Worked from a poor copy of the cipher. His son John continued intermittently. Decades of excavation — significant resources — never found the entrance. The Ennerdale Rocks parallel (submarine granite features undiscovered immediately adjacent to the main harbour channel) provides the credible in-world explanation for how the entrance remained hidden.

## 5. FICTIONAL USAGE

### La Buze in the novel's world
Levasseur is not a character in the novel — he is a historical figure referenced through documents, oral tradition, and SYBIL analysis. He is present as:
- The historical context for the entire investigation
- The cipher's attributed author (in-world genuine; in reality probably 20th-century invention)
- The figure whose settlement in the Seychelles 1724–1728 provided the time for construction
- The quotation Adam finds at the Ravine à Malheur parallel: reportedly "With what I have hidden here, I could purchase the entire island" — usable model for the novel's in-world legendary quotation

### Key scene opportunity
Viktor, in his POV chapters, will have AURORA's full La Buze analysis — including the 2025 wreck confirmation and the absent Cross. His reading of this data (confident, high-probability, pointing toward the cipher as the solution) is the clearest illustration of AURORA's strength and its limitation: she sees everything that is documented and produces the highest-probability answer. The highest-probability answer, in this case, points to the interior navigation only.

### The Hodoul confusion
Hodoul is a real Seychellois privateer, documented in McAteer, active ~1800 — 70 years after La Buze. SYBIL conflates them at one point (confusing two privateers who operated from the same islands, 70 years apart). Juliette separates them immediately, without being asked. This is the first demonstration — visible to the reader — that local lived knowledge catches errors that analytical tools miss.

## 6. LEGAL AND ETHICAL NOTES

La Buze died 1730. He is a historical figure with no living heirs and no legal protection. His story is public domain. There are no constraints on fictional treatment.

The cipher, if reproduced in the novel, should be clearly attributed to La Roncière (1934) in any author's note, acknowledging the scholarly debate about its authenticity. This transparency adds to rather than detracts from the novel's intellectual credibility.
