# THE EDEN CIPHER — Writing Style Guide

---

## Genre Voice

This is a **literary thriller with commercial intentions**. The prose should be:
- As readable as Dan Brown at his best — propulsive, clear, addictive
- As atmospheric as Steve Berry — locations matter; the reader should feel the heat
- Smarter than either in its characterisation — people here have genuine interiority

The ruling principle: **clarity earns complexity**. Never obscure when you can illuminate. But never flatten when depth is available.

---

## Point of View

**Primary:** Close third person, following Adam Gladstone.
**Secondary:** Viktor Volkov — limited third person for selected chapters (maximum 6–8 chapters total). Viktor's sections are shorter and more controlled; he reveals less of himself than Adam does.
**Occasional:** Aimee's perspective for 2–3 key chapters, particularly in Act 3.

**No omniscient narration.** Stay close to a character's head in every scene.

---

## Tense

**Past tense throughout.** No stylistic justification for present tense in this genre. The story is being told after the fact; the reader knows the narrator survived.

---

## Sentence Structure

**Vary it.** Short sentences accelerate. Longer sentences deepen and hold. The rhythm of the prose should mirror the tension of the scene:

- Action / chase / discovery: short, punchy, minimal subordinate clauses
- Reflection / character interiority / description: longer sentences, more texture
- Dialogue exchanges: tight and efficient; trust the reader to track speakers

**Avoid:** Sentence fragments used for false urgency (*He looked. She stared. Silence.*) — these become mannered quickly. Use them sparingly and only when they genuinely serve.

---

## Dialogue

Keep it purposeful. Every line of dialogue should do at least two things:
1. Advance the plot or reveal character
2. Sound like the specific person saying it

**Adam:** Precise, dry, occasionally self-deprecating. He doesn't waste words. When he's wrong about something emotional, he says nothing rather than say something he'll regret.

**Aimee:** Quick, warm, direct. She asks questions because she genuinely wants to know, not to make a point. Her humour is affectionate rather than sharp.

**Viktor:** Measured, wide-ranging, never quite answering the question asked. He asks excellent questions. He gives answers that are true but incomplete.

**Juliette:** Careful, considered. She chooses words the way a linguist does — with awareness of what other words could have been chosen instead. When she is being direct, it feels significant.

**Brigitte:** Steady, precise, and economical. She doesn't perform emotions she is actually feeling. Her French is perfect and she uses it without announcing it. When she reveals something she has been carrying for years, the weight of it is in what she doesn't say around it.

**Chloe:** Wry, precise, slightly competitive with Aimee in the loving way of close sisters. She thinks the way Adam thinks — analytically, linearly — and is comfortable with data. She is good company. She is wrong about one important thing, and she knows it the moment Aimee opens the door.

**Edmond:** Elliptical, story-based. He communicates in examples and anecdotes. He rarely makes a direct statement when he can tell a story that implies it.

**No dialect spelling.** Creole speech should be suggested through rhythm, vocabulary, and cadence — not through phonetic approximation that would feel condescending on the page.

## The Seychelles

The islands are not backdrop. They are **active setting** — the physical environment participates in the story.

Rules:
- Use all five senses in every primary location scene — not a checklist, but naturally woven
- The light in the Seychelles is specific and important; use it
- The granite geology is unlike anywhere else on Earth — it has been there since the Precambrian; let it feel ancient
- The Coco de Mer should be given proper biological weight, not just symbolic weight
- Creole culture appears in texture — the music in the background, the cadence of speech, the way time is experienced differently here

**Avoid:** Brochure writing. Avoid listing beautiful things. Instead, describe *specific* things that happen to be beautiful — the exact quality of light at 6am on Beau Vallon, the particular resistance of the granite channel floor under foot.

## SYBIL and ZARYA (The AI Systems)

### SYBIL (Adam's AI research tool)

SYBIL's outputs appear in a distinct format:

```
[SYBIL: Analysis complete. Three anomalies flagged across the McAteer corpus.
Confidence: 87%. Interpretation: insufficient contextual data for evaluation.
You should ask Juliette.]
```

Rules for SYBIL:
- She is always technically accurate
- She flags what she cannot interpret, rather than guessing
- She notices the human alongside the analytical — she suggests who to ask, not just what the data says
- Her presence in a scene should be felt as *useful but limited*
- She should never be mocked or dismissed — she represents a genuine capability that has genuine limits
- In Act 3, SYBIL appears less. The human characters are solving what she cannot.

### ZARYA (Viktor's AI system)

ZARYA's outputs appear in a contrasting format:

```
[ZARYA: Cipher layer 2 complete. Confidence: 94%. Cultural register
analysis: children's mnemonic, Seychellois Creole tradition. No
operational relevance detected.]
```

Rules for ZARYA:
- She is declarative. No hedging. No suggestions.
- She does not flag uncertainty — she produces conclusions with confidence scores
- She does not notice the human. She does not suggest who to ask.
- She is not inferior to SYBIL in raw capability — she is more powerful
- She is wrong about the one thing that matters: she cannot distinguish *information* from *meaning*
- ZARYA should never be written as stupid. She should be written as brilliant and incomplete.

## Pacing

### Chapter Length
Target: **2,500–4,000 words per chapter.** Shorter for action/transition chapters. Longer for major revelation and emotional chapters.

### Chapter Endings
Every chapter should end on a *hook* or a *turn*:
- A hook: something happens or is revealed that demands continuation
- A turn: the reader's understanding of the situation has changed

Avoid chapters that simply end because the scene is done. Find the moment of maximum implication and end there.

### Act Pacing

| Act | Pacing Note |
|-----|------------|
| Act 1 | Slightly slower — establish, invest, intrigue. Don't rush the arrival. |
| Act 2 | Progressive acceleration — each revelation should raise the stakes |
| Act 3 | Fast until Ch. 37–40, which slow deliberately for emotional resolution |

---

## Research and Accuracy

**Get the real details right.** This novel relies on the Seychelles feeling real, La Buse's historical existence being respected, McAteer's actual books being accurately cited, and the biology of the Coco de Mer being correct.

Where the novel invents (the underground complex, the Deschamps archive), the invention should feel consistent with everything the reader has learned is true.

**La Buse cryptogram:** The real cryptogram should be incorporated and its actual text respected. The novel's explanation of it should be consistent with known facts and clearly flagged as fictional interpretation.

---

## What to Avoid

| Avoid | Because |
|-------|---------|
| Excessive flashbacks | The novel is present-tense urgent; too much retrospection breaks the momentum |
| Over-explaining the cipher | Trust the reader; show the *effect* of the cipher's interpretation, not exhaustive decoding scenes |
| Viktor being cartoonishly villainous | His power comes from being right about things — he must be argued with, not sneered at |
| Aimee being passive until her key moment | Build her contribution in every act so her climactic scene feels earned |
| Seychelles as unambiguous paradise | The political history, the colonialism, the inequality — these are part of the texture |
| Rushing the archive discovery | This is the emotional peak; spend time in it |

## Chapter Opening Lines

Every chapter should open with a line that:
1. Establishes setting or character immediately (no preamble)
2. Creates forward motion — the reader is *already in* the scene

Examples of the right register:
> *The parcel had come from the Seychelles, which was the first thing Adam noticed, and it had been sent by a dead man, which was the second.*

> *Viktor didn't hate the sea. He simply didn't trust anything that couldn't be quantified.*

> *Aimee had been six years old the last time she'd held a Coco de Mer seed. She hadn't forgotten the weight of it.*

---

## The Emotional Register

The novel should make the reader feel:
- **Curiosity** — early; maintain it throughout
- **Unease** — the Seychelles conceals as much as it reveals
- **Investment** — in Adam and Aimee's relationship, in Juliette's quiet grief
- **Exhilaration** — during discovery sequences
- **Satisfaction** — the ending is earned; the reader should feel this is how it had to end

The novel should not make the reader feel:
- Manipulated by false emotional beats
- Confused by the clue chain (it should be complex but always followable)
- Cheated by the resolution (the antagonist's exit must feel complete, not convenient)
