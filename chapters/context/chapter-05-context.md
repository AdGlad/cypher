# Chapter 5 Context — THE CALL HOME
*Drafting session file. Contains only what this chapter needs.*

---

## Chapter at a Glance

**POV:** Adam (close third person, past tense)
**Location:** Adam's home, Perth — evening before departure
**Word target:** 2,000–2,500 words
**Function:** Introduce Brigitte, Aimee, and Chloe as characters. Establish family dynamics before they are all in the same physical space. Establish Aimee's presence in the investigation — not as someone "coming along" but as someone already there. Plant the first sense that Brigitte knows more than she has said.

**Note on continuity:** Brigitte, Aimee, and Chloe are already in the Seychelles. They left two weeks ago for Brigitte's coral survey season. Adam calls them on video. This chapter is a family video call — the first extended scene with the whole family — the evening before Adam flies out.

---

## What Happens — Beat by Beat

**Beat 1 — Adam calls**
Evening, Perth. Adam is at home. He has spent the day packing research materials (Chapter 4's work). The house is quiet with the specific quality it has when the family is away — their absence is spatial, not just personal. A pair of Chloe's running shoes by the door. Aimee's desk still covered in her notebook sketches, which she left.

He sets up the video call. The Seychelles: it is already tomorrow morning there (UTC+4; Perth is UTC+8 — a four-hour difference, which means Adam's 9pm is Aimee's 1am; he has timed this to catch them before bed). The call connects.

**Beat 2 — The family at Juliette's**
They are at Juliette's house on Cerf Island — Brigitte's old family connection; the arrangement for the coral survey season. The camera shows a kitchen he doesn't know, stone walls, warm light. Brigitte and Chloe at the table. Aimee is somewhere slightly off-camera, eating something, not looking at the screen.

This is an ordinary call before it becomes something else. Chloe asks about the parcel — she already knows about it from Brigitte, who knew before Adam called in Chapter 2. They are not three separate people he has to brief sequentially; they have already talked among themselves. Adam is slightly behind the information in his own family.

**Beat 3 — What Aimee knows**
Aimee comes on camera properly when Adam starts talking about what SYBIL found. She has stopped eating. She is paying attention.

She does not say much in this beat. But she asks two questions that are the right questions — not about the cipher, not about the archive, but about the land: *"Which part of Bel Ombre?"* and then, when he can't answer precisely: *"I know that beach. I used to go there with Juliette."*

Adam notes this. He does not yet understand what it means.

**Beat 4 — Brigitte**
Brigitte: not surprised by the parcel, by SYBIL's finding, or by Adam coming. She has been expecting something since McAteer died. She won't say exactly why. She is practical and warm and slightly closed — not evasive but managing something privately.

Her key line in this scene: *"This is what he wanted, I think. He wanted you to come here and look at it with real eyes, not just books."* Whether "he" is McAteer or someone else is deliberately ambiguous for now.

She also says: *"The girls know the Seychelles. They spent a year here. They know things you don't."* This is not an argument — it is a statement of fact. Adam accepts it without fully registering it.

**Beat 5 — Chloe**
Chloe: 26, eighteen months older than Aimee, marine environmental consultant, present and grounded. She is less immediately engaged by the puzzle than Aimee — she is interested in whether it is safe, whether Viktor is a real risk, what happens if they find something and Viktor is already there.

Her questions are the logistical questions Adam should be asking but hasn't gotten to yet. He is slightly reassured by this and slightly unsettled — he was not prepared to be assessed.

Chloe speaks Creole easily (she picks up a fragment of conversation with Juliette in the kitchen, off-camera, and it comes naturally). Adam notices this and doesn't quite know what to do with it.

**Beat 6 — Aimee's thing**
Near the end of the call, Aimee says something that Adam doesn't quite hear over the connection. He asks her to repeat it. She says:

*"I dreamed about Bel Ombre last week. Before the parcel came."*

A beat. Then, lightly: *"That's probably not useful data."*

He says: *"Probably not."* He is trying to be kind. He is also being wrong.

She looks at him for a moment through the screen. Then she smiles. The call ends.

## Adam in This Chapter

- He is calling home and finds himself slightly wrong-footed — they know more than he expected, and the two questions Aimee asks are sharper than he's ready for
- His relationship with Brigitte: warm, real, slightly asymmetric in this moment — she knows something she hasn't said, and he can feel it without being able to name it
- His response to Aimee's dream: an attempt at kindness that is also a failure of imagination. He files it as noise. The reader should understand it is not noise.
- He goes still during the call in the moments when something is said that matters — Aimee's questions about Bel Ombre, Brigitte's statement about "real eyes." He goes still and says nothing, which is his processing mode.

---

## Brigitte in This Chapter

*First appearance.*

- Warm, direct, economical. She is the most efficient communicator in the family — says exactly what she means, no more.
- She is not performing relief that Adam is coming. She is simply carrying forward.
- She has McAteer's private letter in her knowledge (not referenced explicitly in this chapter — let it be a felt presence in what she doesn't say)
- Her statement about "the girls knowing the Seychelles" is a careful statement: she is not pushing Adam; she is informing him. The difference matters.

---

## Aimee in This Chapter

*First appearance.*

- She is slightly off-camera, eating, and then fully present — the transition is natural
- Her two questions about Bel Ombre are specific and good. She is not performing interest; she has it.
- *"I dreamed about Bel Ombre last week"* is offered simply, with a self-deprecating note (*"probably not useful data"*). She has learned to couch her intuitive knowledge in Adam's language. She will eventually stop doing this.
- She is at ease in the Seychelles in a way that comes through even over video — the light on her face is different; she looks like herself.

---

## Chloe in This Chapter

*First appearance.*

- Practical, grounded, logistical. She is interested in the problem at the level of risk and consequence, not at the level of mystery and meaning
- Her Creole appears naturally — she doesn't perform it; it's simply there
- She appraises Adam the way an older sibling appraises a parent: with clear eyes and no particular patience for being managed
- Her relationship with Aimee: easy, occasionally competitive, always honest. They finish each other's sentences a few times.

---

## Tone

A video call in an empty house. The warmth is on the other end of the screen. Perth is warm at 9pm in the Australian summer, but the house is wrong — sized for four people, occupied by one. The family in the Seychelles: warm kitchen light, sounds of the island night behind them (frogs, something in the garden). Adam watching this as both his family and the place he is going.

The chapter should feel domestic and slightly off-balance — ordinary and extraordinary at the same time. The investigation has begun, but the family doesn't treat it as the beginning of something dramatic. They treat it as the next thing that is happening.

---

## What NOT to Do in This Chapter

- Do not have Aimee say "I want to come" — she is already there
- Do not make Brigitte's withheld knowledge explicit — it should be felt, not named
- Do not introduce Juliette in person — she is in the kitchen, off-camera, a voice and a presence
- Do not have this scene run too long — it is a pivot chapter, not a revelation chapter; the real dynamics will be established when Adam arrives
- Do not let Adam dismiss Aimee's questions — he engages with them; he simply underweights them

---

## Chapter Ending

The call ends. Adam looks at the empty screen.

He has been in his own house for forty-eight hours since the parcel arrived. He has not cooked a meal. He has not been outside. He has drunk approximately twelve cups of tea.

He books a taxi for the morning.

*Cut.*
