## What Happens — Beat by Beat

**Beat 1 — After the night**
Adam has been at his desk all night. Three notebooks. He has read the annotated book cover to cover. He has photographed everything. SYBIL is still processing.

The house is quiet. Brigitte and the girls are already in the Seychelles — Brigitte's coral survey season began two weeks ago. The house has the specific quality of a space that's been lived in by multiple people and is now occupied by one. He makes tea. He drinks it this time.

**Beat 2 — The call to Juliette**
He calls Juliette at a reasonable local hour (Seychelles is UTC+4 — Perth is UTC+8, so he calls mid-morning Perth time, which is early morning Seychelles time; she answers immediately, as if she expected the call to come overnight).

The conversation is careful on both sides. She is not unfriendly — she is formal in the way of someone who has prepared what she is willing to say and is saying exactly that.

She confirms:
- McAteer left specific instructions. Adam is the person he wanted to receive the book.
- The delay was also instructed — a specified interval between death and sending. Juliette honoured it.
- She will not say more over the phone. She suggests Adam come to the Seychelles.

Adam asks: *"Why me?"*

Juliette: *"He trusted your particular combination, he said. Mathematical rigour and genuine curiosity. He said most people have one or the other."*

She says nothing more about McAteer's reasoning. She is already managing what she reveals and when. Adam notices this but cannot yet read it clearly.

**Beat 3 — Who McAteer was (woven through)**
This is not an information dump — it is memory, filtered through Adam's relationship with the man.

What Adam knew: McAteer was Brigitte's father's old colleague, then a presence in Adam's intellectual life. A historian who wrote beautifully and thought precisely. A man who asked questions that seemed simple and were not. A man who was interested in Adam in a way that was not quite professional — as if he were watching something develop that he had predicted.

What Adam is now realising: McAteer's preoccupation in his final years — the one no one quite understood. He withdrew from the academic circuit. He spent more time on the smaller islands. He published nothing in his last four years. Adam thought it was age. It wasn't.

What the books represent: Five books covering 400 years of Seychellois history. Published between 1977 and 2009. Well-regarded. Academic but readable. Adam has read all five — as a courtesy to Brigitte's family connection, and because McAteer was worth reading.

**Beat 4 — SYBIL delivers**
SYBIL's initial results arrive (partial — she flags that the full McAteer corpus analysis will take longer):

```
[SYBIL: Initial cross-reference complete. Confidence: 71% (limited data).
Preliminary finding: 14 geographical references across Books 1, 2, and 4
carry unusual specificity relative to the surrounding narrative register.
Statistical probability of this distribution being random: 4.3%.
Note: this analysis is preliminary. Full corpus analysis required.
I notice you have been working through the night. Should I flag any
time-sensitive priorities?]
```

Adam reads this twice. He does not share it with anyone yet.

**Beat 5 — The decision**
Adam looks at the annotated book. He looks at the preliminary SYBIL report. He looks at the address of Juliette's return call from the Seychelles.

He calls Brigitte.

The call is short — he tells her what arrived, tells her Juliette has asked him to come. Brigitte is not surprised in the way he expects. She says: *"I know. I thought this might happen eventually. Come."*

He books the flight.

*Cut.*

## Adam in This Chapter

- He is in analytical mode, but the emotional weight of McAteer's death — and of being chosen by a dead man — is present underneath
- The regret: he was in Singapore at a conference when McAteer had his stroke three years ago. McAteer recovered and lived another two years, but Adam never quite said what he wanted to say. This is in him without being stated.
- His relationship with Brigitte: the call is warm and brief. She already knew about McAteer's preoccupation — she is less surprised than Adam, which is the first hint that she has been carrying something
- He books the flight efficiently, without drama. This is who he is: the decision is made; what follows is logistics

---

## Juliette in This Chapter

*First appearance — voice only (phone call).*

- Her voice: measured, precise, accented (French Creole inflection in English). She is warm in tone but controlled in information.
- She has prepared for this call. She knows exactly what she will say and what she will not say.
- She answers his "why me?" question with McAteer's words, not her own. This is deliberate — she wants Adam to understand this is McAteer's choice, not a conclusion she has drawn independently.
- She says *come* as the end of the conversation. Not urgently. Simply: this is the next step.
- She is already managing the revelation sequence. Adam doesn't know this yet.

## SYBIL in This Chapter

*Second appearance. Still in the overnight process.*

- Her output here is preliminary — she flags uncertainty explicitly
- The 4.3% probability is significant but not conclusive; she knows this and says so
- Her aside about Adam working through the night is her second act of noticing the human — gentle, not intrusive
- She does not over-state her findings. The 14 references are a start, not a conclusion.

## McAteer's Books — What Adam Now Knows

The five books:
- *Rivals in Eden* (1977) — early colonial and French settlement period
- *Hard Times in Paradise* (1983) — British colonial period and World War 2 era
- *To Be a Nation* (1995) — the 1977 coup and independence politics
- *Echoes of Eden* (2002) — environmental and ecological history
- *Another Story* (2009) — oral traditions, community memory, lesser-known islands

Adam has read all five. SYBIL is about to tell him what he missed.

## Tone

Post-night quiet in an empty house. The weight of a decision forming rather than made. Perth morning light (already warm by 8am — this is not Edinburgh). The clarity that comes after a long night: everything is in focus, slightly raw.

The McAteer material should feel elegiac without being sentimental. He was a real person. He is not present. He left instructions that have been followed. This is both the most impersonal and most personal thing Adam has received from him.
