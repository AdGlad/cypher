# Prologue Context — THE ENTRANCE
*Drafting session file. Contains only what this prologue needs.*

---

## Prologue at a Glance

**Structure:** Two scenes, separated by a date header. Total word target: 800–1,200 words.
**POV:** La Buse — close third person, past tense (the only scenes from his perspective in the novel)
**Function:** Scene 1 plants the image the reader will not understand until Chapter 30. Scene 2 is the historical event that launches everything — the cipher thrown into the crowd. Together: he found it; he encoded it; he left. The reader does not know what any of it means yet.

---

## Scene 1 at a Glance

**Location:** Bel Ombre, Mahé, Seychelles — before dawn
**Date:** Approximately 1727
**Word target:** 500–700 words
**Function:** La Buse at the complex. He retrieves what he came for. He looks at the archive and cannot understand it. He scratches a marker on the granite wall. He leaves before the tide returns.

---

## What Happens — Beat by Beat

**Beat 1 — The approach**
Before dawn. The beach at Bel Ombre is empty — it has always been empty; no settlement here, no permanent people on this island. The Seychelles are uninhabited. La Buse knows this stretch of coastline from five visits. He is alone. He has left his two companions at the boat.

The tidal channel: a narrow gap in the granite at the north end of the beach, visible only when the tide has pulled back far enough. He watches the water recede. He has timed this. The window is perhaps forty minutes, perhaps less, depending on the season. He has missed it twice. Tonight the timing is right.

He is not a romantic figure. He is cold, efficient, and he has a lantern.

**Beat 2 — The entrance**
The channel is narrow — shoulder-width. The granite on either side is worn smooth in a way that does not look natural. Not worn by water alone. Worn by passage.

He enters.

The reader sees the physical environment precisely but without explanation:
- The smell: salt and something beneath it. Old air.
- The sound: the tide receding behind him; ahead, silence with texture
- The lantern throws his shadow forward
- The passage opens. He is inside something.

Do not describe the full complex. The reader sees what La Buse sees, held in his lantern's reach.

**Beat 3 — What he finds (and what he does not understand)**
The treasure he came for — the portion he has cached here, separate from the rest. He is a man who does not keep all his gold in one place. He has left things here from an earlier visit. He retrieves them efficiently.

But the other chamber is there, beyond a low stone arch. He has looked inside before. He looks again now.

Shelves cut into granite. The shelves hold objects he cannot name — glass cylinders sealed with wax, bound volumes in a state of perfect preservation, flat drawings mounted on wooden boards. The volumes are in no language he reads. The drawings appear to be of plants, insects, birds — rendered with a precision he has never seen applied to such subjects. Everything is numbered. Everything is ordered.

He understands none of it. He does not touch it. He does not take any of it.

He does not know what it is, but he knows it is not his.

**Beat 4 — The image that echoes at the end**
Before he leaves, he does one thing. This is the beat the prologue is built around.

He crouches at the inner wall of the first chamber — the wall between the passage and the archive — and scratches something into the granite with a blade. Not the full cipher. A fragment. A marker. Something that says: *I was here. I found this. The rest is for whoever comes next.*

The reader does not understand what he scratches. They will understand it in Chapter 30 when Adam reads it with his lantern.

La Buse backs out through the passage. The tide is already returning. The entrance will be underwater in minutes.

He looks at the beach. The palm fronds. The first grey light over the water.

He leaves.

## Scene 2 at a Glance

**Location:** Saint-Denis, Réunion — the Place du Barachois
**Date:** 7 July 1730
**Word target:** 300–500 words
**Function:** The historical event that launches the entire novel. La Buse throws the cipher into the crowd. The reader understands, from Scene 1, that the cipher navigates the interior of something — but not what, not where. End on: *Two hundred and ninety-nine years later.*

---

## Scene 2 — What Happens

**Beat 1 — The capture (compressed to one paragraph)**
He was taken at sea, off Réunion, two months prior. Not a glorious last stand — a practical naval operation. He was outnumbered and he knew it. He surrendered without theatre. On the voyage back to Saint-Denis in irons he had time to think about what he was leaving behind. He thought about the parchment. He thought about who would find it.

He made a decision.

**Beat 2 — The gallows**
7 July 1730. Morning. The Place du Barachois. The crowd is large — executions of this notoriety draw everyone who can get there. He is brought out in good order. He does not perform defiance.

Before the drop, he reaches inside his coat. He has kept it through the imprisonment — it was not found, because he was not searched carefully, because no one believed a dying man's piece of paper mattered.

He holds the parchment up. The crowd sees it.

He speaks — in French, clearly, so the whole square can hear:
*"Trouvez mon trésor, qui peut le comprendre."*
*Find my treasure, he who can understand it.*

He throws it into the crowd.

People scramble. One man catches it. He holds it up, triumphant, not knowing what he has.

The trap falls.

**Beat 3 — The close**
The man with the parchment pushes through the crowd. Around him: the noise of the square returning to itself. Vendors. Soldiers. A child crying somewhere.

The parchment is a page of symbols, numbers, letters. Multi-layered. In more than one language. Precise in a way that suggests something real.

The man cannot read it.

No one who tries will find anything for nearly three hundred years.

*Cut.*

*Two hundred and ninety-nine years later.*

## La Buse in This Scene

*Use these notes — do not load full research files.*

- Nationality: French. Age: approximately 39.
- He is not romanticised. He is not savage. He is professional, methodical, skilled at survival.
- He found this complex; he did not build it. He has no idea who built it or when. He does not try to find out.
- He is not superstitious about the archive — he is simply not interested in what he cannot sell or use.
- Physical: lean, hardened. He moves quietly in the dark, the way a man does when the dark has been his working environment for twenty years. The lantern is not for comfort — it is a tool.
- He has no dialogue in this scene. No internal monologue beyond practical assessment. The prose stays outside him — close but controlled.

---

## Tone

Before dawn on an uninhabited island. The Seychelles in 1727: no roads, no buildings, no human sound. The granite is old in a way that has nothing to do with La Buse or his century.

The prose should be as spare as the landscape. No romanticism about pirates. No flourish about treasure. The prologue's power is in what is not explained — the reader is watching something significant and is not given the tools to understand it yet.

The archive should feel wrong in the most unsettling possible way: it is too ordered. It is too permanent. It has been here longer than the man standing in front of it, and it will be here long after him.

## What NOT to Do in This Prologue

- Do not name the archive's creator — that is a Book 2 thread
- Do not describe the full complex — only what the lantern reaches
- Do not give La Buse inner access to the archive's significance — he doesn't care
- Do not romanticise La Buse or make him heroic
- Do not explain the tidal mechanism or the channel's geometry — show only; the reader does not need to understand it here
- Do not include any character from the main narrative — this is entirely isolated in time
- Do not resolve the image La Buse scratches — leave it opaque

---

## Prologue Ending

La Buse on the beach. Grey light. He is looking at the water.

*Cut.*

*Two hundred and ninety-nine years later.*

## Continuity Notes

- The entrance at Bel Ombre is the tidal channel — only accessible at low tide, specific orientation required (this is confirmed in Act 3; do not over-specify here)
- The Seychelles were uninhabited until French colonisation in the 1770s — La Buse on an empty island is historically accurate
- La Buse was executed at Saint-Denis, Réunion, 7 July 1730 — this prologue is approximately three years before that
- The scratch on the wall is the detail that connects prologue to Chapter 30 — establish it clearly enough that Adam's discovery of it in Chapter 30 registers as a callback
- The archive's creator is a French Huguenot scholar who fled France after the 1685 Revocation of the Edict of Nantes — the archive has been here for approximately 40 years when La Buse enters. He has been dead for some time.
