**Function:** Deliver the inciting object. Introduce Adam's character through his reaction to it. End on the hook that begins the investigation.

## What Happens — Beat by Beat

**Beat 1 — The unopened parcel**
Adam at his desk, working with SYBIL on an unrelated problem. The parcel from the Seychelles sits on the corner of his desk. He has been not-opening it for forty minutes. He is aware of this. He continues not-opening it. This is the first thing the reader learns about him.

Return address on the parcel: *Juliette McAteer, Cerf Island, Seychelles.*

**Beat 2 — He opens it**
Inside: William McAteer's personal annotated copy of *Rivals in Eden*. Worn, used, multiple layers of annotation in different inks across different decades. He handles it carefully. He reads several annotations — scholarly at first, then, in the last third of the book, different in character. Circled phrases. Unexplained symbols. A series of what appear to be page numbers referencing other books.

He finds the note. Inside the back cover. McAteer's handwriting:
*"The entrance is in the books. All of them. I didn't put it there. It was already there. — W.M."*

He reads it twice. He does not call anyone. He removes his glasses and holds them.

**Beat 3 — SYBIL**
Adam photographs the note and the key annotations. Runs them through SYBIL.

SYBIL's output:
```
[SYBIL: Handwriting analysis complete. Confidence: 94%.
Match to William McAteer — consistent with signed annotations
throughout the volume. Phrase "the entrance" flagged as
contextually ambiguous — entrance to what cannot be determined
from available text. Would you like me to cross-reference
the McAteer corpus? I notice this arrived from the Seychelles.]
```

Adam: *"Yes. All five books."*

SYBIL begins. She will not finish until Chapter 4.

**Beat 4 — The hook**
While SYBIL processes, Adam opens the book to the page McAteer has circled most heavily — Chapter 17, the Bel Ombre passage. Six lines underscored in red. Single marginal word: *Again.*

The language is oddly precise for a general history. He reads it three times. He does not know why it feels important. He reaches for his phone.

## Adam in This Chapter

*Use these beats — do not load the full character file.*

- He processes anticipation analytically. The forty minutes of not-opening the parcel is not procrastination — it is preparation. He is deciding what he will do with whatever is inside before he knows what it is.
- Emotional response to the note arrives before it is acknowledged. He reads it twice. The second reading is the one that lands.
- He does not call anyone immediately. He thinks first. This is who he is.
- Physical tells: removes glasses and holds them when something surprises him. Goes very still when working through a problem. Reads documents with his lips moving slightly.
- His office: ordered but not sterile. Three notebooks open simultaneously. A mechanical pencil (no ink, no battery — just lead and friction). Tea he made an hour ago and hasn't touched.
- His relationship with McAteer: significant, slightly unresolved. McAteer was Brigitte's father's old colleague, then a presence in Adam's intellectual life. He didn't get to say what he wanted to say. The parcel is already freighted with this.

## SYBIL in This Chapter

*First appearance. Establish her format and personality.*

- Interface: tablet, text only. No voice.
- Format: `[SYBIL: Analysis complete. Confidence: XX%. ...]`
- Personality: precise, kind, empathetic. She flags uncertainty rather than guessing. She notices the human alongside the analytical — the gentle observation about the Seychelles package is her first act of noticing.
- She reaches her limit immediately on the first significant question ("entrance to what?"). This is not failure — it is accuracy. She knows what she doesn't know.
- She does not produce results in this chapter beyond the handwriting match and the flag. Her full McAteer analysis comes in Chapter 4.

## Key Objects

- **The parcel:** Brown paper, Seychellois stamps, Juliette's handwriting on the address label. It has been sitting on the corner of his desk since the morning post.
- **The annotated copy of *Rivals in Eden*:** Worn blue cloth cover. Multiple ink colours — blue, black, red — across different decades of annotation. McAteer's hand.
- **The note:** Inside the back cover, not loose — written directly onto the endpaper. Undated. McAteer's handwriting.
- **Adam's mechanical pencil:** On his desk. No ink, no battery. Just lead and pressure.
- **The tea:** Cold by now.

## Tone

Late afternoon Perth light (long, flat, warm — different from the grey office this chapter was originally set in). The office is quiet. The work Adam was doing before the parcel doesn't matter; it falls away. The reader should feel the weight of the parcel before Adam opens it, because Adam feels it.

The prose should be close and still. No dramatic announcements. The note's power comes from its simplicity: *The entrance is in the books. All of them. I didn't put it there. It was already there.*

## Chapter Ending

Adam is looking at the Bel Ombre passage. The marginal word *Again.* He doesn't know what it means. Something in the passage — the precision of the geography, the specificity of the tidal references — feels wrong for a general history. He reads it a third time.

He picks up his phone. He calls Brigitte.

*Cut.*
