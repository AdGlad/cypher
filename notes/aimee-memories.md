# Notes — Aimee's Memories

*This file tracks Aimee's memory recovery arc — what she remembers, when she remembers it, and what it means.*

---

## Aimee's Childhood in the Seychelles

Aimee lived in the Seychelles during the period her family was based there — Adam's work and Brigitte's family connection brought them there for several formative years of Aimee's childhood.

**What she has from that time:**
- Fluent Seychellois Creole — genuinely fluent, not tourist-level
- School friendships and cultural knowledge from attending a local school
- Sensory memories of specific places (Beau Vallon, Vallée de Mai, the market in Victoria)
- Knowledge of Creole songs, rhymes, and games learned at school and in the community
- A sense of belonging to this place that she hasn't been able to replicate in Edinburgh

**Why she wanted to come:**
Aimee is at a difficult point — university isn't working the way she hoped. She is intellectually capable but searching for something she can't name. The Seychelles represents the last time she felt genuinely at home.

Her return triggers a progressive recovery of childhood sensory and cultural memories. This is not dramatic amnesia recovery — it is the natural resurfacing of things that were always there, waiting for the right environment.

## Memory Recovery Arc by Chapter

### Chapter 5 — The Decision
Not yet in the Seychelles. Aimee wants to come. Her stated reason: she wants to see it again. Her unstated reason: she feels more real there than anywhere else.

### Chapter 6 — Arrival
Physical return. The smell of the air, the sound of Creole around her, the quality of light. She visibly relaxes in a way she hasn't in months. Adam notices.

### Chapter 10 — Beau Vallon
The beach triggers specific school memories. She hears the elderly woman's fragment of the rhyme. She writes the rhyme in her notebook — not knowing why, just knowing it matters.

### Chapter 14 — Vallée de Mai
The Coco de Mer seed in her hands. She was brought here as a child — this is not the first time she has held this weight. Her drawing of the seed is unconscious, compulsive.

### Chapter 17 — Praslin / After Père Henri
Père Henri's reference to "the room behind the room" lands differently for Aimee than for Adam. She is quiet for the rest of the evening. Something is assembling in her mind that she can't yet name.

### Chapter 25 — After Père Henri's Second Call
*"The key to that room is not written down anywhere. It lives in the people."*

Aimee hears this repeated by Adam. She goes still. Then: *"I know what it is."*

She is not certain yet. But the direction is clear.

### Chapter 29 — The Second Door

Aimee: *"It's not coincidental."*

She shows Adam her notebook. The rhyme. He reads it, doesn't see the function. She does.

### Chapter 30 — The Opening

## What Aimee's Arc Argues

Aimee is not clever in the way Adam is clever. She is not analytical. She does not work with data.

What she has is:
- Embodied cultural knowledge (the game's physical sequence)
- Patience with non-linear understanding (she doesn't push her memories; she lets them surface)
- Emotional openness (she can be in a place and let it affect her, rather than processing it)

She is the novel's argument that understanding has a dimension data cannot capture. Viktor's system is more powerful than anything Adam possesses. Viktor's system cannot do what Aimee does.

## Aimee's Decision at the End

She tells Adam she is not going back to university immediately. She wants to stay in the Seychelles — to be part of what happens next, or simply to be here.

This decision is:
- Autonomous (her choice, not anyone's suggestion)
- Consistent with her character (she found something she was looking for)
- Not naively sentimental — she knows it's complicated; she chooses anyway
