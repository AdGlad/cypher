# The Creole Rhyme — *Ti chat ki pe dòmi*
*Novel anchor: the key to the inner chamber. Layer 3 of the three-layer mystery.*

---

## The Rhyme — Full Text

**Ti chat ki pe dòmi**
*(The little cat who sleeps)*

---

**Verse 1**
Ti chat, ti chat ki pe dòmi,
Res trankil anba la nwi.
Pa fer bri, pa fer bri,
Laport-la garde so sekre.

*Little cat, little cat who sleeps,*
*Rest still beneath the night.*
*Make no noise, make no noise,*
*The door keeps its secret.*

---

**Verse 2 — the sequence**
Frape twa fwa lo roch fri,
Ay agos, epi adwar.
Poze ou lame dan milyé,
Laport louvri pour seki kone.

*Knock three times on the cold stone,*
*Go left, then go right.*
*Place your hand at the centre,*
*The door opens for those who know.*

---

**Verse 3**
Rant andedan, pa per nanyen,
Ti chat konnen so smin.
Solèy leve, solèy kouse,
Seki kone trouv so lakaz.

*Come inside, fear nothing,*
*The little cat knows its path.*
*Sun rises, sun sets,*
*Those who know find their home.*

---

## The Physical Sequence

Embedded in Verse 2. Five steps, performed in order:

| Step | Creole phrase | Action |
|------|--------------|--------|
| 1 | *Frape twa fwa lo roch fri* | Three knocks on the specific cold stone at the left of the arch |
| 2 | *Ay agos* | Step left — to the left wall |
| 3 | *Epi adwar* | Cross to the right wall |
| 4 | *Poze ou lame dan milyé* | Both hands placed flat on the central keystone at chest height |
| 5 | *Laport louvri pour seki kone* | The mechanism releases |

**Note:** The sequence is adjustable at draft stage — number of knocks, specific walls, exact position of the keystone. The architecture above is a working draft.

## The Key Line

*"Laport louvri pour seki kone"*
**"The door opens for those who know."**

This line carries the novel's central argument. It is not a password — it is a condition. The door does not open because someone recites the words. It opens because the sequence is performed correctly by a body that has the rhyme in it.

SYBIL can locate this rhyme in the corpus of documented Seychellois children's songs.
ZARYA can translate it.
Chloe can recite all three verses correctly.
None of that opens the door.

Aimee learned it at age six at a Seychellois school. Her body has it. She does not think through the sequence when the moment comes — she performs it, the way a musician's hands know a piece they no longer need to count through.

## The Rhyme's Origin (In-World)

The rhyme was not composed by Abel Deschamps. It predates his archive — it is a genuine piece of Seychellois oral tradition that describes, in plain language, the physical sequence required to open the inner chamber. Whether Deschamps knew the rhyme, or whether the rhyme grew up around the knowledge of the door sequence after his time, is deliberately unresolved.

The oral tradition preserved what the written record never contained. This is the novel's argument made concrete.

## Research Note

*This rhyme has been created for the novel. Before publication, verify authenticity of the Seychellois Creole (Seselwa) with a cultural consultant. The vocabulary, grammar, and rhythm are based on documented Seselwa patterns but require native-speaker review.*

*Key vocabulary used:*
- *ti* — little/small
- *chat* — cat (from French)
- *ki pe dòmi* — who is sleeping (progressive)
- *laport* — door (from French *la porte*)
- *louvri* — open
- *agos / adwar* — left / right (from French *à gauche / à droite*)
- *seki kone* — those who know
