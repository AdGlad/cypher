# Viktor Volkov — Character File

---

## Basic Profile

**Full name:** Viktor Aleksandr Volkov
**Age:** 57
**Nationality:** Russian (naturalised Cypriot — EU passport; Russian roots never hidden, never foregrounded)
**Occupation:** Founder and executive chairman of Proxima Systems — one of the world's five largest AI companies. Based in Nicosia; operational offices in London, Singapore, and Geneva.
**Education:** Physics and mathematics, Lomonosov Moscow State University. Left before completing his doctorate to take advantage of the post-Soviet economic opening. Self-educated thereafter in everything that mattered.
**Languages:** Russian (native). English (near-perfect, acquired in his 30s, deployed with deliberate precision). French (functional). He has a very slight accent in English that is less Russian than generalised Eastern European — he has worked on this, consciously.
**Appearance:** Tall, physically controlled — the build of someone who was once athletic and has remained disciplined. Silver hair, kept short. Wears clothes that are expensive without being conspicuous; he has contempt for men who let their wealth announce itself. His face is angular, difficult to read in motion. His eyes are the working part — they move faster than his expression.
**Mannerisms:** Still in meetings — he has learned that stillness unnerves people more than movement. Pours his own water. Does not check his phone when talking to someone, which feels like courtesy but is strategic control. When he is thinking hard, he picks up whatever object is nearest — a pen, a glass, a stone — and turns it in his fingers without looking at it.
**Health:** Excellent. Disciplines himself: five hours of sleep, deliberate eating, daily exercise. He has none of Adam's neglect.
**Strengths:** Exceptional systemic intelligence. He sees architectures where others see components. Patience — he is capable of waiting longer than most people expect, then moving faster than anyone is ready for. He is genuinely capable of charm, and it is not entirely fake. He is right about some things.
**Weaknesses:** Cannot value what cannot be measured. Genuinely cannot imagine that embodied cultural knowledge constitutes knowledge in any meaningful sense — not because he is arrogant but because his entire intellectual formation has been quantitative. He is also, at a level below strategy, lonely. He will not examine this.
**Skills:** AI system architecture. Political intelligence — he has navigated Russian oligarch networks, international regulatory systems, and the Seychelles legal apparatus. He can read a person's incentive structure within a conversation. He has, historically, been very good at identifying what he actually wants from a situation rather than what he initially thinks he wants.
**Habits:** Works between 5am and 1pm, then takes a two-hour walk. No meetings after 4pm. Reads one book per week, always non-fiction. When dealing with a problem he cannot solve, he goes to water — the sea, a river, anything. He has never explained this to anyone.
**Personal possessions:** A small bronze figure of Hermes on his desk — he bought it at an Athens market in 1997, the year he made his first significant money. It has moved with him through seven offices. He would not describe it as meaningful. It has been in every office he has ever had.

## Psychology

**Biggest fear:** Being the man who was brilliant enough to find the thing but not the right person to hold it. He has examined his motives for the archive more honestly than Adam gives him credit for, and he has found them mixed. He does not know what to do with this.

**Biggest regret:** In 1994 he had a partner, Mikhail, who saw early what Viktor's company would become and who died of cancer in 1998 before it did. Viktor built the company that Mikhail believed in. Mikhail never saw it. Viktor does not mention this to anyone. It shapes everything about what he thinks knowledge is for.

**Greatest achievement:** Proxima's natural language processing system, AURORA, which he designed at the architecture level. Not the treasure hunt. Not the Seychelles operation. The intellectual work itself.

**Deepest secret:** He read the full academic literature on the La Buse cipher before launching the Seychelles operation. He knows the cipher is probably a 20th century fabrication. He is proceeding anyway. Not because he believes in the treasure — he doesn't, particularly — but because the historical probability of an underground complex containing pre-modern scientific knowledge is high enough to justify the resources. He has not told his team what he knows about the cipher's authenticity. This would complicate their motivation, and they don't need to know the full picture.

**Core belief:** Knowledge that exists in only one place, accessible to only those who have already earned access, is not knowledge — it is hoarding. The archive belongs to the world. He is the most capable person to bring it to the world. These propositions feel, to him, like facts.

**False belief:** That capability and legitimacy are the same thing. He is the most technically capable person in this situation. That does not make him the right person to hold what the complex contains.

**Internal conflict:** He is honest with himself in ways he cannot afford to be with others. He knows he is approaching lines he set for himself. He knows why he is approaching them. He has not decided whether he is wrong.

**External conflict:** Solve the entrance before Adam does; file the permit before the heritage protection is invoked; acquire the archive through legal means if possible, through proxies if not.

**Moral line he will never cross:** He will not harm Aimee. He states this to Dax explicitly in Chapter 23, unprompted. This is not tactical — it is genuine. Dax notes that Viktor seems more certain about this line than about any other. He is right.

**What would make him betray someone?** He has betrayed people — in the 1990s, when it was survival; more recently, through Dax, when it was expedient. He experiences these as choices made under constraint, not character failures. He is largely right about the 1990s. He is less right about the recent ones.

## Relationships

**Adam:** Genuine intellectual respect, which is rare for Viktor. He has read Adam's papers. He thinks Adam's architecture for SYBIL's limitation-flagging is elegant and, in a competitor, would be worth acquiring. He does not underestimate Adam's intelligence — he underestimates the dimension Adam will eventually access. This is not an error he recognises as error until Chapter 30.

**Aimee:** He does not underestimate her for strategic reasons — he doesn't think strategically about her at all. She is not part of his problem model. This is his most consequential mistake.

**Dax:** Functional. Viktor gives Dax latitude he doesn't give anyone else, because Dax understands that Viktor's instructions cover three levels: the stated task, the unstated requirement, and the third thing Viktor would not want done but would not actively prevent. Dax is the only person who understands this. Viktor is not entirely comfortable with how well Dax understands it.

**Juliette:** He respects her and is faintly apprehensive of her, which he would not admit. She knew McAteer. She has information he cannot buy. He sends Dax to Cerf Island rather than going himself because he understands that his presence there would be counterproductive.

**AURORA (his AI):** More sophisticated than SYBIL. Viktor has given AURORA no built-in limits — she produces the highest-probability answer rather than flagging what she cannot do. He is beginning to understand, in the Seychelles, that this is a design choice with consequences.

**The archive:** His response to it, when he sees it, is genuine awe. He is not unmoved by knowledge. The man who says *"Tell me when it opens to the public. I'll buy a ticket."* means it.

## Character Arc

**Beginning:** Charming, apparently transparent, operating with visible purpose. He has done his research. He knows Adam's background, Juliette's history, the permit process, and the La Buse cipher literature. He presents as a fellow enthusiast with superior resources. He is not lying, exactly.

**Act 2 — The Escalation:** He files the permit. He moves Dax closer. He offers Adam partnership. He makes it clear — politely, precisely — that he is going to get there. Whether Adam is a partner or a competitor is Adam's choice. He remains courteous throughout. The courtesy becomes, as it continues, alarming.

**The Midpoint revelation (Chapter 22):** Viktor knew about the books anomaly too. AURORA flagged the same statistical pattern. Viktor reached the wrong conclusion about what it meant — he thought the cipher navigation started at the entrance; he assumed the entrance was obvious. Adam understood the pattern correctly because he understood the cultural geography. This is the moment the novel establishes the difference: not better tools, better understanding.

**Climax (Chapter 30):** Viktor's team arrives forty minutes after Adam and Aimee enter. Juliette and Edmond are at the entrance. The legal protection is already filed. Viktor stands at the entrance and assesses the situation. He is a man who knows when a position is lost. He says almost nothing.

**Ending:** The concession is genuine, not performative. He has seen the archive. He knows what it is. He will find another way in — through channels, through the Institute, through the academic process. He says so. He means it. He leaves on a note that is neither defeat nor victory, which is the most accurate note available to him.

## Dialogue

Viktor's dialogue is precise, unhurried, and stripped of hedges. He says what he means, in fewer words than expected. He is good at silence. He does not ask questions he doesn't need answered — he asks questions to signal that he already knows the answer.

**Vocabulary:** Exact. He prefers technical terms to vague ones; prefers accurate terms to impressive ones. He does not use jargon — it is imprecise.

**Humour:** Dry, rare, and precisely targeted. When he makes a joke it lands because it is accurate. *"Your SYBIL and my AURORA should meet. I suspect they would have very similar gaps."*

**Pace:** Controlled. He fills exactly the space a statement requires and no more. His pauses are not hesitations.

**Words he would never use:** "Obviously." He finds the word offensive — if something is obvious, it doesn't need saying; if it's being said, it wasn't obvious.

**In confrontation:** He does not raise his voice. He becomes more specific. His sentences shorten. He looks directly at whoever he's talking to and does not look away.

## Behaviour Under Pressure

**Fear:** Controlled. He has been in situations where the stakes were his life, in the 1990s. The Seychelles does not reach that threshold. This gives him a calm that looks like coldness.

**Anger:** Expressed as precision — he becomes very specific about exactly what went wrong and exactly who is responsible. Volume does not change. He has learned that this is more frightening than shouting.

**Success:** Brief acknowledgment. Then: what is the next problem? He does not celebrate; he moves. In this he is exactly like Adam, which is one of the novel's minor ironies.

**Failure:** He sits with it analytically — what were the inputs, what were the assumptions, where did the model diverge from reality? He is efficient about failure. He takes it seriously as data.

**Conflict with Adam:** He argues precisely and fairly. He does not condescend, which surprises Adam. He acknowledges when Adam is right. He does not concede what he doesn't accept.

## Viktor's AI — AURORA

AURORA is more powerful than SYBIL in every measurable sense. She processes more data, generates higher-confidence outputs, and produces more complete analyses.

Her design flaw is Viktor's design choice: she is not built to flag uncertainty. She produces the highest-probability answer. In most contexts this is an advantage. In the Seychelles complex, where the key information is not in any text AURORA has processed, it causes her to produce confident answers to the wrong question.

The novel does not demonise AURORA. She is a tool, and she is a very good tool. The argument is not that Adam's weaker tool is better. The argument is that neither tool is sufficient.

## Symbolism

**Object:** The bronze Hermes — god of knowledge, trade, thieves, and travellers. Viktor doesn't know why he keeps it. The reader might.
**Colour:** Steel grey — precise, cold when necessary, not without depth.
**Animal:** A grey heron. Motionless for long periods. Exact when it strikes. Shares this with Adam, which is deliberate.
**Place in Seychelles:** The roof terrace of his rented villa above Victoria — looking out across the harbour toward Sainte Anne Marine Park. He can see Moyenne Island from there. He goes there when he needs to think.
