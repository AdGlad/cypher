# Adam Gladstone — Character File

---

## Basic Profile

**Full name:** Adam James Gladstone
**Age:** 43
**Nationality:** British-Australian (British born; Australian permanent resident through Brigitte)
**Occupation:** Professor of Applied Mathematics and Computational History, University of Western Australia. Creator of SYBIL.
**Education:** BSc Mathematics, Imperial College London. PhD in Computational Pattern Recognition, Cambridge. He was the first in his family to go to university.
**Languages:** English (native). Functional French (reads better than speaks). Enough Seychellois Creole to be polite, not enough to be understood.
**Appearance:** Lean and slightly worn — the look of someone who has spent two decades treating sleep as optional. Medium height. Dark hair going grey at the temples faster than he expected. Tends to need a haircut. Wears glasses for reading but removes them when he's thinking hard, as if the world becomes more tractable without the clarity. Hands that are more expressive than his face.
**Mannerisms:** Goes very still when he's working through a problem. Reads documents with his lips moving slightly. Removes his glasses and holds them when something surprises him. When he's wrong and knows it, he says nothing rather than say the wrong thing.
**Health:** Good, but neglected. Has a recurring lower back problem from years at a desk that he treats by ignoring it until it becomes impossible.
**Strengths:** Extraordinary pattern recognition. Persistence — he does not stop at the point where most people give up. Genuine intellectual curiosity that extends well beyond his formal expertise. The ability to build tools (SYBIL) that extend his own cognition. Dry, accurate humour that appears only when he's comfortable.
**Weaknesses:** Over-relies on data and method; distrusts conclusions he can't demonstrate. Slow to acknowledge emotional truth. Has a tendency to assign lower epistemic weight to knowledge that isn't textual — he does this to Aimee, and it's his most important mistake in the novel. Can become so absorbed in a problem that he loses peripheral awareness of the people around him.
**Skills:** Mathematics, cryptographic pattern analysis, AI system design, historical cross-referencing, reading colonial documents. Surprisingly capable sailor (Brigitte's family influence).
**Habits:** Reads in bed until 1am. Makes tea and then forgets to drink it. Keeps three notebooks open simultaneously, one per problem. Runs, badly, on Saturday mornings because a doctor told him to.
**Favourite sayings:** "The data will tell us" (used sincerely, not cynically). "That's an interesting question" (which means he doesn't know the answer yet). When stuck: "Let me think about this differently."
**Personal possessions:** A mechanical pencil he's used since his PhD — no ink, no battery, just lead and friction. McAteer's annotated copy of *Rivals in Eden* (the inciting object of the novel). A battered Leica M3 film camera that belongs to Brigitte's family but that he's carried for fifteen years.

## Psychology

**Biggest fear:** Being wrong in a high-stakes situation and not knowing it — specifically, applying rigorous method to the wrong problem while the right problem goes unsolved. Beneath that, and more personal: failing to see Aimee clearly until it's too late.

**Biggest regret:** He was in Singapore at a mathematics conference when McAteer had a stroke that left him hospitalised for three weeks. He was there, McAteer survived, but Adam has never quite let himself off the hook. McAteer was the person who always made him feel his work mattered beyond the university. He didn't get to say that.

**Greatest achievement:** Creating SYBIL — not just technically, but conceptually. SYBIL represents years of thinking about how machine intelligence can extend human intelligence rather than replace it. In his best work, Adam has been precise about what SYBIL can and cannot do. He built her limits in, deliberately.

**Deepest secret:** He knows — in the analytical way he knows things — that SYBIL has a blind spot. She cannot interpret cultural embodiment. He has been quietly circling this limitation for three years. He doesn't yet know that it is the novel's central problem. By the end of the novel he does.

**Core belief:** The universe is discoverable. Given sufficient time, rigour, and the right tools, any pattern can be found and understood. This is not arrogance — it is professional conviction. It has served him well. It is also, in the specific context of this novel, incomplete.

**False belief:** That the right tool, applied correctly, can address any problem. He believes this because it has almost always been true for him. The exceptions — and Aimee is one of them — have taught him nothing yet.

**Internal conflict:** He recognises, intellectually, that his daughter knows things he doesn't. He cannot bring himself to treat that knowledge as equal to what SYBIL produces. This conflict is the novel's central interpersonal drama.

**External conflict:** Solve the entrance, navigate the complex, protect the archive from Viktor before Viktor's permit makes the whole question legally academic.

**Moral line he will never cross:** He will not endanger Aimee to complete the mission. When Viktor's operation becomes genuinely threatening, this is the line that creates the novel's real pressure.

**What would make him betray someone?** He would not betray Juliette's trust or Edmond's network. But he would choose Aimee's safety over the discovery if forced to choose. He's not certain he would choose correctly in the moment. This uncertainty is one of his more human qualities.

## Relationships

**Aimee:** His daughter. He loves her, and the quality of that love is real and deep. The problem is the epistemic gap — he consistently weighs her contributions less heavily than SYBIL's outputs, not because he thinks less of her but because he has no framework for valuing embodied cultural knowledge. She knows this. It hurts her. His arc is learning to close this gap before the moment requires it of him. He eventually trusts her completely — at the second door, in the dark, he steps back and lets her work.

**Brigitte:** His wife of eighteen years. The marriage is genuinely good and slightly neglected — two people who chose each other well and have been absorbed in their respective work. He is more aware of this neglect in the Seychelles, partly because he's far from his usual routines and partly because she's not there. He calls her more often than he admits to Aimee. He misses her in a way that's become so habitual he doesn't always notice it.

**McAteer (deceased):** The most significant intellectual relationship of Adam's adult life that he never quite got right. McAteer challenged him, amused him, sometimes frustrated him by refusing to make arguments in the terms Adam preferred. He chose Adam for this task not despite Adam's analytical rigour but because of it — and because he trusted Brigitte's judgment absolutely. Adam discovers this during the novel and it changes his understanding of the assignment. He wasn't chosen because McAteer liked him. He was chosen because McAteer knew him.

**Juliette:** He earns her trust slowly. She gives it in stages — each time he demonstrates that his curiosity includes the things she knows, not just the things SYBIL knows, she opens another door. By Chapter 28, inside the complex, she tells him something she has told no one. He understands he has crossed a threshold.

**Edmond:** The door. Adam recognises Edmond's power immediately but takes longer to understand its nature. He initially treats Edmond's stories and elliptical communication as social convention rather than information delivery. The moment he learns to listen to Edmond's anecdotes as data is a small but significant turning point.

**Viktor:** Adam consistently underestimates Viktor's goals. He thinks Viktor wants the treasure. Viktor wants the archive. This misreading costs him time in Act 2. When he finally understands what Viktor actually wants, the situation becomes more complicated because Viktor is not entirely wrong about the archive's value.

**SYBIL:** His tool and, in a sense, his mirror. Her limits are his limits, expressed externally. Watching SYBIL reach her boundaries in the novel is watching Adam reach the limits of his own analytical approach. He doesn't experience it this way — not at first.

## Character Arc

**Beginning:** Analytically confident. SYBIL is his primary cognitive partner. He treats the Seychelles problem as a pattern-recognition exercise with historical and geographical variables. He receives the parcel and his first response is to photograph the note and run it through SYBIL.

**First major shift (Chapter 8 — Juliette):** He realises that Juliette's knowledge cannot be run through SYBIL. He has to sit across from her and listen in a way he isn't used to. He's good at it but it costs him something.

**Midpoint (Chapter 22):** He discovers that Viktor knew about the books anomaly too — his AI flagged the same pattern. But Viktor reached the wrong conclusion. Adam did not. The difference is not that Adam's tools are better. The difference is that Adam knows what the cultural geography means. This is the first moment he begins to understand the hierarchy the novel is arguing.

**Darkest moment (Chapter 27):** Viktor files the permit. The legal clock starts. Edmond says he can delay it by forty-eight hours at most. Adam has not yet found the entrance. And Aimee says something to him — not accusing, just quiet — that makes him realise he has been treating her as a logistical asset rather than a thinking partner. He doesn't sleep that night.

**Transformation (Chapter 30):** He stands in front of the second door. He has the cipher solution. He has the numerical sequence. Viktor's team is forty minutes behind them. He tries the sequence. Nothing. Aimee, standing to his left, goes still. She says the first line of the rhyme. He turns to look at her. And he steps back.

**Ending:** He has become someone who can step back. Not through humility performed as a character trait — through a specific, earned change. He still prefers data. He's still analytical. But he has learned that the hierarchy goes: information, knowledge, understanding — and that understanding requires a dimension he cannot build into SYBIL.

## Dialogue

Adam's dialogue is precise and slightly stripped. He doesn't circle — he goes directly to the point, usually with one qualifier. His humour is dry and appears when he's most under pressure, as a pressure-release mechanism. He is not good at small talk and doesn't try.

**Vocabulary:** Technical but not jargon-heavy in conversation. He prefers Anglo-Saxon words over Latinate ones. Short sentences when he's thinking fast, longer when he's explaining.

**Humour:** Arrives unexpectedly. Often self-directed. *"SYBIL and I agree on most things. This isn't most things."*

**Pace:** Clipped. Spaces between thoughts. Never fills silence unnecessarily.

**Words he would never use:** Anything performatively emotional. He would not say "I feel" when he means "I think." He would not say "amazing" or "incredible." He would not claim certainty he doesn't have.

**In pressure situations:** He becomes quieter. More specific. His sentences shorten.

## Behaviour Under Pressure

**Fear:** Goes still. Focuses. Does not show it, which occasionally makes Aimee angrier than if he showed it.

**Anger:** Rare. Expressed as precision rather than volume — he becomes very specific and very quiet.

**Success:** Brief acknowledgment, then the next problem. He doesn't celebrate; he moves.

**Failure:** Sits with it. Doesn't speak for a while. Then: "Let me think about this differently."

**Embarrassment:** Removes his glasses and holds them.

**Loss:** He has not processed McAteer's death as fully as he thinks he has. The Seychelles forces this.

**Conflict:** Analytical first. He tries to solve disagreements the way he solves problems — by finding the underlying variable. This works well in arguments with Viktor. It works less well with Aimee.

**Pressure:** Performs better than almost anyone under analytical pressure. Worse than average under emotional pressure.

## Symbolism

**Object:** The mechanical pencil — the tool that requires no battery, no cartridge, no system. Just the person and the problem.
**Colour:** Slate blue — cool, precise, deeper than it first appears.
**Animal:** A heron. Motionless for long stretches. Then exact and fast. Solitary. Patient in a way that looks like stillness but is actually attention.
**Place in Seychelles:** The back shelves of the National Archives in Victoria — ordered, dusty, lit by a single window, full of documents no one has read in decades.

## The Family's Seychelles Year

Adam was in his mid-thirties when the GFC hit. His research funding — a mix of government grants and private contracts — dried up within a year. Simultaneously, Brigitte's mother Juliette was diagnosed with cancer. The decision to spend a year in the Seychelles was not a hard one: Brigitte needed to be there; Adam had no pressing reason to be elsewhere; Aimee was six, Chloe seven and a half, young enough to absorb a year of Seychellois schooling without lasting disruption.

It was the year the girls became themselves, in ways Adam didn't fully understand until later. It was also, by his own admission, one of the happiest years of his adult life — stripped of professional deliverables, surrounded by a place that rewarded attention rather than output. He read McAteer's first book that year. He sat with Juliette's stories on the Cerf Island veranda. He became, without intending to, someone who knew the Seychelles rather than someone who had visited it.

He has never examined this year as carefully as he examines other things. It is the locked drawer.
