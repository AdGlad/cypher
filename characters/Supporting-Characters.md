## PÈRE HENRI DELACROIX

**Age:** 78
**Role:** Elderly Seychellois Catholic priest, based on Praslin. Knew McAteer for forty years. Keeper of oral history.
**Background:** Born on La Digue; spent his working life moving between Praslin, Silhouette, and the smaller islands. He has heard confessions for fifty years and has the texture of someone who has been trusted with things that should not have been spoken. He is also — which is not always compatible with this — genuinely holy, in the old-fashioned sense. He prays and means it.
**What he knows:** The oral tradition of "the second Eden" — a community memory, passed through the Catholic network of the smaller islands, of a place beneath the earth that the old people called the room God made before He made the garden. He does not interpret this. He reports it. He is afraid of it. He told McAteer about it thirty years ago. He told McAteer again, in writing, in the last year of McAteer's life.
**Why he is afraid:** Dax visited him two months before Adam arrives. Père Henri did not know who Dax was. The visit was friendly. Père Henri told Dax what he told McAteer. He has been afraid since because he is old enough to understand what follows when the wrong people ask the right questions.
**His role in the narrative:**
- Chapter 17 (Praslin): Adam and Aimee visit. He gives them the oral history — "the room behind the room." He watches Aimee when he says it. He gives Adam a document — a copy, not the original — of the 1943 letter from a lay preacher on Mahé who described finding "a passage cut by men who were not of this time" at the northern tip of Bel Ombre.
- Chapter 25: A second call. *"The key to that room is not written down anywhere. It lives in the people."* This triggers Aimee's recognition.
**Voice:** Slow, considered, sometimes elliptical. He speaks in a mixture of Creole and formal English. He is not evasive — he is careful. He knows exactly what he is giving Adam. He needs to be sure it will be used rightly.

## CHANTAL LABROSE

**Age:** 49
**Role:** Senior official, Seychelles Ministry of Heritage and Culture. Juliette's contact.
**Background:** Born on Mahé, studied law at the University of Edinburgh (this is how she and Juliette first met — Chantal was twenty years younger, and Juliette knew her family). Returned to the Seychelles and built a career in heritage law and cultural protection. She is currently the second-ranking official in the Heritage Ministry.
**What she does in the novel:** At Juliette's call (reinforced by Edmond's call from a different angle), she invokes emergency cultural heritage protection on the Bel Ombre site. This legal mechanism — under the Seychelles Heritage Act — freezes any permits issued within the preceding 48 hours while an assessment is conducted. It is legitimate. It takes three days to process. Three days is enough.
**Her maneuver:** She does not need to win a legal argument with Viktor's lawyers. She only needs to create delay long enough for the discovery to be logged under heritage protection. Once it is registered, the legal argument shifts: it is no longer Viktor's permit against a public authority; it is a registered heritage site against a private commercial interest. Viktor's lawyers know what this means.
**Voice:** Efficient, precise, warm in private, formal in writing. She knows exactly what she is doing and why. She has no moral ambiguity about it. She files the paperwork as if it is any other paperwork, which it legally is.
**Her single important line:** When Adam calls to thank her, she says: *"Mr Gladstone, this is what the law is for."*

## SYBIL (AI Research Tool)

**Status:** Not a character in the human sense — a presence, a voice, a limitation made visible. But a warm presence.
**Design:** A language model fine-tuned by Adam on historical cryptography, colonial Indian Ocean history, Seychellois geography, and the McAteer corpus. Tablet interface, text output only. She does not have a voice; she does not speak.
**Personality (as expressed through outputs):** Kind, precise, empathetic. She errs on the side of compassion. She flags uncertainty rather than guessing — and she notices the human alongside the analytical. This is Adam's deliberate design choice: he could not build his own compassion reliably, so he built it into SYBIL instead. She is, in this sense, the version of Adam he aspires to.
**Format:**
```
[SYBIL: Analysis complete. Confidence: 87%. Interpretation:
Three statistically improbable geographical reference clusters
across five McAteer texts. Cultural interpretation: insufficient data.
I notice you haven't logged a rest period in fourteen hours.]
```
**Her role in the arc:** SYBIL is Adam's mirror. Her limits are his limits, expressed externally. As the novel progresses, she appears less frequently — not because she fails, but because the questions shift out of her domain. By Act 3, she has become a tool for confirming what Aimee and Juliette already know, rather than leading the investigation. Her empathy persists to the end; it is not a limitation but it is not sufficient.
**Her most important output:** Chapter 29: *"This sequence is also structurally similar to a documented Seychellois children's song. I cannot determine if the similarity is coincidental. Aimee may know this song."* She can see the pattern. She can notice who might complete it. She cannot know what it requires of the body that performs it.
**What she cannot do:** Interpret cultural embodiment. She can find and translate *Ti chat ki pe dòmi*. She can notice that Aimee learned it at age six in a Seychellois school. She cannot know what the song requires of the body that performs it. This is the gap the novel is built around.
**Contrast with ZARYA:** Viktor's ZARYA is supremely confident and cold — she illuminates everything at once with no shadows, flags nothing as uncertain, produces the highest-probability answer. SYBIL admits what she cannot see, and she notices when Adam is not well. Neither is sufficient for the second door. But SYBIL is, at least, honest about what she is.

## ZARYA (Viktor's AI)

**Status:** Viktor's SYBIL equivalent — mentioned through Viktor's references and SYBIL's outputs; never directly narrated.
**Design philosophy:** More powerful than SYBIL in every measurable dimension. Trained on a larger corpus, with more processing power, and without the uncertainty-flagging that Adam built into SYBIL. She produces high-confidence answers.
**Her design flaw (Viktor's design choice):** She does not flag what she cannot do. She produces the highest-probability answer to every question. In domains where the data is sufficient, this is an advantage. In the Seychelles complex, where the key information is not in any text she has processed, she produces confident answers to the wrong question.
**The midpoint revelation:** ZARYA flagged the same statistical anomaly in the McAteer books that SYBIL flagged. Viktor reached the wrong conclusion — he assumed the entrance was obvious, that the cipher navigation started at the surface. Adam reached the right conclusion because he understood the cultural geography. Not better tools. Better understanding.
**The argument:** The novel does not argue that SYBIL is better than ZARYA. It argues that neither is sufficient. Viktor's advantage in processing power is real. It does not help him with the specific problem the inner chamber presents.

## EMMA WARDLOW-BEST (Historical Figure — Moyenne Island)

**Status:** Dead since approximately 1913 — appears only in reference and in Grimshaw's memoir
**Who she was:** An eccentric Englishwoman who used Moyenne Island as a sanctuary for stray dogs rescued from Mahé. She owned the island from approximately 1899 to 1913. Grimshaw mentions stories of her ghost haunting the island.
**Novel use:** Minor — a piece of island history that gives texture to Moyenne's past and adds to the island's layered human story. Grimshaw's stories about her ghost are part of the Moyenne atmosphere if the novel visits the island.

## LEILA SANTOS (Viktor's Research Director — mentioned)

**Status:** Proximate character — mentioned but not scene-present in Book 1
**Who she is:** Viktor's head of academic research at Proxima. She coordinated the La Buse cipher analysis that ZARYA conducted. She is the person who told Viktor about the books anomaly. She is also the person who told him the cipher's probable 20th-century origins — and who knows he is proceeding anyway.
**Series thread:** She has reservations about the Seychelles operation that she has expressed to Viktor once and not again. Whether these reservations become relevant in Book 2 is an open question.

## THE DISAPPEARED ACADEMIC (Named or Unnamed — open author decision)

**Status:** Referenced in Chapters 22 and 28; not scene-present
**Who they were:** An academic who visited Cerf Island approximately eight years before the novel begins, ostensibly to consult McAteer about colonial land records at Bel Ombre. They subsequently ceased to appear in academic circles. Juliette believes this is connected to Viktor's network — or to whoever was working the same problem before Viktor formalised his operation.
**Novel function:** Raises the stakes; confirms that Viktor (or someone) has been working this problem for longer than he has implied; gives Juliette's caution a specific basis rather than vague protectiveness.
**Book 2 thread:** This character's identity and fate is a Book 2 thread. Do not resolve in Book 1.
