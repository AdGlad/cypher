# Raymonde — Character File

---

## Basic Profile

**Full name:** Raymonde (family name TBD)
**Age:** Late sixties — approximately Juliette's contemporary; they met when both were around eighteen
**Nationality:** Seychellois-French (French roots; born or raised in the Seychelles; French is her first language)
**Occupation:** Formerly: owner and operator of Fisherman's Cove hotel and marina, Beau Vallon beach, Mahé. Currently: beekeeper, market gardener, keeper of dogs and one large white parrot. She would not describe herself as retired — she would say she changed direction.
**Languages:** French (primary — she addresses everyone in French regardless of their language). Seychellois Creole (fluent — she has lived here all her adult life). English (functional — she uses it when she must and with slightly theatrical reluctance).

**Appearance:** A tall woman, lean from outdoor work. She has the kind of face that has become more itself with age rather than less — sharper, more particular. Hair gone silver-white, kept long and pinned up loosely. Her clothes are practical and slightly chaotic — she dresses the way someone dresses when they no longer have anyone to dress for and find this liberating. Her hands are a beekeeper's hands: careful, patient, slightly scarred from work that demands attention.

**Mannerisms:** Calls everyone *mon cher* — men, women, dogs, the parrot, occasionally inanimate objects that have disappointed her. Speaks French at people and expects them to keep up. Moves through her property with the ease of someone who knows every inch of it in all weathers. Has a habit of beginning sentences with *"You see—"* and then not explaining what you are supposed to see, because she assumes you will see it in a moment if you pay attention.

**Household:**
- One large white parrot (African grey or cockatoo — TBD). It speaks. Its vocabulary consists almost entirely of the names of men who have stayed at the house over the years, which Raymonde does not want repeated in company. The parrot has no such discretion. This is a recurring source of mild social chaos.
- Several dogs (number and breed TBD — working dogs, not ornamental)
- Beehives — she keeps several hives and produces honey she sells at the Mahé market
- Market garden — vegetables, herbs, some fruit. She grows what she eats and gives away the excess

**Location:** A remote house near Bel Ombre, northern Mahé. She has lived here since being forced out of Fisherman's Cove. The house is old, stone, surrounded by the garden she has built. The tidal channel at the Bel Ombre entrance is within walking distance.

## Background

**Fisherman's Cove:** Raymonde owned and ran Fisherman's Cove — the hotel and marina at Beau Vallon beach — for approximately twenty years. She was a good host and a shrewd operator. The hotel was known for warmth and genuine quality rather than corporate scale.

**Bill and Juliette met here.** McAteer came to an evening social event at Fisherman's Cove in the late 1970s. Juliette was there as Raymonde's friend. They were introduced. This is the hinge on which the McAteer marriage turned, and therefore the hinge on which Brigitte, Aimee, and Chloe exist. Raymonde has never been particularly modest about this fact.

**Forced out:** Developers — the same commercial machinery that has been working the Seychelles coastline for decades — applied enough pressure (legal, financial, bureaucratic) to make continued ownership untenable. Raymonde fought for several years. She lost. The hotel changed hands. She took what she could and retreated.

She did not go far. She went to Bel Ombre, where the land is harder to develop, where the granite runs down to the sea in a way that has so far discouraged the kind of investment that took her hotel. She bought an old stone house and set about building a different life.

**Her connection to Viktor's operation:** The developers who forced her out were not Viktor, but they were the same class of interest — commercial extraction dressed in permits and legal instruments. When she understands what Viktor's survey operation is, it is not abstract. She has seen this before. She knows exactly how it ends.

## Psychology

**Biggest fear:** That the Seychelles she has loved — the particular, stubborn, eccentric Seychelles that resists being managed — will eventually be managed. That the thing she retreated to will follow her.

**Biggest regret:** Not fighting harder for Fisherman's Cove. She knows she probably would have lost anyway. She still thinks about it.

**Core belief:** That you live where you live and you pay attention to it. The garden, the bees, the tidal channel she has watched for thirty years — this is not philosophy. It is practice.

**What she knows about the entrance:** She has seen the channel at low tide more times than she can count. She noticed, over the years, that the water pulls back in a particular way in that specific stretch of granite — differently from the surrounding rock. She went in once, three or four years ago. She followed the channel as the tide receded. She felt the air change — cooler, different. She went far enough to put her hand on the granite walls of a passage wider than it should be for a natural formation. Then she came back out. She has not gone back. She told Juliette about it before Juliette died — the same night, the way you tell your oldest friend something you need witnessed. Juliette said: *"Don't go back. Someone will come."*

**Internal conflict:** She has been waiting eleven years since Juliette died. When Adam and Aimee arrive at her door, she understands immediately that someone has come.

## Role in the Novel

**Chapter 26 — The visit to Raymonde:** Adam and Aimee come to her. (She has been told to expect them, via Edmond, who did not explain at length.) She feeds them, deploys *mon cher* at both of them, lets the parrot cause a brief awkwardness, and then takes them to the edge of the property where you can see the Bel Ombre beach and the granite headland. She tells them about the channel. She tells them she went in once. She does not describe what she felt — she shows them where to look.

**Her recognition of Aimee:** Immediate. She speaks to Aimee in French and Creole and switches between them without pausing. She sees what Aimee carries — the same quality of attentiveness she recognised in Juliette. Before Adam and Aimee leave, she takes Aimee's hand and says something in Creole that Adam does not catch. Aimee nods.

**Chapter 29 — Contacting Chantal:** Without telling Adam, and without being asked, Raymonde calls Chantal Labrose at the Ministry of Heritage. She has known Chantal for years through the Seychelles social fabric — they are not close friends, but Chantal's family knew Raymonde's family. Raymonde explains what Viktor's survey operation is and what the Bel Ombre site represents. She invokes emergency cultural heritage protection. This is personal: she was pushed off her land by exactly this kind of operation. She will not watch it happen to the land she now looks at every morning.

She does not tell Adam she has done this. He finds out when Chantal's filing is confirmed.

**At the entrance (Chapter 30/31):** Raymonde is there. She stands at the top of the granite with Edmond while Adam and Aimee go in. She has earned this moment across thirty years of paying attention.

## Relationships

**Juliette (deceased):** The defining friendship of Raymonde's life. They met at eighteen and were close until Juliette's death in 2015. Juliette's death settled something in Raymonde — she stopped pretending she was going back to anything. The house, the garden, the bees: this is what Raymonde built after losing her. When Aimee arrives, Raymonde sees Juliette in her — not literally, but in the quality of knowing she carries. This is not sentimental; it is accurate.

**Edmond:** Old friends in the way of people who have been part of the same social fabric for fifty years. They are not intimate — their connection runs through Juliette. After Juliette died, they maintained a careful, respectful warmth. He is the one who tells Adam and Aimee to go to her. She is the one who contacts Chantal without telling him she's going to — he finds out after, and is not surprised.

**Brigitte:** Raymonde knew Brigitte as a child during the McAteer family's Seychelles visits. She recognised in Brigitte what she recognised in Juliette — the specific intelligence of a woman who sees clearly and chooses carefully when to say so. She and Brigitte have a warm ease that requires no maintenance.

**Aimee:** Immediate recognition. Raymonde is the first adult outside the family who treats Aimee as the most important person in the investigation. Not because she knows the specifics, but because she can see it.

**Adam:** She likes him more than she expected to. She was prepared for a brilliant man who doesn't listen. He listens better than she expected. She calls him *mon cher* with the same frequency as everyone else, which means she has decided he is part of this.

**Viktor:** She does not meet Viktor. She does not need to. She has already filed the paperwork that stops him.

**The parrot:** The parrot has no loyalty to anyone and announces information without judgment. Raymonde has accepted this as the price of his company.

## Dialogue

Raymonde speaks in French by default, switches to Creole with Aimee and Brigitte, and deploys English as a last resort. Her English is perfectly good but she treats it as a courtesy she is extending rather than a natural register.

**Vocabulary:** Direct, concrete, occasionally startling. She does not circle around things.

**Humour:** Dry and self-aware. She is funny about her own situation in a way that does not invite pity.

**Key lines:**
- *"Mon cher, you are looking at the wrong rock. Look at where the water goes."* (to Adam, at Bel Ombre)
- *"She said someone would come. You are later than I expected, but you are here."* (to Aimee)
- *"I made one call. It is what the law is for."* (when Adam learns about Chantal — echoing Chantal's own line back at him)

## Symbolism

**Object:** A jar of her own honey — she gives one to Aimee when they leave. Sweetness produced from attention and patience and a willingness to get stung occasionally.
**The parrot:** Everything that wants to stay private, doesn't. Knowledge finds its way out.
**The garden:** The life you build after the life you planned is taken from you. Not a consolation — a different thing, grown from the same ground.
