# Brigitte Gladstone — Character File

---

## Basic Profile

**Full name:** Brigitte Anne Gladstone (née McAteer)
**Age:** 44
**Nationality:** British (born Seychelles — moved to Britain in her late teens)
**Occupation:** Marine biologist; research fellow, University of Edinburgh. Her work is on Indian Ocean coral ecosystems. Her specialism gives her a professional relationship with the Seychelles that exists independently of her family connection.
**Education:** BSc Marine Biology, University of Portsmouth. PhD Marine Ecology, University of Edinburgh — where she met Adam at a faculty dinner he was taken to by a colleague and almost didn't attend.
**Languages:** English (native). French (fluent). Seychellois Creole (native-level — her mother tongue before English). She speaks Creole with Aimee in private. She and Aimee have a private language built from Creole, French, and references Adam doesn't share.
**Appearance:** Brigitte has the appearance of someone whose face has been shaped by outdoor work — she has spent decades in boats and on reefs. Darker than she was at twenty. Moves economically, without waste. Her hands are a marine biologist's hands — weathered, capable. She is often described by people who know her as more striking than conventionally beautiful, which is accurate.
**Mannerisms:** Direct eye contact. Waits for people to finish before speaking. Occasionally does three things simultaneously and does all three well. When something worries her, she becomes very organised — schedules, lists, systems.
**Health:** Excellent. Physically active; she does not sit still easily.
**Strengths:** Practical intelligence. Emotional clarity — she knows what she feels and knows when to act on it. The capacity to see both Adam and Aimee with equal clarity and love both without taking sides. Professional competence that is entirely separate from the family drama and which gives her an independence neither Adam nor Aimee quite sees.
**Weaknesses:** Can be impatient with people who take longer to reach obvious conclusions. Has her father's tendency to process things privately and only share when she's ready — Adam has learned to wait for this. Sometimes her practical orientation means she solves the wrong version of a problem.
**Skills:** Ocean navigation, diving, marine surveying. The same ability to read landscape that Aimee has — but for underwater landscape. An administrator's capacity for managing multiple people and timelines simultaneously. The best cook in the family.

---

## Psychology

**Biggest fear:** That she is sending Adam and Aimee into something she doesn't fully understand, and that her father's final act of trust was also an act of risk she agreed to without knowing what she was agreeing to.

**Biggest regret:** She was not in the Seychelles when McAteer died. She had planned to visit that spring. She moved the trip once, then he was gone. She and Adam share this particular kind of unfinished business, though they arrived at it from different directions.

**Core belief:** That the Seychelles is real in a way other places are not, and that whatever her father found there — in the history, in the landscape, in the community — matters in a way that should be taken seriously by serious people.

**Internal conflict:** She is not on the island for most of the novel, and she finds this harder than she expected. She made the practical decision to let Adam and Aimee go without her — her field season was committed, her students needed her. She knows it was the right decision. She is not certain she made it for the right reasons — there is also something in her that didn't want to be in the room when her father's work became the novel's problem.

**What she knows that she doesn't tell Adam:** That McAteer's private note to her was different from the one Adam received. McAteer wrote to Brigitte first, months before his death: *"I have left the books to Gladstone. Make sure he takes Aimee. She is the reason the rest works."* Brigitte has been carrying this. She suggested Aimee accompany Adam because McAteer asked her to. She has not told Adam this.

---

## Role in the Novel

Brigitte is largely off-stage after Chapter 5 — she appears in the opening chapters, then communicates by phone and occasional message. She is not marginalised by this; she is load-bearing in a way the reader gradually understands.

Her most significant act in the novel happens before Adam's investigation begins: she suggests that Aimee accompany Adam. She does this casually, in the way she does everything that matters. Adam thinks it's an impulse. It is not.

Her second most significant act: she is the one Adam calls when he finally understands what Viktor actually wants (the archive, not the treasure). She says, after a pause: *"Dad knew that. That's why he chose you instead of someone who would make it public."* This reframes Adam's understanding of the assignment.

She appears in the closing section, arriving in the Seychelles after the discovery. The final scene of the novel involves Brigitte on Moyenne Island — she is there for professional reasons (the coral surveys she's been planning for years) as well as personal ones. She and Aimee are together on the water. Adam is on the island watching them. This is the right ending arrangement.

## Relationships

**Adam:** Eighteen years of genuine marriage — two people who chose each other well and who have both been absorbed in their respective work. The marriage is slightly under-attended without being troubled. She understands Adam better than he understands himself, which she finds occasionally maddening. She tells him the truth, directly, which he depends on even when he doesn't want to hear it. Their conversations by phone during the novel are among the clearest exchanges in the book — more honest than the conversations he has with anyone in the Seychelles.

**Aimee:** Their relationship is easy in the way Aimee's relationship with Adam is not. Brigitte sees Aimee's knowledge as knowledge — she doesn't have to overcome a framework to do this. They communicate in Creole. They understand each other's silences. Brigitte's faith in Aimee is absolute and unspoken. Aimee knows it. It sustains her during the novel without either of them naming it.

**McAteer:** Their relationship was complex and close. She is his daughter; she carries his methodical intelligence and also the things he couldn't write down. She is not grieving cleanly — McAteer's death was not a surprise (he had been unwell) but it opened a gap she had not prepared for. Being off-stage during Adam's investigation is, in part, an act of self-protection she would not name as such.

**Juliette:** They love each other. They have been mother and daughter in every practical sense. Brigitte was Juliette's student before she was a scientist — in the Creole, in the landscape, in the community. Their reunion in the final chapters is brief and quiet and weighted.

**Edmond:** Uncle. He was the adult who was always present in Brigitte's childhood when McAteer was absorbed in his research. She knows Edmond better than anyone — she knows which of his stories contain information and which are simply his enjoyment of his own voice. She is the one who first suggested Adam call on Edmond when the investigation began.

---

## Voice

Brigitte's dialogue is direct, warm, and precise. She does not waste words. She does not perform emotion. When she says something difficult, she says it clearly and then waits. She has her father's patience with what she cannot yet confirm.

Her calls to Adam during the novel function as orientation points — she grounds him, asks the right questions, and says what he needs to hear at the moments he most needs it.

Her physical presence in the closing chapter brings a different quality to the narrative — Brigitte in the Seychelles is someone slightly released from everything she manages elsewhere.

---

## Symbolism

**Object:** A dive computer on her wrist — she has worn one for twenty years. It measures depth, time underwater, and ascent rate. A different kind of tool from Adam's mechanical pencil; same era; same principle.
**Colour:** Deep ocean blue — still, deep, containing more than the surface suggests.
**Place in Seychelles:** The water around Moyenne Island — where she has been planning a coral survey for three years and where she finally arrives at the novel's end.
