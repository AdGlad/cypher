# Chloe Gladstone — Character File

---

## Basic Profile

**Full name:** Chloe Marie Gladstone
**Age:** 25 (born 1 January 2000 — two years and six months older than Aimee)
**Nationality:** Australian (born Australia; Seychellois heritage through Brigitte)
**Occupation:** Marine environmental consultant, based in Perth. Works on reef assessment and coastal development impact projects. She chose her mother's world, more or less — the practical applied version of it.
**Education:** BSc Environmental Science, University of Western Australia. Working toward a professional qualification in marine impact assessment.
**Languages:** English (native). Seychellois Creole (genuinely good — she was nine during the Seychelles year, old enough to acquire it well but not quite at the absorption age Aimee was at seven). French (functional — better than Aimee's, less fluid than Brigitte's). She knows the Creole but she uses it more consciously than Aimee does; it doesn't slip out involuntarily the way Aimee's does.
**Appearance:** More like Adam than Brigitte — taller, fairer, the build of someone who swims. More physically organised than Aimee; her clothes fit and are where she left them. Has Brigitte's directness in the face; has Adam's eyes.
**Mannerisms:** Practical and quick to act. Does not linger on a problem — identifies the most probable solution, commits to it, revises if wrong. Impatient with extended deliberation. When she's wrong, she says so without drama and moves on. This is her best quality. She is often the first person to say the obvious thing that everyone else has been circling.
**Health:** Excellent. Surfs. Runs. Does not treat her body as something to be managed.
**Strengths:** Clear, fast thinking. Practical competence — she can read a situation and identify the next action without needing the full picture. Loyalty. She will not leave someone behind. Honesty — she does not say things she doesn't mean and does not mean things she doesn't say.
**Weaknesses:** Can move to action before the situation has fully resolved itself — she solves the problem in front of her rather than the problem beneath it. Less patient than Aimee with ambiguity. Has a slight tendency to treat Aimee's knowledge as softer than it is, which she would immediately deny if challenged.
**Skills:** Reef assessment, coastal navigation, marine survey methodology. Practical Creole (can hold a conversation, negotiate a price, make a friend). Reads maps faster than anyone in the family. Can fix most things.

## The Seychelles — Annual Holidays and the 2008 Year

From early childhood, Chloe and Aimee travelled to the Seychelles every Australian winter (June–August) for up to six weeks, staying at Bill and Juliette's house in Glacis, northern Mahé. They went to Beau Vallon beach, explored the islands by boat (Amit's *Sea Shell*), and visited Cerf Island to see Edmond. Adam came sometimes; other times he remained in Australia for work. These annual visits gave Chloe and Aimee a childhood geography of Mahé — Beau Vallon, Glacis, the inner islands — that is embedded rather than visited.

**The 2008 year:** Chloe was nine during the family's full year in the Seychelles. She and Aimee attended the same school (the Independent School, Victoria, Mahé). Chloe's Creole came quickly — she was competitive about it, in the way older children are competitive, wanting to match her younger sister. She did match her, almost. But the gap between nine and seven is real: Aimee's Creole went in at the cellular level; Chloe's went in as a language she learned.

She remembers the year vividly and consciously. The school, the friends, the beach, the tortoises on Moyenne Island. She can tell you what happened; Aimee can show you what it felt like.

She also knows *Ti chat ki pe dòmi*. She learned it at school, sang it in the playground, knows every word. She could recite it now if asked.

She cannot perform it.

This is the distinction the novel is built around, and Chloe is its clearest illustration: knowing a thing is not the same as having it in your body. Chloe knows the rhyme. Aimee's body knows the sequence. When Aimee opens the second door, Chloe is standing beside her — knowing every word, and unable to do what her younger sister does.

## Psychology

**Biggest fear:** That she's wrong about something important and won't know it until it's too late. She moves fast; fast movement and blind spots go together.

**Core belief:** That most problems are practical problems, and practical problems have practical solutions. She is right about this more often than anyone expects. She is wrong about it at the second door.

**Internal conflict:** She is proud of Aimee — genuinely, without qualification. She is also aware that the thing Aimee has is something she doesn't have, and she has spent twenty years not quite examining this. The second door forces the examination.

**Her relationship with her own Creole knowledge:** She has never thought of it as insufficient. She has good Creole. She can do things with it. The moment in Chapter 29 when SYBIL flags the rhyme and Aimee says *"I know what it is"* — Chloe also knows what it is, and she thinks she understands. She doesn't yet understand the difference between knowing and having.

## Relationships

**Aimee:** The defining relationship of both their lives. They are close in the way siblings are close when they've moved through the same experiences in genuinely different ways. Chloe is protective without being patronising — usually. She has always been slightly ahead in the conventional markers (older, faster to finish things, first to get a job) and has occasionally, without meaning to, used this to calibrate Aimee against a scale that doesn't fit Aimee. She would be horrified to know Aimee has noticed. Aimee has noticed and has never said.

**Adam:** She has always found him easier than Aimee has. She thinks in a register closer to Adam's — practical, direct, problem-oriented. She respects him. She also sees, clearly, that he underestimates Aimee, and she is more willing than Brigitte to say so to his face. She does, once, in Act 2. Adam doesn't receive it well. She was right anyway.

**Brigitte:** They argue more than Brigitte and Aimee do, and are more alike. Chloe is the one who pushes back on Brigitte's decisions rather than accepting them. Brigitte values this, even when she doesn't show it.

**Juliette (deceased, 2015):** Warm and slightly formal — Chloe loved Juliette, though Juliette's knowledge was not Chloe's knowledge. She was respectful, attentive, and honest with her. Juliette was fond of her and trusted her practically. The deeper recognition — the one Juliette had with Aimee — was not there, and neither of them minded.

**Viktor:** She clocks him immediately and dislikes him with more specificity than Aimee does. Aimee distrusts him instinctively; Chloe dislikes him analytically — she can name what she doesn't trust. She is, in one exchange, unexpectedly effective against Dax: she identifies him as operational before Adam does, because she reads practical intentions clearly.

## Role in the Novel

Chloe is present throughout. She is not the novel's central consciousness — that is Adam and Aimee — but she is the family's practical engine and its most direct voice. She:

- Organises logistics that Adam doesn't think about and Aimee would deprioritise
- Says the obvious thing that moves the plot forward when everyone else is deliberating
- Has the exchange with Adam in Act 2 that he doesn't receive well but that plants something
- Reads Dax correctly before Adam does
- Stands beside Aimee at the second door — knowing the rhyme, unable to perform it, watching her younger sister do the thing she cannot

Her ending is not as transformed as Aimee's. She goes back to Perth and her work. But she goes back having understood something she hadn't understood before. The second door is hers too — she just couldn't open it.

## Dialogue

Fast, direct, occasionally blunt. She says what she means. Her humour is quick and situational — she notices the absurdity in things before anyone else and doesn't always wait to see if it's appropriate to name it. When she's wrong: brief acknowledgment, then forward.

**Key line:** After the second door opens and they are inside, Chloe is quiet for a long time. Then, to Aimee, not to anyone else: *"You knew, didn't you. Since Beau Vallon."*

Aimee: *"I thought I did."*

Chloe: *"You did."*

## Symbolism

**Object:** A battered waterproof notebook — she has always been the one who writes things down practically, so they don't get lost.
**Colour:** Sand — warm, shifting, more useful than it looks.
**Place in Seychelles:** The water itself, not the shore — she is most herself moving through something.
