# Rivals in Eden — Plot Candidates

*All elements below are real historical details from William McAteer's Rivals in Eden (1742–1827), confirmed from the repaired OCR scan. Each entry notes the novel use.*

---

## Real Characters

### Jean-François Hodoul — The Privateer
**What McAteer records:** A privateer who captured the English privateer *Henriette* near Sainte Anne island on 28 May 1800. McAteer states explicitly that this action "gave rise to several of the reports of buried treasure in Seychelles." Hodoul later settled in Seychelles, built a house at Les Mamelles south of Victoria, owned a plantation on Silhouette Island. Died 5 January 1835, buried in the town cemetery. He signed the 1794 capitulation to Britain alongside Hangard.

**Novel use:**
- There are at least two buried treasure legends in the Seychelles — La Buse and Hodoul
- Viktor's AI conflates them; it cannot distinguish between a 17th-century pirate's hidden complex and a 19th-century privateer's prize money without local contextual knowledge
- Hodoul's treasure story is a real, documented false trail built into the historical record
- Juliette (or Edmond) knows the difference; Adam has to learn it early

---

### Jean-Baptiste Quéau de Quinssy — The Strategic Surrenderer
**What McAteer records:** Colonial commandant who surrendered the Seychelles to the British multiple times while preserving French colonial culture intact. His policy was formal capitulation combined with practical continuity. McAteer describes his portrait in the Seychelles museum — "the mouth, twisted in a smirk." He married twice in the Seychelles, served until the islands were formally ceded to Britain.

**Novel use:**
- Thematic parallel for the novel's central argument: deliberate concealment can be a form of protection
- Juliette can reference Quinssy explicitly when explaining why McAteer chose not to publish: *"Quinssy surrendered the islands to save them. William kept quiet about the archive for the same reason."*
- His "smirking portrait" in the Seychelles museum is a real object Adam could encounter

---

### Alexis-Marie de Rochon — Astronomer and Scientist
**What McAteer records:** A French astronomer who visited the Seychelles, studied the Coco de Mer in depth, and theorised about how its seeds dispersed across the Indian Ocean. Described as a "French traveller and scientist." He argued about the routes to the Indies, made astronomical observations, and wrote about the natural history of the islands.

**Novel use:**
- Direct historical model for Van Houten, the archive's fictional scientist
- Rochon was real and documented; Van Houten is invented in the same mould
- His Coco de Mer observations can be referenced — Van Houten's archive contains what Rochon only theorised

---

### Nageon de l'Étang — Colonial Assembly Leader
**What McAteer records:** A prominent settler and political figure in the colonial assembly. His wife, Madame Nageon de l'Étang, appears later as a significant social figure. The family recurs throughout the colonial period. Nageon signed the capitulation document.

**Novel use:**
- A real family name that Edmond or Juliette can reference as part of the old Seychellois network
- The kind of established family whose descendants still hold social influence
- Adds authentic depth to Edmond's description of who really runs the Seychelles

---

### Pierre Hangard — First Landowner of Bel Ombre
**What McAteer records:** A former soldier who acquired "an immense piece of land along North West Bay (today's Beau Vallon and Bel Ombre)." Described as unscrupulous but effective. The most powerful settler of the early colony. Signed the 1794 British capitulation. His descendants are part of the land ownership chain.

**Novel use:**
- The Bel Ombre land ownership chain begins with Hangard in the 1770s
- His signature on the 1794 capitulation means the land ownership crossed the French-British transition intact
- SYBIL flags the recurrence of the Hangard name (and its variants) across all five McAteer books as a statistical anomaly

## Real Events

### The 1793–1794 Capitulation
**What McAteer records:** Quinssy surrendered formally to the British in May 1794. Signed by Quinssy, Hangard, Nageon de l'Étang, Cornier Bellevaut, and Commodore Newcome of HMS Orpheus. The document is reproduced in the book.

**Novel use:**
- Hangard's signature connects Bel Ombre land ownership to the formal British takeover
- The capitulation document itself is a real historical object Adam could find at the National Archives in Victoria
- The names on the document are the same names in the land ownership records — a thread SYBIL connects

---

### Poivre Orders the Coco de Mer Kept Secret
**What McAteer records:** When Poivre discovered the Coco de Mer on Praslin, he "decided that the location of the palm should remain secret" to protect its rarity and value. The secrecy failed — Duchemin returned the following year to load a cargo of nuts for the Indian market, attracting English attention.

**Novel use:**
- A direct historical precedent for deliberate concealment of a natural discovery in the Seychelles
- Père Henri references this: the Seychelles has always hidden its treasures — not just pirate gold
- The Coco de Mer secrecy decision is the philosophical ancestor of hiding the archive
- Poivre's failed secrecy (someone always talks) foreshadows the archive's eventual discovery

---

### The Malavois Survey with Secret Instructions
**What McAteer records:** A detailed survey of the Seychelles conducted in the late 1780s by Malavois, accompanied by a surveyor named Bataille. The survey was accompanied by "secret instructions" given separately to the commandant. A previous surveyor "was only able to mark the greatest" features — the Cap Ternay area was incompletely charted.

**Novel use:**
- The incomplete Cap Ternay survey is a documented gap in colonial records
- In the novel, a footnote in *Hard Times in Paradise* references this survey and the specific area that was not charted — the tidal channel at the base of the headland
- The "secret instructions" accompanying the survey add to the pattern of official concealment
- Adam finds the survey report reference in McAteer; SYBIL notes the gap in coverage aligns precisely with the Bel Ombre entrance coordinates

---

### The Coco de Mer's Near-Extinction
**What McAteer records:** The Coco de Mer "was rescued from extinction thanks, in part, to the intervention of General Gordon" (of Khartoum). The destruction of giant tortoises and turtles by both French and English colonists is documented. The giant tortoise was "virtually extinct by 1830."

**Novel use:**
- The archive documents the Coco de Mer and other species at their pre-colonial abundance
- Van Houten's biological records are not just historically interesting — they are a baseline for conservation science
- The Coco de Mer's near-extinction and rescue is a direct parallel to the archive's concealment and recovery

## Real Geographical Details

### Bel Ombre Beach Rock Carvings
**What McAteer records (Footnote 27):** The La Buse cipher was deciphered in the 1920s by Charles de la Roncière and "subsequently linked to mysterious carvings on rocks at Bel Ombre beach on Mahé island." Early excavations found only two coffins with human remains and a body in the sand — pirates killed helpers to keep burial locations secret.

**Novel use:**
- The rock carvings are the misdirection — they sent every previous searcher digging in the wrong place
- Adam finds the carvings early in Act 2. They are real, impressive, clearly intentional. Viktor's team has already catalogued them.
- SYBIL's analysis: the carvings are navigational markers, not a treasure map — they point toward the water, not into the hillside
- The human remains confirm pirate activity at the site without confirming treasure location

---

### The Pierre de Possession
**What McAteer records:** The stone carved by Morphey in 1756 to claim the island for France — "its carving now eroded and vandalised, possibly during the Revolution — is on display at the National History Museum in Victoria."

**Novel use:**
- A real object in a real museum in Victoria
- Adam visits it on Day 1 — his first contact with a physically inscribed piece of Seychelles history
- The carving's vandalism "possibly during the Revolution" is a reminder that history is not stable — it is contested, damaged, rewritten
- Thematic resonance: the most official claim France ever made on the Seychelles has been "eroded and vandalised"

---

### North West Bay as Hidden Anchorage
**What McAteer records:** Ships anchored at North West Bay during the south-east monsoon specifically because it was "more sheltered" and "out of sight" from Victoria. Access to the town required crossing the mountain via a pass.

**Novel use:**
- Historically confirmed that the northwest coast was operationally screened from Victoria
- Explains why the complex entrance was never stumbled upon — it sits in a documented blind spot
- The mountain pass route confirms the geography: you cannot see Bel Ombre from Victoria

---

### The Incomplete Cap Ternay Survey
**What McAteer records:** A 1793 surveyor notes that "the surveyor here before me was only able to mark the greatest" features in the Cap Ternay area. The previous survey was incomplete.

**Novel use:**
- The entrance channel sits in the gap of the colonial survey record
- McAteer documents the gap without knowing its significance
- Adam cross-referencing the survey reference in one book with the reef reference in another is the moment the entrance location becomes precise

## The Core False Trail

### Hodoul's Treasure vs. La Buse's Complex
The historical record contains two distinct Seychelles treasure legends:
1. **La Buse** — pirate, cipher, Bel Ombre, 1730 execution
2. **Hodoul** — privateer, captured prizes near Sainte Anne, 1800

Viktor's AI ingests all five McAteer books and cannot cleanly separate these two legends. The AI identifies multiple treasure-related geographical references and cannot weight them correctly without understanding the cultural difference between a pirate's long-term hidden complex and a privateer's short-term prize money.

Juliette knows the difference immediately. She does not explain it to Adam until he asks the right question — which he only asks after SYBIL produces a confused output that merges both legends into a single probability map.

**This is a scene:** Adam shows Juliette SYBIL's merged treasure-location output. Juliette looks at it for a moment. *"She's conflating La Buse and Hodoul."* Adam: *"They're the same person?"* Juliette: *"No. That's the problem."*
