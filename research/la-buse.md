# Research — La Buse (Olivier Levasseur)

---

## Basic Facts

**Full name:** Olivier Levasseur
**Nickname:** La Buse ("The Buzzard") — for his swiftness in attack
**Born:** c. 1688, Calais (or Boulogne), France
**Died:** 7 July 1730, Saint-Denis, Réunion — executed by hanging
**Active period:** c. 1716–1730

---

## Career Overview

- Began as a privateer in the War of Spanish Succession
- Transitioned to piracy in the Caribbean c. 1716
- Formed alliances with notable pirates including Benjamin Hornigold and Paulsgrave Williams
- Moved operations to the Indian Ocean, operating around Madagascar, the East African coast, and the Mascarene Islands
- Became one of the most successful pirates of the Golden Age of Piracy

---

## The Great Prize — Nossa Senhora do Cabo (1721)

La Buse's most celebrated capture.

**The ship:** The *Nossa Senhora do Cabo* (also known as *Vierge du Cap*), a Portuguese frigate
**Where captured:** Réunion harbour (then called Île Bourbon)
**Date:** April 1721

**Cargo (historical accounts):**
- Gold bars
- Silver ingots
- Diamonds and precious stones
- Sacred religious objects including a seven-foot gold cross set with diamonds
- Liturgical items, vestments, sacred vessels
- The personal treasures of the Archbishop of Goa
- Cargo intended for the viceroy of Portuguese India

**Estimated value (1721):** Millions of livres. In modern terms, potentially hundreds of millions.

---

## The Cipher

**The execution scene:**
On 7 July 1730, before being hanged at Saint-Denis, Réunion, La Buse allegedly threw a parchment into the watching crowd.

**His words (per legend):** *"Find my treasure, he who may understand."*
(French original: *"A qui de droit"* — "To whom it is due")

**The parchment:** Contains the La Buse cryptogram — a multi-layered encoded text approximately 17 lines long, in a mix of symbols, numbers, and letters. Multiple languages appear to be present.

**Authenticity:** The cipher's authenticity is debated. The parchment's history is incomplete. However, it has been studied seriously by numerous cryptographers and is considered a genuine historical document by most researchers.

**Decipherment status (real world):** Unsolved. Various partial decipherments have been claimed but none verified.

## Treasure Theories and Searches

### Mahé Theory
The most widely held: La Buse's treasure is buried on Mahé, Seychelles — specifically in the Bel Ombre area. Numerous excavations from the 19th century onward. No confirmed treasure found.

### Notable Searches
- **1920s:** Charles de la Roncière (keeper of printed papers, Bibliothèque Nationale, Paris) deciphered a paper believed to contain Le Vasseur's clue, linking it to rock carvings at Bel Ombre beach. Early diggings found two coffins with human remains and a body buried in the sand — interpreted as pirates killing helpers. No gold.
- **Late 1940s onward:** Reginald Cruise-Wilkins formed a syndicate, raised capital, and excavated Bel Ombre for approximately 20 years. Died still searching.
- **1988:** Cruise-Wilkins' sons resumed with $100,000 from an American investor.
*(All of the above confirmed from McAteer's Footnote 27, Rivals in Eden)*

### The Hodoul Confusion — A Competing Seychelles Treasure Legend
McAteer documents a second distinct Seychelles treasure legend that is routinely conflated with La Buse:

**Jean-François Hodoul**, a privateer, captured the English privateer *Henriette* near Sainte Anne island on 28 May 1800. McAteer states this action "gave rise to several of the reports of buried treasure in Seychelles." Hodoul later settled in Seychelles and became one of its wealthiest inhabitants.

**The key distinction:**
- La Buse: 1730, pirate, hidden underground complex, Bel Ombre area
- Hodoul: 1800, privateer, prize money / buried booty, Sainte Anne area

Viktor's AI conflates both legends when processing McAteer's books — both involve "buried treasure in Seychelles" and both are documented by McAteer. Without cultural contextualisation, the probability map the AI produces is unreliable. Juliette separates them immediately. This is one of the novel's clearest demonstrations of what human knowledge provides that data alone cannot.

### Praslin Theory
Alternative: treasure hidden on Praslin, possibly connected to the Vallée de Mai.

## La Buse in Context

- Pirates of this era operated across a network of safe harbours — Madagascar (St. Mary's Island/Île Sainte-Marie) was a major base
- The Seychelles were uninhabited by humans until French colonisation in the 1770s — they were, however, visited by pirates earlier as stopping points
- The timing of La Buse's Seychelles connection is historically plausible but not documented with certainty

---

## Character for the Novel

La Buse himself does not appear in the novel. He exists as:
- A historical shadow
- The author of the cryptogram
- The person who found (not built) the underground complex and used it
- A man more pragmatic than legendary — a professional criminal who found something extraordinary and had the foresight to use it

**The novel's reframing:** La Buse is not the hero of his own story in this novel. He is the man who found something he didn't fully understand and preserved it, imperfectly, for reasons that were probably selfish. The archive's preservation is almost accidental — the result of the complex's natural geology rather than La Buse's intention.

## McAteer's Account of La Buse (Confirmed)

From the scan of *Rivals in Eden* (lines 608–621), McAteer covers La Buse directly:

> *"Of those who terrorised the Indian [Ocean] in the early 18th century one in particular, Olivier le Vasseur, known as La Buse, has had his name directly linked with the Seychelles. The fabulous treasure he supposedly [buried] prompted several attempts to locate it, first in the 1920s and again in the 1950s. Unlikely as the story is, Le Vasseur is [said] to have offered a clue to the whereabouts of the treasure as he was about to be hanged on Bourbon island. He threw to the crowd a piece of paper, shouting 'Find my treasure who can' or words to that effect. The marks on the paper have been deciphered, but no one, so far as is known, has yet succeeded in finding the hidden hoard."*

**Note:** Square brackets indicate words inferred from garbled OCR text. The passage is clear in substance.

McAteer also notes treasure islands reputed in Seychelles lore: **Aride, Frégate, Praslin, Astove, and Mahé**.

This passage is the anchor for the novel's premise — McAteer, the respected historian, documented La Buse sceptically and accurately. The irony is that his other books inadvertently contained the real clues he dismissed in print.
