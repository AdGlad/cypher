## 1. BIOGRAPHY

### Full Name
Brendon Derek Grimshaw

### Birth
- **Date:** 27 July 1925
- **Place:** Dewsbury, Metropolitan Borough of Kirklees, West Yorkshire, England
- **Parents:** Raymond Grimshaw (father — owned a radio and television business; born c.1894) and Kate "Kitty" Grimshaw (mother — fostered Brendon's love of books and theatre)
- He grew up in a modest home with extended family as neighbours

### Death
- **Date:** 3 July 2012 (some sources cite 10 July 2012; 3 July is more consistently cited)
- **Place:** Victoria, Seychelles (on or near the island)
- **Age at death:** 86 (some sources say 87 — the discrepancy arises because he died three weeks before his 87th birthday)
- **Cause:** Described only as "a short illness" in available sources. No specific medical cause documented publicly.
- **Burial:** On Moyenne Island, beside his father Raymond (died 1986) and the two "unknown pirate" graves
- **Tombstone inscription:** "Brendon D. Grimshaw 1925–2012. Moyenne taught him to open his eyes to the beauty around him and say thank you to God."

### Education
- Left school at approximately 15 (one source says 13 he demonstrated writing talent; his mother arranged a meeting with the Batley News editor during WWII, suggesting he began journalism as a teenager)
- No university education documented

## 2. JOURNALISM CAREER

### Batley News (Batley, West Yorkshire)
- First employer, a local West Yorkshire newspaper
- Began in the front office: wrapping papers, operating switchboard, adding figures
- Progressed to printing operations (typesetting, press machines), then reporter covering two districts
- Became film critic, writing two weekly columns
- By age 23 was described as Britain's youngest chief reporter

### The Star, Sheffield
- Moved to Sheffield at approximately age 25 (c. 1950)
- The Majorcan journalist Donald Trelford (later editor of *The Observer*) noted he and Grimshaw "trained on Sheffield newspapers" — this confirms the Sheffield phase

### East African Standard, Nairobi, Kenya
- Joined as Senior Sub-editor
- Had to carry a shoulder-holster gun due to the Mau Mau uprising
- Over eight years rose to Featured Editor, drama critic, and book critic
- One source says he also worked for Reuters during his African period

### Tanganyika Standard and Sunday News, Dar es Salaam, Tanzania
- Became editor and director of both newspapers
- Spent approximately eleven years in Dar es Salaam
- Trained his 300-person African and Asian staff to assume full editorial responsibilities
- This act earned the thanks and friendship of Julius Nyerere, who subsequently invited Grimshaw to serve as **Tanzania's first public relations consultant** (1970–1973), travelling overseas with official delegations and supervising trade stands at international fairs

### Departure from Africa
- The assassination of Eduardo Mondlane (Mozambican independence activist, killed by a book bomb, 3 February 1969) was reportedly a catalyst for Grimshaw's decision to leave Africa permanently
- He had already purchased Moyenne Island in 1962 but continued working in Tanzania until 1973
- He finally moved permanently to Moyenne in 1973

## 3. DISCOVERY AND PURCHASE OF MOYENNE ISLAND

### First Visit (1962)
- Grimshaw was 37 years old
- He went on holiday to the Seychelles, the purpose described in his own words as looking for "a change of life" as much as a change of scenery — he was aware that East African independence meant jobs like his would soon pass to locals
- On the second-to-last day of his holiday, a young local man in Victoria (Mahé) approached him and mentioned an island was for sale
- Grimshaw was taken to see Moyenne Island
- His first impression, in his own words (from the documentary): "It was totally different. It was a special feeling. This is the place I'd been looking for."

### The Purchase
- **Seller:** Philippe Georges (some sources spell as Phillip Georges)
  - Georges had inherited the island from his godfather
  - He and his wife Vera Georges had lived on the island in its early years, then moved to Mahé (a property called "Fairview")
  - A beach and viewpoint on the island were named after Vera
  - Georges was initially reluctant to sell but became convinced that Grimshaw would care for the island
- **Price:** £8,000 (approximately $10,000 at the time; equivalent to roughly £149,000–£216,000 in 2025 values — sources vary slightly)
- **When signed:** "Four minutes to midnight on the last day of his stay" — Grimshaw signed an agreement with Philippe Georges

### Condition of the Island at Purchase
- Uninhabited since 1915 (approximately 47 years of abandonment)
- Severely overgrown — vegetation so dense that "falling coconuts never reached the ground" due to the thick canopy
- Rat-infested
- Almost no birds
- According to a BBC interview Grimshaw gave in 2012, there were "just four trees" on the island when he first visited (contrast: other sources say it was choked with vegetation, suggesting those "four trees" were the only mature trees visible among dense scrub and weeds)
- No fresh water infrastructure, no electricity, no buildings

## 4. MOYENNE ISLAND — GEOGRAPHY AND EARLY HISTORY

### Geography
- **Name:** "Moyenne" is French for "medium" or "middle" — dates to the colonial period
- **Size:** 9.9 hectares (24 acres / 0.038 square miles)
- **Dimensions:** Approximately 480m × 300m (some sources cite 400m × 300m)
- **Maximum elevation:** 61 metres
- **Location:** Off the north-east coast of Mahé, Seychelles
- **Distance from Mahé coast:** 4.5 km
- **Distance from Victoria (capital):** Approximately 8 km (sources variously say 3.7 miles east, or "less than 3 miles")
- **Marine park context:** Located within the Sainte Anne Marine National Park (established March 1973, the first marine park of its kind in the Indian Ocean), though Moyenne itself later received its own separate national park designation
- **Coordinates:** 4°37′11″S 55°30′31″E
- **Climate:** Tropical maritime; temperatures 24–30°C year-round; approximately 2,200 mm annual rainfall
- **Terrain:** Rocky shores, granite boulders, sandy beaches, coral reefs; dense forest interior; the island is granitic (not coral)

### Pre-Grimshaw Ownership History
The island changed hands several times. Documented owners in sequence:

1. **Melidor Louange and Julie Chiffon (1850–1892):** Children of freed enslaved people; gifted the island on their marriage in 1850; lived there with their three children for 42 years
2. **Alfred d'Emmerez de Charmoy (1892–c.1899):** An unconventional Englishman from Berkshire; sold or transferred to the next owner
3. **Emma Wardlow-Best (c.1899–c.1913):** An eccentric Englishwoman who used the island as a sanctuary for stray dogs rescued from Mahé; died approximately 1913 (sources vary on her exact name — one source calls her "Mary Best," another "Emma Best," the most detailed source gives "Emma Wardlow-Best"). Grimshaw mentions stories of her ghost haunting the island in his memoir
4. **Unoccupied (1913/1915–1946)**
5. **Philippe Georges (1946–1962):** Inherited from his godfather; later sold to Grimshaw

### Pirate History
- Legends associate the island with 18th-century pirates, specifically Olivier Levasseur ("La Buse" / "La Buze"), who was hanged on Réunion in July 1730
- La Buse is said to have buried treasure on several Seychelles islands after his 1721 capture of the Portuguese ship *Nossa Senhora do Cabo* — a haul that reportedly included the Fiery Cross of Goa, studded with diamonds, rubies, and emeralds, reportedly so large that three men were required to lift it
- The alleged treasure value on Moyenne is cited as approximately £30,000,000
- Legends persist but no verified discoveries have been made
- Under British rule (1811–1976) the island saw limited economic use including guano extraction and copra production

## 5. RENÉ ANTOINE LAFORTUNE

### Who Was He?
- A Seychellois man, son of a local fisherman
- He was approximately **19 years old** when he joined Grimshaw's project (various sources agree on this age)
- One source describes him as "son of the previous guardian" of the island, which if accurate might mean his father had some caretaking role for the Georges family — this detail is not definitively confirmed elsewhere

### When Did He Join?
- Grimshaw began working on the island in 1962 but lived full-time in Tanzania until 1973
- Lafortune appears to have joined in the early years of the project, likely the late 1960s or early 1970s — exact date not documented in available sources

### How Long Did He Stay?
- He remained with Grimshaw for approximately 34–40 years (sources vary)
- He died in 2007, five years before Grimshaw

### Nature of Their Partnership
- Grimshaw described Lafortune as "much more than a mere Man Friday" — the dedication in *Another Grain of Sand*
- They were partners in every practical aspect of island life: path-cutting, tree planting, tortoise care, path maintenance, and hosting visitors
- Lafortune appears never to have been an employee in a conventional sense; he was a permanent co-inhabitant and collaborator
- The subtitle of Grimshaw's second book, *Another Grain of Sand*, is "The Story of Two Men and an Island" — explicitly a tribute to Lafortune

### Death
- **Year:** 2007
- **Cause:** Cerebral malaria
- **Location of death:** Singapore (he was visiting when he became ill)
- He was approximately 5 years younger than Grimshaw's project — if he joined at 19 and died in 2007 having spent roughly 35–40 years on the island, he may have been born in the late 1940s or early 1950s (exact birth date listed as "unknown" on Find a Grave)
- After Lafortune's death, Grimshaw — then aged 81 — lived alone on the island for his remaining five years

### Was He Buried on the Island?
- Sources do not confirm this. The island's known graves are: two "unknown pirates," Raymond Grimshaw (Brendon's father, died 1986), and Brendon Grimshaw himself (died 2012). Lafortune's burial location is not documented in available sources.

## 6. THE CONSERVATION WORK

### Trees
- **Total planted:** Over 16,000 trees (all sources agree on this number)
- **Species documented:**
  - 700 mahogany trees (grew to 60–70 feet tall)
  - Palm trees (including 2,000+ coconut palms; 40 endemic palm species mentioned; all six endemic Seychelles palm species represented)
  - 13 coco de mer palms
  - Mango trees
  - Pawpaw (papaya) trees
  - Takamaka (a native Seychelles tree)
  - Bwa dou
  - Mahogany
- The island now contains two-thirds of all endemic plant species of the Seychelles
- The tree canopy became so dense that "falling coconuts don't hit the ground"

### Methods
- Grimshaw and Lafortune began by cutting pathways through dense scrub using crowbars and heavy hammers
- Hacked steps out of rocks
- The first path alone took approximately two years to complete
- Total paths eventually constructed: approximately 4.8 km (some sources say 3 miles / "2.9 miles")
- Fresh water was a constant challenge: collected rainwater; rows to Mahé for fresh water in early years ("My hands were covered in blisters" — Grimshaw); eventually an underground water reservoir and generator were installed; hand-pumped water systems up the hillside

### Tortoises
- Species: **Aldabra giant tortoise** (*Aldabrachelys gigantea*), native to the Seychelles but near-extinction from habitat destruction and over-hunting
- Grimshaw reintroduced them and allowed them to breed
- **Numbers cited:** 50 / 100 / 109 / 120 — sources vary; the 2009 documentary states 109 at time of filming; most general sources say "120 giant tortoises" by the time of Grimshaw's death; current sources say "50–100 still live there today"
- Baby tortoises were raised in Grimshaw's bedroom: "The babies are kept in my bedroom. If you let them go, you'll never see them again." (*Financial Times*, 2008)
- The island now has two tortoise hatcheries

### Birds
- At purchase: almost no birds (rats had destroyed nesting habitats)
- Grimshaw initially brought 10 birds from a neighbouring village
- Sources variously state the island attracted:
  - "approximately 2,000 birds" (a population count)
  - "over 200 bird species" (species count)
  - "2,000 bird species" — this is clearly an error in some sources (the Seychelles has nowhere near 2,000 species); the accurate figure is approximately 200 species recorded on the island
- Species mentioned: pigeon hollandais (Dutch pigeon), orange-red fody, bulbuls, sunbirds, Seychelles fruit bats (endemic mammals, not birds, but part of the wildlife recovery)

### Conservation Status / Recognition
- **1973:** Sainte Anne Marine National Park established around the island group — Moyenne was within its boundaries but not separately protected
- **1998:** Grimshaw founded the **Moyenne Island Foundation Society** as a nonprofit, to secure the island's future
- **2008:** One source places formal inclusion in Sainte Anne Marine National Park in 2008; most sources cite 2009 for the separate national park designation
- **2009:** Grimshaw negotiated with the Seychelles Ministry of Environment to create a permanent trust; Moyenne Island was designated **Moyenne Island National Park** — described as "the world's smallest national park" (9 hectares / 22 acres)
- **No UNESCO designation** documented in available sources
- The island is also claimed to have "more species per square foot than any other national park in existence" — this claim appears frequently but without a cited measurement source

## 7. THE TREASURE HUNT

### Did Grimshaw Search for Treasure?
Yes — explicitly confirmed in multiple sources. He "enjoyed entertaining the idea of pirates on his island" and took it seriously enough to excavate.

### What He Found
- While clearing the north-western corner of the island, Grimshaw discovered **two graves** with tombstones inscribed: **"Unhappily Unknown"**
- Local legend held these were pirate deckhands killed by their captains, whose spirits were meant to protect buried treasure and haunt the island
- The island cemetery dates to at least 1892
- Grimshaw excavated **two sites** on the island (now marked with skull-and-crossbones symbols on the island's current maps)
- He dug near the graves hoping to find treasure. He abandoned the effort after striking what appeared to be coral concrete: large coconuts reportedly fell with a thud when his spade touched it, which unnerved him
- One source states he dug approximately twenty feet deep at one location
- "Pirate's Cove" was already the name of one beach when Grimshaw arrived
- The island also contains Arab sailors' graves

### What He Found (Physical Discoveries)
- Two "Unhappily Unknown" tombstones (confirmed)
- Possible coral concrete beneath one excavation site (confirmed — interpretation of what it means is uncertain)
- The island also contains: a small open-air chapel listing historical owners; the pirates' grave area

### His Stated View on the Treasure
- Grimshaw neither confirmed nor denied finding anything significant. Sources indicate he believed the legend was plausible but treated it as one of the island's romantic attributes rather than its primary purpose
- He did not pursue extensive excavation once conservation work became his dominant concern

### La Buse Connection
- The association between La Buse and Moyenne specifically (as opposed to other Seychelles islands like Bel Ombre on Mahé, or Frégate) is part of the general pirate lore of the archipelago
- No source documents Grimshaw making a specific intellectual or practical connection between the La Buse cipher and Moyenne Island beyond the general legend

## 9. OFFERS AND REFUSALS

### Volume of Offers
- Offers began coming in the 1980s, described by some sources as "almost daily" from the 1980s onwards as the Seychelles became a high-profile tourist destination
- Grimshaw refused them all

### The Highest Offer
- Multiple sources describe a $50 million offer from "a Saudi prince"
- One source describes it as coming from "an American hotel group reportedly backed by Saudi businessmen" (slightly different framing — the "Saudi prince" formulation may be a simplification)
- Another source cites the offer as £24 million
- The Seychelles government estimated the island's value at €34 million (c. 2009, at time of documentary)
- The actual highest offer in Grimshaw's own stated words is not definitively established; the "$50 million from a Saudi prince" is the most widely repeated version

### Grimshaw's Response
- His most frequently quoted response to offers: "The island is not for sale."
- He asked every prospective developer the same questions: "What will happen to the tortoises?" / "Where will the birds nest?" / "What about the fauna?"
- In his own words: "I didn't want the island to become a holiday destination for millionaires, but a national park for everyone to enjoy."
- In his own words: "I didn't spend years transforming the island for money. I did it because I was making the island a wonderful place to live."

## 10. LEGACY AND NATIONAL PARK STATUS

### Moyenne Island Foundation Society
- Founded by Grimshaw in 1998
- A nonprofit organisation, established to ensure the island's permanent protection
- After Grimshaw's death, managed by his long-time friend **Suketu Patel** (Patel had known Grimshaw since 1976)
- Quote from Suketu Patel: "His vision was to leave an unspoiled island for future generations of Seychellois and the world."

### National Park Designation
- Designated **Moyenne Island National Park** in 2009
- Agreement signed between Grimshaw personally and the Seychelles Ministry of Environment
- Classified as the world's smallest national park (9 hectares)
- The island was not handed to the Seychelles government outright — it is managed by the private Moyenne Island Foundation

### Grimshaw's Will
- "Moyenne Island is to be maintained as a venue for prayer, peace, tranquillity, relaxation and knowledge for Seychellois and visitors from overseas of all nationalities, colours and creeds."

### Current State (as of mid-2020s)
- Aldabra giant tortoises still roam freely; tortoise population 50–100
- Forest of 16,000 trees intact
- Approximately 3 miles / 4.8 km of nature trails
- A small museum about the island's history
- The "Jolly Roger" restaurant (outdoor / tiki-bar style, serves Seychellois buffet)
- A small open-air chapel listing historical owners
- The pirate graves ("Unhappily Unknown")
- Grimshaw's former residence
- **Visitor policy:** Maximum 50 visitors at any given time; day-trip access only (no overnight stays); no dock (tender boats required); no hotel; no shops beyond the restaurant
- **Entry fee:** Approximately £12–€20 per adult (included in most tour packages)
- **Access:** Boat from Mahé (20–30 minutes); access via organised tours only, no public ferry

## 12. PERSONALITY AND PHILOSOPHY

### Character Descriptions
- Described as "enthusiastic" and "different" as a journalist
- Multiple accounts emphasise his complete indifference to money: "Money didn't interest him, because he knew from the beginning that he was on a life mission"
- He welcomed visitors to the island but on strict terms — as guests, not tourists in a commercial resort sense
- He kept dogs on the island, including one named Yellow that outlived him

### On His Relationship with His Father
His own words: "We had a wonderful time together and became the best of friends." (Referring to the years after 1981 when his 88-year-old father Raymond joined him on the island; Raymond died in 1986 and was buried on Moyenne at age 93 — the only person buried there who arrived voluntarily rather than as an unknown.)

### Stated Philosophy
- On why he undertook the project: "Out of a desire for peace and a love for nature. Out of a wish to change something and leave future generations with encouragement and faith that something magical can always be created from nothing."
- His teaching, summarised by those who knew him: "money doesn't matter — but what we create from the heart with our own two hands, without asking anything in return, does"
- He described the island as a place of "prayer, peace, tranquillity" (language used in his will)
- In interviews, Grimshaw consistently used questions about the tortoises and birds as his standard rejoinder to development offers — suggesting his conservation concern was entirely genuine rather than rhetorical

### On Solitude
- He lived effectively alone after Lafortune's death in 2007 (at age 81) until his own death in 2012
- He allowed visitors during the day but maintained the island as a private domain overnight
- No accounts describe him as reclusive or hostile to company; the general picture is of a sociable man who had simply found his purpose

## 13. CONNECTION TO WILLIAM McATEER

### Documented Connection
McAteer explicitly names Grimshaw and Moyenne Island in *Hard Times in Paradise* (Book 2 of his Seychelles history series). This is the cross-reference noted in the project's memory file, sourced from the user's own reading of that text.

### From Available Public Sources
No public sources document any personal relationship, correspondence, or mutual reference between Brendon Grimshaw and William McAteer beyond McAteer's mention of Grimshaw in the histories. Grimshaw's books are not known to reference McAteer.

Both men were active in the Seychelles during overlapping decades (McAteer was researching and writing through the 1990s–2000s; Grimshaw was living on Moyenne from 1973 to 2012), so they were contemporaries in the archipelago and it is plausible they knew of each other, but no documented interaction has been found.
