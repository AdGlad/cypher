## THE McATEER CONNECTION — Exact Text

Page 40 (approximately), Grimshaw writes about Tanzania colleagues:

> *By coincidence, George [Rockey] and another newspaper colleague, Bill McAteer, both married Seychellois girls. George's son, Tim, attended Seychelles College and visited me on Moyenne before settling in London where for some time he ran a champagne bar in St James.*

> *Bill McAteer, recently editor of the Gulf Times in Qatar, has just published a book on French and English relations in the early days of Seychelles history called* Rivals in Eden. *With property on Mahe, I expect he and his family will become neighbours fairly soon.*

> *Two other authors have established homes recently on neighbouring Cerf Island: Wilbur Smith, whose novel* Wild Justice *opens in Seychelles, and Bill Travis whose books* Beyond the Reef *and* Shark for Sale *tell of life in the islands thirty years ago.*

**Key facts extracted:**
1. McAteer and Grimshaw were **newspaper colleagues in East Africa** — both working in the region at the same time
2. McAteer **married a Seychellois girl** — consistent with Juliette Beaumont in the novel
3. McAteer had been **editor of the Gulf Times in Qatar** before the Seychelles — new biographical detail not previously known
4. At the time of writing, McAteer had **property on Mahé** — not yet on Cerf Island
5. Grimshaw expected McAteer and family to become **neighbours "fairly soon"** — suggesting the Cerf Island move came after this was written (~1995–1996)

**Novel adjustment:** The novel-bible has Juliette and McAteer on Cerf Island throughout. This is consistent — Grimshaw's book was written 1995, published 1996; at that point McAteer had property on Mahé; the move to Cerf Island presumably came after. The novel is set 2026, McAteer died ~2021 — thirty years later. The Cerf Island positioning is correct for the novel's present-day timeline.

## THE PIRATE GRAVES AND CEMETERY

From chapter approximately page 54–55:

- Two pirate graves found two-thirds of the way down to Pirate Cove, on a tiny plateau
- "Referred to as pirate graves, they had been mentioned in the deeds when Julie and Melidor sold the island in 1892 and had been just two piles of rocks until Miss Best had them dressed with coral cement and the crosses put in place in the early 1900s"
- **Coral concrete** discovery: "Clearing the cemetery, I had discovered a square of coral concrete only a **few feet** from the graves and so one evening decided to take a spade and find out if anything was buried underneath"
- Grimshaw abandoned digging when, "at the precise moment my spade touched the coral concrete, two large coconuts fell with a thud and rolled close to where I was standing"
- **Correction to previous research**: The coral concrete was a "few feet" from the graves, not at 20 feet depth. The 20-foot depth figure refers to the Big Dig (a completely separate site)

**The dream story:**
- A six-year-old girl on Mahé had a dream about Moyenne — described two graves and a mango tree at the foot of the graves
- Grimshaw found the mango tree where she said it would be
- Someone later dug at the foot of the graves without permission; found only charcoal

**Seychellois folk belief (from text):** A Seychellois visitor told Grimshaw that "if a person dreams about a treasure then they can dig and find it but if anyone else digs then they will find specifically charcoal and nothing [else]"
- The dreamer later confirmed the treasure was not at the foot of the graves but "in the cemetery quite nearby" — Grimshaw declined to dig there once the area was consecrated

## THE BIG DIG — Most Important Finding

Found 2 February 1975, above Rocky Point, high on the island's north-western quadrant.

**Discovery:** While walking with his dog Fiapi, Grimshaw noticed "two small rods set at a right angle, which is not at all usual." Clearing dead leaves, he found what resembled the marking for a double grave. No previous record or mention by any owner.

**Excavation timeline:**
- First weeks after discovery: cleared to ~4 feet; found pick marks on the walls — "man had been there before us"
- Eventually, inside the dig: "five-inch-thick walls making two small rooms inside the dig and the whole area was enclosed by man-made walls, on one side the labs having been stim into place in what I termed Egyptian fashion"
- At ~13 feet: "a small cave in which the cores of two rocks had been placed side by side. The cores must have been much larger rocks broken down to their present cannon ball size and shape"
- By 1 December 1986: **8 feet wide, 15 feet long, 14 feet deep** — full photographic record taken

**The booby trap (critical detail):**
- At depth of 6–7 feet, two giant boulders above the working were becoming unstable
- Grimshaw discovered "they had been jammed in position by small rocks and if left alone could prove to be excellent booby traps, squashing Rene and myself in the hole"
- The boulders were deliberately set — he carefully removed the small jamming rocks, the larger boulder fell clear on 2 June 1986, its mate a week later
- "Clearing the areas where the boulders had been standing, I found they were flat and there was no doubt that they had been put in place by man and that fifteen to twenty men would have been needed. This, in turn, required a ship."
- "But, what sort of ship? Perhaps, just perhaps, a pirate ship. Certainly, no previous owner of Moyenne had any reason for such an elaborate 'dig' or the necessary labour it must have involved."

**Resumed excavation (1992–1993):**
- Grimshaw had hernia operation in Singapore in April 1992; work paused
- Early 1993: resumed; "boulders rolled into the site and presumably on top of whatever might [be] buried below"
- Halted 1 April 1993: **8 feet deep, 16 feet long, 16 feet wide** (different measurement axis than 1986 recording)
- Grimshaw notes this "hope will be the final dig" — suggests he eventually stopped

**Conclusion from the text:**
- This is a deliberately constructed double chamber, with two small rooms inside, laid in "Egyptian fashion"
- Booby-trap boulders requiring 15–20 men to place
- No treasure found; no definitive conclusion about what it was for
- Still marked on the visitor map as "Big Dig"

**Novel significance:**
- The Big Dig's construction — Egyptian-fashion stonework, deliberate booby traps, cannon-ball-sized core stones — is a direct parallel to the underground complex in the novel
- The Moyenne Big Dig can be Adam's first parallel: if Grimshaw found a constructed chamber here, requiring a ship and 15-20 men, then Levasseur had the capacity and the precedent for the Bel Ombre construction
- Viktor's AURORA will have the Big Dig documentation in its database; it will probably misread it as the treasure location

## ISLAND NAMES AND FEATURES (for scene-setting)

**Flora with Creole/French names given in the text:**
- Bois noir (blackwood / woman's tongue — seedpods rattle)
- Bois malgache (green plastic-like shrub used to ward off evil spirits — planted by Madame in front of the cottages)
- Bois table (the table or looking-glass tree — young leaves flash in sunlight like a mirror)
- Bois cuillere (spoon tree — spoon-shaped seedpods)
- Bois nam / ghost tree (stretched from cemetery to main landing)
- Bigaradier (cross between orange and lime)
- Dragon tree (dragons blood tree — oozes red sap, flowers cascading laburnum-like, usually 27 November, "the island dragons")
- Takamaka (endemic Seychelles tree)

**Wildlife names from text:**
- Nams: Seychellois word for ghosts
- Bon homme de forêt: forest spirit who frightens trespassers
- Dodociers: Seychelles "living dead" / zombies; "do not appreciate the arrival of electricity, which effectively makes them vanish"
- Gris gris: black magic practice; local practitioners
- Skinks: "smooth, brown-speckled lizard"

**Grimshaw's dog:** Don't Care (female) — first dog; shivered with back hairs on end when the ghost knocked

## GHOST STORIES — For Seychellois Cultural Texture

The island's ghost tradition is rich and specific:

- A female ghost who knocks on windows (female ghosts knock windows; male ghosts knock doors)
- The trudge-trudgers: shuffling sounds on the path from cemetery to main landing at full moon — "presumably made by people in chains" (the path of the enslaved)
- Miss Best's ghost: mentioned by visitors but Grimshaw is agnostic about this
- The ghost does not like policemen (Superintendent Stone and wife both heard the knocks)
- Madame's gris gris powder placed on steps to attract visitors — obtained from a practitioner called "Daisy" in the hills of Mahé

**Novel use:** Aimee would know what a nam is. Adam would not. The word appears without explanation in Creole-inflected speech and Adam has to ask. Juliette would describe the trudge-trudgers as factual island history, not superstition.

## KEY QUOTES FOR THE NOVEL

**"The island is the treasure"** — from a visitor's book entry, not Grimshaw himself. Full context: *"those with a little thought behind them such as: 'The island is the treasure, at last a man who lives and enjoys it'"*
- This is a visitor comment; Grimshaw quotes it approvingly. Usable as a visitor's book quote if the novel visits Moyenne.

**On treasure:** *"Why not? Any of our granite islands with a good anchorage nearby must qualify as a possible resting place for pirates and their ill-gotten gains."*

**On the Big Dig's conclusion:** *"fifteen to twenty men would have been needed. This, in turn, required a ship. But, what sort of ship? Perhaps, just perhaps, a pirate ship."*

**Grimshaw's response to treasure questions:** *"When visitors ask if I believe there is a treasure on Moyenne my reply is: Why not?"*

**On La Buze / Cruise-Wilkins:** *"on Mahé, John Wilkins is still seeking the £120 million hoard of La Buze — Olivier Vasseur — which eluded his father, who solved the pirates cryptogram treasure map thirty-odd years before."*
- "John Wilkins" is John Cruise-Wilkins (OCR error on surname, or Grimshaw's usage)
- Confirms Grimshaw was aware of and interested in the Cruise-Wilkins operation
- Notes the father "solved" the cryptogram — Grimshaw treats this as fact; the cipher's authenticity is secondary to the general cultural belief
- Uses "La Buze" spelling — consistent with McAteer

**On Hodoul:** *"Hodoul the privateer made his base on neighbouring St Anne and sailed from his safe anchorage a mere half-mile from our Jolly Roger to raid shipping and return there with his loot."*
- This is the Hodoul/La Buze conflation that SYBIL makes in the novel — Grimshaw also names both in the same chapter, treating them as parallel figures who used the same islands

## AUTHOR'S NOTE (Page 5)

> *Personally, I don't know of anyone else who owns and lives on a tropical island, often totally alone. In fact, I have never met anyone who has done so either. But most visitors to my island home consider it their ultimate dream and usually ask if I have written a book about it all. So, what better way to mark my 30th anniversary with Moyenne than to share my way of life, answering the many questions visitors ask and providing a permanent record of what for me is still a unique experience?*
>
> *Brendon Grimshaw*
> *16 May 1995, St Brendan's Day*
> *Moyenne Island, Seychelles.*

**Note:** Written on 16 May 1995, St Brendan's Day (Brendon = a form of Brendan). The 30th anniversary means his connection to Moyenne began in 1965 (first visit 1962; but regular work on the island from approximately 1965). Published January 1996.
