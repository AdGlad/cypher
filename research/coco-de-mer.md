# Research — Coco de Mer

---

## Biological Overview

**Scientific name:** *Lodoicea maldivica*
**Family:** Arecaceae (Palm family)
**Endemic to:** The Seychelles — naturally occurring only on Praslin and the nearby island of Curieuse
**Conservation status:** Vulnerable

---

## Physical Characteristics

### The Tree
- One of the largest and slowest-growing palms in the world
- Can reach 25–34 metres in height
- Lifespan: estimated 200–400 years
- Distinctive fan-shaped fronds — up to 10 metres long
- Separate male and female trees (dioecious)

### The Seed — The World's Largest
- The Coco de Mer produces the largest seed of any plant on Earth
- Weight: 15–25 kg
- The seed takes six to seven years to fully develop
- Shape: a deeply lobed, bilateral structure — famously resembling the female buttocks and pelvis
- The internal cavity contains a clear jelly in the early stages — the "flesh" develops over years

### The Nut's Structure
- Two distinct halves joined at a central seam
- Each half is a mirror image of the other
- The hidden internal cavity (before full maturity) is where the seed's nutrition is concentrated
- The bilateral symmetry is the defining visual characteristic

## Symbolism and Cultural History

### The Legend of Eden
General Charles Gordon (British general) visited Praslin in 1881 and became convinced that Vallée de Mai was the original Garden of Eden, and the Coco de Mer was the "forbidden fruit" of Genesis. This claim was fanciful but widely circulated.

### Arab and Indian Mythology
Long before the islands were formally known to Europeans, Coco de Mer seeds — shed into the ocean and carried by currents — were found across the Indian Ocean and washed up on distant shores. These mystery seeds were called:
- *Coco de Mer* (French — coconut of the sea)
- *Lodoicea callipyge* (older scientific name — "beautiful-buttocked")
- The seeds were believed to come from a legendary underwater island; they were highly valued as magical objects

Arab traders attributed them to a mythical underwater palm tree. They were used medicinally and held as precious objects by sultans and emperors.

### Sexual Symbolism
The seed's shape led to extensive mythologising about fertility, sexuality, and the divine feminine. The Seychellois relationship to the Coco de Mer is partly earthy, partly proud — the palm is a national symbol and also an enduring source of wry humour.

## The Novel's Use of the Coco de Mer

### As Structural Metaphor
The Coco de Mer's bilateral structure — two halves, each a mirror of the other, with a hidden interior — directly mirrors the underground complex's two-chamber structure:
- The treasure chamber: visible, material, the "first half"
- The scientific archive: concealed, intellectual, the "second half"
- The internal cavity: the hidden knowledge that can only be accessed from inside

### On the Second Door
The Coco de Mer symbol is carved above the second door in the underground complex. This is both:
- A directional marker (the door leads to the "second half")
- A key: understanding what the symbol means is part of understanding that a second chamber exists

### Aimee's Drawing
During the visit to Vallée de Mai, Aimee handles a Coco de Mer seed and begins drawing it — the bilateral symmetry, the seam, the cavity. She is drawing the structure of the complex without knowing it. When she later encounters the door symbol, she recognises it instantly.

### "The Second Eden"
Père Henri's phrase — "the second Eden" — takes on multiple meanings by the end of the novel:
1. The underground complex as a preserved world beneath the visible world
2. The scientific archive as a record of an ecosystem that no longer exists (the pre-colonial Seychelles and Mascarene region)
3. The Coco de Mer as the symbolic pivot between the two halves of the discovery

## Ecological Significance

- The Coco de Mer forest of Vallée de Mai is genuinely ancient — the trees live for centuries
- The forest provides habitat for the endemic Black Parrot (Coracopsis barklyi)
- Seed poaching is a significant conservation problem — a single mature seed sells for thousands of dollars on the black market
- The Seychelles Island Foundation manages Vallée de Mai under UNESCO World Heritage designation

**For the novel:** The Coco de Mer's vulnerability to poaching and over-exploitation parallels the archive's vulnerability — both are unique, irreplaceable, and coveted by people who value them for the wrong reasons.

## Key Facts for Writing Scenes in Vallée de Mai

- The forest is dense and the light is filtered — a deep, cool green quality even in bright sun
- The fronds are enormous; they create a canopy that reduces ambient sound
- The sound of the fronds moving is distinctive — heavy, deliberate
- The Black Parrot is heard more often than seen
- The paths are maintained but the forest edges are genuinely wild
- The scale of the palms is difficult to convey without comparison — the fronds dwarf a standing adult
