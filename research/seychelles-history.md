# Research — Seychelles History

---

## Overview

The Seychelles is an archipelago of 115 islands in the Indian Ocean, northeast of Madagascar. Its history is one of colonialism, piracy, slavery, political upheaval, and remarkable natural heritage.

---

## Pre-Colonial Period

- The islands are believed to have been uninhabited before European contact
- Arab traders knew of the islands; they appear on Arab maps from the early medieval period
- The Coco de Mer seeds were known to Arab and Indian traders as ocean drift items long before the islands were formally identified
- Portuguese explorers encountered the islands in the early 16th century
- No permanent settlement before the 18th century

---

## French Colonial Period (1742–1810)

- **1742:** French expedition under Lazare Picault formally surveys the islands
- **1756:** France claims the islands — named "Îles de Séchelles" after Jean Moreau de Séchelles, French minister of finance
- **1770:** First permanent French settlement on Mahé — a small agricultural colony with enslaved workers
- The colony grew slowly; the islands were valued for timber, spices, and strategic position
- During this period: pirates used the islands as stopping points; the Seychelles were not policed effectively
- The French Revolution and Napoleonic Wars disrupted colonial governance

---

## British Colonial Period (1810–1976)

- **1810:** British forces took control of the Seychelles from France (during the Napoleonic Wars)
- **1814:** Officially ceded to Britain under the Treaty of Paris
- The British maintained French laws, French-speaking population, and the Creole culture — creating a distinctive hybrid colonial experience
- **1835:** Abolition of slavery in the British Empire — significant transformation of Seychellois society; formerly enslaved people formed the core of the Creole population
- Late 19th–early 20th century: the Seychelles as a place of political exile (various figures exiled here by the British, including the Archbishop of Uganda and later, the Cypriot leader Makarios)

---

## Independence and Political Upheaval

- **1964:** First political parties formed
- **1976:** Full independence from Britain. James Mancham became first president; France-Albert René became prime minister
- **1977:** René staged a coup while Mancham was abroad. One-party socialist state under René's Seychelles People's Progressive Front (SPPF)
- The coup is important to the novel: Edmond's family was affected. The political upheaval caused many families to lose property, leave, or recalibrate their lives around the new reality.
- **1981:** Failed mercenary coup attempt — the "Seychelles affair" — backed by South African interests. A significant moment of Cold War tension in the Indian Ocean.
- **1993:** Return to multi-party democracy; René's party won the first multi-party elections
- **2004:** René stepped down; James Michel became president
- Recent years: Wavel Ramkalawan elected president in 2020 — the first opposition president in Seychelles history

---

## Economic History

- Tourism became the primary economic driver from the 1970s onward
- Fishing (tuna especially) significant industry
- The Seychelles has developed one of the highest per capita incomes in Africa through tourism and financial services
- However: economic inequality, the legacy of the one-party period, and development pressures on natural environments create ongoing tensions

---

## The Seychelles and the Cold War

- The Seychelles' strategic position in the Indian Ocean made it a Cold War flashpoint
- The US operated a tracking station on Mahé; the Soviet Union sought influence
- This context is relevant to the novel: Edmond's family navigated not just local politics but international power games

---

## The 1977 Coup — Relevance to the Novel

Edmond (Brigitte's uncle) is from a family that was directly affected by the coup. This means:
- He has complicated feelings about institutional authority
- He has connections across the political spectrum — people who stayed, people who left, people who adapted
- His network includes those who lost property and those who acquired it
- He understands that power in the Seychelles operates through relationships, not bureaucracy

---

## Cultural Heritage

- **Language:** Seychellois Creole (Kreol Seselwa), French, English — all official languages
- Creole is the mother tongue of most Seychellois; French and English used in formal contexts
- The culture is a blend of African, Asian, European, and Malagasy influences — genuinely creolised in the anthropological sense
- Traditional music: *sega* and *moutia* — both African-rooted, historically the music of enslaved people
- Folk traditions, oral history, and Catholic religious practice are deeply interwoven
