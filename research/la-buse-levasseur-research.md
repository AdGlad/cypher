# Research Report: Olivier Levasseur "La Buse"
# Compiled for THE EDEN CIPHER — June 2026

This report compiles exhaustive research on Olivier Levasseur, the French pirate known as "La Buse." All sections distinguish between well-documented historical fact, disputed claims, and probable legend.

---

## SECTION 1: BIOGRAPHY

### Name and Identity

Full name: **Olivier Levasseur** (also rendered in period documents as Le Vasseur, Levasseur, and Oliver de la Bouche).

**Nicknames:**
- **La Buse** — French for "The Buzzard." Most sources agree this referred to the speed and ferocity with which he swooped on prey. Some sources add that his beak-like nose contributed to the name. This is his primary historical nickname and the most commonly used in academic and popular sources.
- **La Bouche** — French for "The Mouth." Some sources say this referred to his ability to verbally attack opponents; others treat it as an alternative transliteration of La Buse rather than a separate meaning. The two names appear interchangeably in some early sources.
- Portuguese sources refer to him as **O Falcão** (The Falcon).
- Multiple spelling variants appear in historical records: La Bouse, Leboose, La Buze, La Bouze.

**On "La Buze":** This spelling variant (used by McAteer) is attested historically and appears in French-language sources alongside La Buse. It is not an error; it reflects the same name in an older or regional orthographic convention.

### Birth

**Birthplace:** Calais, France. This is consistent across almost all sources. One French source (France Télévisions' La1ere) cites a surviving birth record giving a date of **5 November 1695** in Calais. This is the most specific primary-sourced date available, though most English-language secondary sources give the birth year as 1688, 1689, or 1690 (believed to be during the Nine Years' War). The 1695 date from a surviving Calais birth record should be treated as more reliable than the round-number guesses in secondary sources.

**Family background:** Born to a wealthy bourgeois family. His grandfather had maritime connections and gave him early nautical training. Some sources describe his father as a merchant. His family had the resources to educate him well.

**Education:** Described as excellent. He acquired navigation, seamanship, and possibly classical languages. One source describes him as becoming an architect — this appears to be a romanticised addition. His genuine education and middle-class origins set him apart from many pirates of his era.

### Early Career

**Naval service:** He served as a midshipman (Garde de la Marine) in the French Navy during the War of the Spanish Succession (1701–1714). He obtained a **letter of marque** from King Louis XIV, becoming a French privateer targeting Spanish shipping in the Caribbean.

**War injury:** During the war, he sustained a wound or injury that left a scar over one eye, eventually causing partial and then complete blindness on that side. He subsequently adopted an eye patch. This is consistent across sources and appears in the official naval records referenced by some researchers. The eye patch became iconic in pirate mythology and some sources (probably mythologising) credit La Buse with popularising the eye patch as a pirate symbol.

**Post-war:** When the Treaty of Utrecht ended the war in 1713–1714, Levasseur was discharged like thousands of other privateers. He chose not to return home and instead turned to piracy around 1715–1716.

## SECTION 2: PIRATE CAREER — SHIPS AND ALLIANCES

### Early Piracy in the Caribbean (1716–1718)

**Alliance with Hornigold and Bellamy:** Around 1716, Levasseur joined the pirate company of **Benjamin Hornigold** in the Bahamas, based at Nassau. He commanded a sloop called the **Postillion** (8 guns). He became associated with **Samuel "Black Sam" Bellamy**, one of the most celebrated pirates of the era, and the broader **Flying Gang** based at Nassau.

**Timeline of the Bellamy–Levasseur partnership:**
- In August 1716, Levasseur and Bellamy jointly expelled Hornigold from Hispaniola, irritated by Hornigold's refusal to attack English ships.
- From mid-1716 through early 1717, Bellamy and Levasseur "were the terror of the Caribbean," reportedly capturing an estimated fifty ships together.
- On 9 November 1716, Levasseur was documented flying a flag "with a death's head and bones across" from his mast when capturing the sloop *Bonetta*.
- In September 1716, they fought a French frigate between Puerto Rico and St. Thomas.
- Around January 1717, the partnership dissolved. Some sources say they separated in a storm; others say Levasseur's crew voted to strike out independently near La Blanquilla island.
- Bellamy died in a storm off Cape Cod, Massachusetts, on 26 April 1717, taking most of his crew with him. La Buse's separation from him was thus fortunate.

**Independent operations (1717–1718):** Levasseur appeared at Nassau in May/June 1717 commanding a 20-gun, 250-ton vessel with a 200-man crew. By November 1717, he had a 26-gun ship with 250 men, flying a white ensign depicting a dead man. **On 12 June 1718**, HMS *Scarborough* (Captain Francis Hume) attacked La Buse at anchor near **La Blanquilla**, Venezuela. La Buse cut his anchor cables and escaped under cover of darkness with about sixty surviving crew members on a small sloop. This was a significant setback — he lost his large ship.

**Ships commanded in the Caribbean period:**
- *Postillion* (sloop, 8 guns) — his first documented command
- *La Louise* — commanded in the Caribbean; wrecked off Cotinga Island, Brazil, following a 1718 grounding
- Various unnamed prizes

### West Africa Operations (1718–1720)

After the HMS *Scarborough* encounter, Levasseur made his way to the West African coast.

**Cocklyn and Davis alliance:** By early 1719, he had allied with **Thomas Cocklyn** and **Howell Davis**, operating in the Sierra Leone river mouth. From April 1719, they jointly attacked and captured several vessels in the Sierra Leone estuary. Their most notable joint action was the **attack and destruction of Fort São João Batista de Ajuda**, a Portuguese fortress at **Ouidah** (in modern Benin). This is documented in Portuguese records.

**Davis's death:** Howell Davis was killed in an ambush in May 1719 at the island of Príncipe. After this, Levasseur and the others dispersed.

**Mozambique Channel shipwreck:** In 1719 or 1720, Levasseur was shipwrecked in the **Mozambique Channel** while heading toward the Indian Ocean. He was stranded on **Anjouan** (Johanna Island), one of the Comoros Islands. **Edward England** found him there and rescued him. This is how the England–Levasseur alliance formed.

### Indian Ocean Operations (1720–1721)

**New base:** From 1720 onward, Levasseur established his base at **Île Sainte-Marie** (now Nosy Boraha), a small island off the northeast coast of Madagascar. This was the era's premier pirate haven, used by Henry Avery, William Kidd, Christopher Condent, and many others. Its natural harbour gave protection and access to Indian Ocean shipping lanes.

**The England–Taylor–Levasseur trio:** Levasseur operated alongside **Edward England** and then **John Taylor**. They looted the Laccadive Islands (off India's southwest coast) and sold plundered goods to Dutch merchants for approximately **£75,000**. This is documented.

**Cassandra incident:** In August 1720, England and Levasseur attacked the East India Company ship *Cassandra*, commanded by Captain **James Macrae**, near Anjouan. After a hard three-hour battle (England lost approximately 90 men), Macrae was forced aground. Taylor, who wanted to kill Macrae, was furious that England showed him mercy. Taylor organised a crew vote that **deposed England from command** and voted Taylor his replacement. England was marooned on Mauritius with three loyal men and eventually made his way to Madagascar, where he died in poverty. Taylor took command of the *Cassandra* (renamed by the pirates). He then met Levasseur and gave him a prize ship renamed **Victory**. Together, Taylor (on *Cassandra/Fancy*) and Levasseur (on *Victory*) sailed toward Réunion.

**John Taylor:** English pirate, former Royal Navy lieutenant. Active in Indian Ocean from 1718–1722. His ship at the time of the Cabo heist: *Cassandra* or *Fancy* (38 guns, 280 crew). He was the dominant tactical commander of the partnership.

**Jasper Seagar:** A third pirate captain, associated with England and Taylor. He is confirmed present at the Nossa Senhora do Cabo raid. The Portuguese Viceroy's own report named Seagar commanding the *Fancy*, Levasseur commanding the *Victory*, with Taylor as quartermaster (though Taylor's precise role at the heist varies between sources). Seagar died of unknown causes shortly afterward while at Madagascar.

## SECTION 3: THE NOSSA SENHORA DO CABO HEIST (April 1721)

### The Ship

Full name: **Nossa Senhora do Cabo e São Pedro de Alcântara** (Our Lady of the Cape and Saint Peter of Alcantara). In French sources: **La Vierge du Cap** or **Notre Dame du Cap**. In English sources most commonly: *Nossa Senhora do Cabo*. The name "Notre Dame du Cabo" appears to be a hybrid form used by some popular sources.

**Ship specifications:** Built as a Dutch man-of-war (circa 1710), originally carrying 72 cannons, 800 tons displacement, two gun decks. Purchased by Portugal in 1717. In 1721 it served as the flagship of the Portuguese Indian squadron.

### The Storm and Vulnerability

The *Nossa Senhora do Cabo* had sailed from **Goa** bound for **Lisbon** in early 1721, carrying the outgoing Viceroy and Archbishop plus their personal fortunes. En route, it was **struck by a severe storm** in the Indian Ocean. The crew were forced to **jettison all 72 of its cannons** overboard to prevent the ship capsizing. The mast, rudder, and hull were also damaged. The crippled vessel anchored at **Saint-Denis, Île Bourbon (Réunion Island)** for emergency repairs.

### Date of the Attack

There is a documented discrepancy:
- **April 8, 1721**: The date most commonly cited in historical accounts, and the date when some sources say the pirates arrived and spotted the ship.
- **April 20, 1721**: The date given by the Shipwreck Center's archaeological research for when the pirates spotted the ship anchored.
- **April 26, 1721**: Cited in the French Wikipedia article on the attack (*Prise de la Vierge du Cap*) as the actual date of capture.

The most defensible interpretation: The pirates arrived at Réunion approximately April 8; they may have observed the ship for some days while it was being repaired; the actual boarding and capture occurred **April 26, 1721**. The French Wikipedia source and several detailed analyses use April 26.

### The Attack

Levasseur (on *Victory*) and Taylor (on *Cassandra*/renamed *Fancy*) sailed into Saint-Denis harbour. The pirates initially disguised their approach — some sources say the crew ashore assumed they were legitimate British vessels. The *Nossa Senhora do Cabo*'s crew had many men ashore for repairs. With no cannons and a skeleton crew aboard, the ship could offer virtually **no resistance**. The pirates boarded without significant combat. This was an opportunistic strike, not a battle.

### Passengers and Their Fates

**Dom Luís Carlos Inácio Xavier de Meneses, 5th Count of Ericeira** — outgoing Viceroy of Portuguese India, returning to Lisbon. He was held and **ransomed for 2,000 rix-dollars** (some sources say £400 paid by the Bourbon Island governor). He was released and sheltered by Governor Beauvolier de Courchant for months before being transported to Mozambique. He eventually returned to Portugal but was **received in disgrace and banished from court for 10 years** — apparently held responsible for the loss of the treasure.

**Dom Sebastião de Andrade** — Archbishop of Goa (Patriarch of the East Indies). He was also aboard. His fate after the attack is undocumented; what happened to him "isn't clear" according to the Smithsonian's account.

**Approximately 200 enslaved Africans from Mozambique** were aboard. Their fate is undocumented.

The pirates **did not rob individual passengers** — such was the scale of the official cargo that the personal possessions of passengers were beneath their interest.

### The Treasure: What Was Taken

Contemporary estimates and later analyses give the following inventory:

**Precious stones:**
- Uncut diamonds (some sources say 110 diamonds, 250 emeralds, 20 rubies, 20 sapphires from the 2025 wreck archaeology)
- Pearls

**Precious metals:**
- Gold bars
- Silver bars
- Dozens of chests of golden guineas / gold coins

**Luxury goods:**
- Asian silks
- Spices
- Porcelain (Chinese export)
- Artworks
- Ivory furniture and objects

**Religious artefacts from Sé Cathedral, Goa:**
- Sacred vessels
- Gold crosier encrusted with rubies
- The **Flaming Cross of Goa** (see below)

**The Flaming Cross of Goa:** Described as a crucifix of **pure gold, seven feet tall, encrusted with diamonds, rubies, and emeralds**, so heavy that **three men were needed to carry it**. It supposedly came from Sé Cathedral in Goa. Levasseur is said to have personally claimed this cross as his share. **Historical caveat:** No historical cathedral inventories from Goa mention such a cross. The academic paper "Demystifying La Buse's Cryptogram and the Fiery Cross of Goa" (published via University of Tartu, author Carola Dahlke, 2024) examines this and finds the evidence thin. The cross first enters the record through La Roncière's 1934 book and may be a literary elaboration. The weight calculation (pure gold at that size) produces an impossibly heavy object. This does **not** mean treasure wasn't taken from Goa's churches — it means the specific cross as described may be legendary rather than factual.

### Value Estimates

- £800,000 to £1,000,000 in 1721 terms (cited by multiple contemporary and near-contemporary sources)
- Approximately **$138 million** in 2025 terms (cited by Smithsonian and archaeology.org based on the wreck discovery)
- 4–5 billion euros (cited by some treasure hunt promotional sources — this is almost certainly inflated)
- Each ordinary crew member reportedly received **£50,000 in golden guineas plus 42 diamonds**

This heist is consistently described as **one of the most valuable pirate captures in history**, possibly the most valuable single seizure ever.

### The Ship's Fate After Capture

The pirates towed the *Nossa Senhora do Cabo* approximately 400 miles west to **Île Sainte-Marie** (now Nosy Boraha), Madagascar. There, La Buse took command of it, had it careened and refitted, reduced its cannon to 60 guns, and **renamed it the *Victorieux*** (Victorious). He used it as his flagship for a period. Eventually the pirates abandoned and **burned the ship**, sinking it in the harbour of Îlot Madame, near Sainte-Marie. This wreck was **confirmed by archaeology in July 2025** (see Section 9).

### Division of the Spoils

After the heist, **Taylor and Levasseur separated**. Taylor went back toward the West Indies and eventually reached **Panama by 1722–1723**, where he negotiated a pardon from the Governor of Portobello in exchange for his ship. What became of Taylor's enormous fortune is unknown — he vanishes from the historical record, presumably living in comfort somewhere. Levasseur retained his share in the Indian Ocean.

## SECTION 4: LEVASSEUR'S LATER CAREER (1721–1730)

After the Nossa Senhora do Cabo heist, Levasseur had enough wealth to retire several times over. What he actually did for the next nine years is only partially documented.

### Amnesty Negotiations (1724)

In 1724, a **general amnesty for Indian Ocean pirates** was declared by France. Levasseur sent a **negotiator to the governor of Bourbon Island** to explore terms. However, the French government's condition was that he must **return the stolen treasure**. Levasseur refused this condition and rejected the amnesty.

He then **secretly settled in the Seychelles archipelago**, living quietly and avoiding naval patrols. Some sources say he took multiple wives under local custom. He remained relatively inactive as a pirate in this period, operating on a much reduced scale as French naval pressure increased.

### Settlement in Madagascar

By approximately 1728–1729, Levasseur had moved to **Antongil Bay (Baie d'Antongil)** on the northeast coast of Madagascar. He was working as a **harbour pilot** — a skilled job guiding ships through difficult entrances — under an assumed identity. This was a common fate for aging or retired pirates.

### Capture (1729)

In 1729, Levasseur boarded the French merchant vessel **La Méduse** in Antongil Bay, offering his services as pilot. The captain, **Captain Dhermitte** (described in some sources as a notorious slave trader), **recognised him**. Some sources suggest Dhermitte was specifically looking for La Buse. Levasseur was arrested and put in irons.

He was transported in chains to **Île Bourbon (Réunion)** to stand trial. He arrived in early 1730.

## SECTION 5: CAPTURE AND TRIAL

### Trial

The trial took place before the **Superior Council** of Île Bourbon at **Saint-Denis**, the colonial capital. Levasseur was interrogated on **15 May, 20 May, and 3 June 1730**. He was identified in the proceedings as "a native of Calais known by the alias La Buse."

**Charges:** Piracy. Evidence came from survivor testimonies of crews and passengers he had attacked over his career, principally the 1721 *Nossa Senhora do Cabo* raid, and attacks on East Indiamen and other merchant vessels.

**Conviction:** On **3 July 1730**, the Superior Council condemned Levasseur:
- To perform **public amende honorable** (public penance) at the main door of Saint-Denis parish church
- Then to be **executed by hanging** in the public square behind Gabriel Martin Hospital
- His body to be displayed on a gibbet as a deterrent

The archival record of the conviction exists: **Archives Nationales d'Outre-Mer (ANOM), COL C3/5/002, ff. 120–121**.

### Statements at Trial

No dramatic statements made by Levasseur at trial are documented in historical sources. One later tradition (from French sources) mentions that while being escorted to execution across the **Ravine à Malheur** bridge, he told his guards: *"With what I have hidden here, I could purchase the entire island."* This appears in regional oral tradition and later chronicles but is not in the trial record.

## SECTION 6: EXECUTION

### Date and Location

**Date:** 7 July 1730.
**Time:** 5:00 PM.
**Location:** There is a confusion in many sources between Saint-Denis (where the trial was held) and the actual execution location.

The execution took place at **Saint-Paul** (not Saint-Denis), in the public square behind Gabriel Martin Hospital. Multiple French sources, Find a Grave, and the detailed blogpost reconstruction at bcbrooks.blogspot.com confirm **Saint-Paul** as the execution location. Some sources conflate the location of the trial (Saint-Denis) with the location of the hanging (Saint-Paul).

The execution sentence specifies the Levasseur was to perform his amende honorable at the church in Saint-Denis before being taken to Saint-Paul for hanging. This sequence explains the confusion.

### The Cryptogram Legend at the Execution

**What popular sources say:** As Levasseur stood on the scaffold with the noose around his neck, he tore a necklace from his throat containing a folded piece of paper with 17 lines of coded symbols, and threw it into the crowd, shouting: *"Mes trésors à qui saura comprendre"* ("My treasures to he who will know how to understand").

Variant formulations of what he supposedly said:
- "Mes trésors à qui saura comprendre" (most commonly cited)
- "Mon trésor à qui saura comprendre"
- "Mon trésor à qui saura le déchiffrer"
- "Pour celui qui le découvrira" (For he who discovers it)
- In English: "Find my treasure, whoever can understand it" / "My treasure for he who understands"

**What the historical record actually says:** No contemporary account of the execution — no eyewitness report, no official record, no newspaper article — mentions the cryptogram, the necklace, or the scaffold speech. The story first appears in writing in **Charles de La Roncière's 1934 book**. Modern historians of piracy regard the legend as **a 20th century fiction** (possibly a 19th century oral tradition that La Roncière committed to print and elaborated).

**The body:** After execution, Levasseur's body was "exhibited by the seaside" on a gibbet as a deterrent to other pirates.

**The grave:** A monument in the Cimetière Marin de Saint-Paul, Réunion, is popularly identified as La Buse's grave and is a tourist attraction. This is **impossible** — the cemetery was not established until 1788, 58 years after the execution. Research in 2010 revealed the tombstone on the "grave" actually belonged to **Delphine Hélod**, a formerly enslaved woman who died in 1836; her stone had been turned over, concealing its true inscription.

## SECTION 7: THE CRYPTOGRAM / CIPHER

### Origin of the Document

The cryptogram was **first published by French historian and archivist Charles Germain-Marie Bourel de La Roncière (1870–1941)** in his 1934 book *Le Flibustier Mystérieux: Histoire d'un trésor caché* (The Mysterious Buccaneer: History of a Hidden Treasure), published by Éditions du Masque.

La Roncière was head of the manuscripts department at the Bibliothèque Nationale de France — a credible scholarly position. He appears to have been contacted by a woman from the Seychelles connected to the Savy family who showed him papers she believed were related to La Buse. He also linked the cipher to **rock carvings discovered at Bel Ombre, Mahé**, which had been exposed by a storm in 1923.

**The "Rose Savy" story:** In 1923, a storm on Mahé exposed carved rocks on the beach near the home of a woman referred to as **Rose Savy** (or Rose Savouy). She (or members of her family) is said to have possessed papers believed to be La Buse's cryptogram, obtained from the Archives Nationales de la Réunion. The chain of custody between 1730 (La Buse's execution) and 1923 (discovery) is entirely unverified. La Roncière apparently saw the cipher through the Savy connection. No direct documentary evidence of the cipher predating La Roncière's 1934 book has ever been found.

### Physical Description

The cryptogram consists of **17 lines of symbols** forming a pigpen cipher (a geometric substitution cipher). Each symbol is a shape derived from grid patterns (tic-tac-toe / pigpen grid), with dots inside or outside the shapes indicating different letters. The cipher alphabet is the variant most associated with **Freemasonry** (also called the Masonic cipher, Napoleon cipher, or Tic-tac-toe cipher). Some symbols in this version can represent either letters or numerals (AEIOU = 1-2-3-4-5; LMNR = 6-7-8-9).

Total character count: approximately 200+ symbols across 17 lines.

**Where the original is held:** Disputed. La Roncière claimed to have seen it; subsequent accounts place it variously in the Archives Nationales de la Réunion, in the possession of the Savy family, or elsewhere. No confirmed museum or archive currently claims custody of an authenticated original.

**Two cryptograms:** A second, different cryptogram (22 lines rather than 17) emerged approximately 50 years after La Roncière published the first. This second version was **proven to be a modern fake** — its imagery borrows anachronistically from known historical sources and its final lines echo Edgar Allan Poe's "The Gold Bug" suspiciously closely. Researcher Nick Pelling identified the probable forger as photographer **Yannick Benaben**, who likely created it for a fictional story ("Les Diamants de Goa") before 2009.

### Decipherment

**La Roncière's decipherment (1934):** He successfully applied a standard pigpen key to the cipher, producing a French plaintext. However, the decrypted text was "rather jumbled French" — a sequence of what appear to be folk recipes, mixed with obscure allusions and partially incoherent passages. One decrypted line reportedly reads something like: *"APREJMEZUNEPAIREDEPIJON..."* ("After taking a pair of pigeons..."). The overall cleartext has been described by cryptographers as containing magical or folk recipes rather than treasure map instructions.

**The "bad copy" problem:** Multiple researchers (particularly Nick Pelling at Cipher Mysteries) have argued that the document La Roncière worked from was already a poor copy — possibly copied multiple times from the original, with rotating symbols, missing dots, and transcription errors that make reliable decipherment impossible without recovering the original.

**Historical linguistic problem:** The presence of the letter **K** in decrypted versions is problematic — this letter was not standard in French until the mid-1800s, supporting either the "bad copy" theory or a later fabrication date.

**Reginald Cruise-Wilkins's interpretation:** Cruise-Wilkins, who obtained the papers in 1947, proposed a very different interpretive framework. He argued the cipher was not a simple substitution system but a multi-layered code referencing:
- The **Twelve Labours of Hercules** (each task to be performed in sequence at Bel Ombre)
- The **Zodiac** (astrological references for orientation and timing)
- The **Clavicles of Solomon** (Solomonic magical texts)
- Masonic symbolism

Under his interpretation, the rock carvings at Bel Ombre correspond to cipher symbols, and completing each "Labour" in sequence would eventually reveal the treasure chamber. He reported understanding 9 or 10 of the 12 Labours before his death.

**Academic assessment:** No academic cryptographer or historian has endorsed the Cruise-Wilkins interpretation as credible. The connection to the Labours of Hercules appears to be post-hoc pattern matching rather than demonstrated decipherment.

### What the Decoded Text Supposedly Says

The most honest summary: after decipherment, the text does not clearly reveal any geographical location. It contains fragments that could be interpreted as location references (some involving astronomical coordinates around 4–6° south latitude, which aligns with the Seychelles) but the text is so garbled that almost any interpretation can be projected onto it.

One site (labuse-facts.com, citing Swiss historian Daniel Krieg) claims a new complete interpretation was achieved circa 2020–2024, describing a "riddle within a riddle" that points to "an island in the Indian Ocean." This has not been independently verified or published in peer-reviewed form.

### Is the Cryptogram Authentic?

**Arguments for authenticity:**
- La Roncière was a credible scholar, not a pulp fiction writer
- The 2025 wreck discovery confirms the scale of the 1721 heist, lending general credibility to the La Buse legend
- The Savy family connection to Réunion colonial records could provide a genuine provenance chain
- Physical rock carvings at Bel Ombre do exist and do correlate with the cipher symbols in some interpretations

**Arguments against authenticity:**
- No mention of the cryptogram, necklace, or scaffold speech in any contemporary source
- 200-year gap in provenance
- La Roncière's own book was published as a "novel" (a "paperback novel" per Wikipedia), blurring the line between research and fiction
- The decoded text's content (folk recipes) makes no narrative sense as a treasure clue
- The Fiery Cross of Goa, central to the treasure story, is not in any Goan church inventory
- The K-letter problem in decryption

**Likely reality:** The heist was real and enormous. Some treasure may genuinely have been buried somewhere in the Indian Ocean by Levasseur or his associates. But the cryptogram as we have it — with its 1934 first attestation and zero contemporary corroboration — is most likely a **20th century literary invention**, perhaps based on a genuine fragment or legend from Réunion Island oral tradition, but embellished beyond any historical reliability.

## SECTION 8: THE TREASURE — THEORIES AND SEARCHES

### Estimated Total Accumulated Wealth

Beyond the Nossa Senhora do Cabo heist, Levasseur accumulated plunder throughout his career:
- Caribbean plunder 1716–1718: significant but unquantified
- West Africa operations 1718–1720: significant but unquantified
- Laccadives raid sold to Dutch for £75,000
- *Nossa Senhora do Cabo* haul: £800,000–£1,000,000

Total accumulated wealth before his capture: potentially well over £1,000,000 in 1721 values, making him one of the wealthiest pirates in history.

### Where Is the Treasure Thought to Be?

**Primary theory: Bel Ombre, Mahé, Seychelles**

This is by far the most developed and active theory, sustained by nine decades of treasure hunting.

**Why Bel Ombre?**
1. Rock carvings were exposed there in 1923 and appear to correlate with cipher symbols
2. Two pirate burials were found nearby (skeletons with gold earrings in the left ear, a pirate identifier)
3. Evidence of constructed underground tunnels has been reported (guarded by tidal mechanisms)
4. Levasseur is known to have spent time in the Seychelles 1724–1728

**The Savy family excavation (1913–1923):** Before Cruise-Wilkins, the Savy family was digging at Bel Ombre Beach. They uncovered the rock carvings and reportedly found early 18th century artefacts.

**Reginald Herbert Cruise-Wilkins (1913–1977):**
- Former British Guards officer, previously living in Kenya
- Obtained the cryptogram papers in 1947 and moved to Mahé
- Began excavating at Bel Ombre beach in **1950**
- Spent 27 years and reportedly over **£24,000–£30,000** of his own money
- Interpreted the cipher as referencing the Twelve Labours of Hercules and various occult-Masonic symbolism
- Found: one gold earring, one copper coin, human bones, pistols, musket balls, porcelain statuettes, two coffins containing pirate skeletons, "a pirate's sarcophagus"
- Reported evidence of underground tunnels with tidal protection mechanisms
- Believed he had completed 9 or 10 of the 12 symbolic "Labours"
- Died in 1977 without finding the main hoard
- His work was done before the Seychelles 1983 Antiquities Act required permits

**John Cruise-Wilkins** (Reginald's son):
- Took over the search in 1988
- Continued the Labours-of-Hercules interpretive framework
- Has found petroglyphs on Mahé island (twelve carved images)
- Found a handmade domino marked "six and two" which corresponds to a reference in La Buse letters
- Stated he is "two Labours from the treasure" and believes he knows where the final cavern lies
- Estimates the treasure at $250 million
- Has had ongoing permit battles with the Seychelles government (250,000 Seychellois rupee licence fee required; government claims 50% of any finds)

**Robert Graf** (American treasure hunter):
- Moved to Mahé in 1984
- Created a man-made lagoon as part of his excavation approach
- Claimed the rock carvings match La Buse's writings and found the "six and two" domino
- Obtained excavation permits at various points (1984–1998, again 2003)
- Still active as of the last reporting

**Secondary theories:**

**Frégate Island, Seychelles:** A significant alternative. The Seychelles News Agency documents a wall at **Anse Parc** on Frégate that is believed to predate the island's first permanent settlers and may be 300+ years old — potential pirate construction. Frégate has a museum dedicated to the La Buse legend with photos, artefacts, and documents. Frégate Island has been a known pirate harbour (documented in McAteer's research with lead water channels and cut stone inscriptions — see the novel-bible research files).

**Réunion Island:** Some argue the treasure is on Réunion itself, not the Seychelles. Key evidence: La Buse's supposed statement at the Ravine à Malheur bridge ("with what I have hidden here, I could buy the whole island"). The article "La Buse's Pirate Treasure: A Billion-Dollar Mystery" (monsieur-de-france.com) argues Réunion is the most likely location, noting the pirates must have unloaded the ship before continuing. A treasure hunter spent 30 years excavating **Ravine-à-Malheur** on Réunion (where engraved rocks were also found) before committing suicide in 1995.

**Rodrigues Island (Mauritius dependency):** Alternative theory supported by Robert Charroux's 1962 research, which connects the treasure to the testament of **Bernardin Nageon de L'Estang**, a corsair who mentioned treasures hidden in Mauritius and Rodrigues.

**Madagascar:** La Buse operated from Île Sainte-Marie and Antongil Bay for years. Some treasure was almost certainly cached there.

**Summary:** No treasure has ever been found at any of these locations despite decades of searching.

## SECTION 9: HISTORICAL SOURCES AND RELIABILITY

### Contemporary Accounts

Primary documentary evidence about Levasseur from his lifetime:
- **French naval records** relating to his privateering career (mentioned in analyses but not individually itemised)
- **Trial records from Île Bourbon, 1730** — the ANOM document (COL C3/5/002, ff. 120–121) confirming conviction and sentence on 3 July 1730
- **Captain Francis Hume's report** to the Admiralty, documenting the HMS *Scarborough* attack at La Blanquilla, June 12, 1718
- **Portuguese Viceroy's report** on the *Nossa Senhora do Cabo* attack (written by the Count of Ericeira) — this document names the pirate ships and their commanders (Seagar on *Fancy*, Levasseur on *Victory*, Taylor as quartermaster)
- **East India Company records** relating to the Cassandra attack (1720) — document Edward England and Levasseur's role
- Scattered records from French colonial archives on Réunion / Île Bourbon

Contemporary English-language summaries: Captain Charles Johnson's **A General History of the Pirates** (1724, second edition 1728) was the foundational reference work on Golden Age pirates. Levasseur receives only passing mention; his major career developments (India Ocean career, the Cabo heist) occurred after the first edition and Johnson's coverage is incomplete.

### Modern Academic Sources

**Best general academic sources on the Golden Age of Piracy (which include Levasseur):**
- Colin Woodard, *The Republic of Pirates* (2007) — focuses on the Nassau period
- David Cordingly, *Under the Black Flag* (1995)
- Marcus Rediker, *Villains of All Nations* (2004)
- Jan Rogozinski, *Pirates!: Brigands, Buccaneers, and Privateers in Fact, Fiction, and Legend* (Da Capo, 1996)

**Specifically on La Buse:**
- Charles de La Roncière, *Le Flibustier Mystérieux: Histoire d'un trésor caché* (Éditions du Masque, 1934) — the foundational text; scholarly but with a romanticised fictional frame
- Jacques Gasser and Baylus Brooks, *Captain La Buse: The Golden Age of Piracy* (ebook, 2021) — described as based on rich archival research
- Cyrille Lougnon, Buti (Gilbert), and Vaxelaire (Daniel), *Olivier Levasseur dit "La Buse" — Piraterie et contrebande sur la Route des Indes au XVIIIe siècle* (2023) — the most recent and likely most rigorous French-language academic treatment; Lougnon discovered the "cairn de la Ravine à Malheur" structure on Réunion
- Gui Viala, *La Buse: Olivier Levasseur, dit La Buse, un pirate dans l'océan Indien* (available via Internet Archive)
- Carola Dahlke, "Demystifying La Buse's Cryptogram and the Fiery Cross of Goa" (University of Tartu, 2024) — peer-reviewed academic paper examining authenticity questions

**Online research resources:**
- Nick Pelling's **Cipher Mysteries** blog (ciphermysteries.com) — the most rigorous cryptographic analysis of the cipher
- **La Buse Facts** (labuse-facts.com) — separates fact from legend systematically; cites Swiss historian Daniel Krieg

### The 2025 Archaeological Discovery

In **July 2025**, researchers from the **Center for Historic Shipwreck Preservation** confirmed the identification of the *Nossa Senhora do Cabo* wreck in the harbour of **Îlot Madame**, near **Nosy Boraha (Île Sainte-Marie)**, Madagascar. Director Brandon Clifford confirmed the identification.

Over **3,300 artefacts** were recovered, including:
- 13 gold coins (minted between 1649 and 1718)
- 1,500+ pieces of Chinese export porcelain
- Religious objects including an ivory plaque with "INRI" inscribed in gold letters, Madonna statues
- ~500 cowrie shells
- Structural timbers consistent with a Portuguese East Indiaman

This confirms the scale and reality of the 1721 heist. The Flaming Cross of Goa was not among the artefacts — consistent with the account that Levasseur personally retained it.

The wreck has been described in reporting from Smithsonian Magazine, Archaeology Magazine, Live Science, the Jerusalem Post, and others (all July–September 2025).

## SECTION 10: THE NAME QUESTION

### "La Buse" vs "La Bouche"

**La Buse** = the buzzard (a bird of prey, *Buteo buteo* in Latin taxonomy). This is the correct and primary nickname. French maritime historians universally use La Buse. The buzzard connotation — swift predator, swoops on prey — perfectly fits his piratical style.

**La Bouche** = the mouth. This appears in a minority of sources as an alternative nickname or as a mis-transcription of "La Buse" by non-French writers unfamiliar with the French word "buse." Some sources say it referred to his ability to verbally attack opponents; this seems like a post-hoc rationalisation of a name variant rather than an independent nickname.

The confusion likely originated because in some handwritten 18th century documents, "buse" and "bouche" could be misread by later copyists.

### "La Buze"

This spelling variant (used by McAteer in his books) is historically attested. It appears in some French colonial documents and in the French academic tradition alongside "La Buse." It reflects the same word — "buse" — in an older or regional orthographic rendering. It is not an error; it is a legitimate historical variant. For the novel, consistency with McAteer's usage ("La Buze") would be historically defensible.

### Portuguese rendering: "O Falcão"

The Viceroy's own report, written in Portuguese, apparently refers to the pirate as "O Falcão" (The Falcon). Falcon and buzzard are both birds of prey, and the Portuguese may have been adapting the French nickname rather than independently coining one.

## SECTION 11: LEGACY AND CULTURAL IMPACT

### Literature

- **Robert Louis Stevenson, *Treasure Island* (1883):** Stevenson used the capture of the *Nossa Senhora do Cabo* as source material — Long John Silver references a fictional "Viceroy of the East Indies" in a passage describing the greatest pirate haul ever. Stevenson combined the Archbishop and Viceroy into a single reference.

- **Charles de La Roncière, *Le Flibustier Mystérieux* (1934):** The foundational La Buse text. Written as a "novel" but presented as historical research. First attestation of the cryptogram.

### Manga and Popular Culture

- **Eiichiro Oda, *One Piece* (1997–present):** Oda has confirmed that La Buse's execution story (as told in La Roncière's 1934 book) directly inspired the opening of One Piece, in which the Pirate King **Gol D. Roger** is executed and his last words — challenging the world to find his treasure — launch the "Great Pirate Era." This is one of the most famous pieces of fiction in the world, making La Buse's legend arguably the direct inspiration for the best-selling manga in history.

### Film

- **Captain Blood (1935):** Errol Flynn / Basil Rathbone. The villain character is named "Levasseur" but is based on **François Levasseur** (a different historical figure, governor of Tortuga 1640–1652), not Olivier Levasseur. The name confusion is common.

## SUMMARY: WHAT IS RELIABLE vs. UNCERTAIN vs. LEGEND

### Well-documented historical facts:
- Levasseur was French, born in Calais (birth records survive, possibly 1695)
- He served as a privateer in the War of the Spanish Succession
- He joined the Nassau pirate community around 1716
- He operated with Hornigold, Bellamy, Davis, Cocklyn, England, and Taylor at various times
- He was involved in the attack on Fort São João Batista de Ajuda, Ouidah, 1719
- He was shipwrecked in the Mozambique Channel and rescued by England
- He operated from Île Sainte-Marie, Madagascar, from 1720
- He and Taylor captured the *Nossa Senhora do Cabo* in April 1721 — this is exhaustively documented, including the Viceroy's own account
- The treasure was enormous: minimally £800,000 in 1721 values
- The ship was renamed Victorieux and later sunk at Île Sainte-Marie — confirmed archaeologically in 2025
- Levasseur rejected the 1724 amnesty rather than return the treasure
- He settled secretly in the Seychelles circa 1724–1728
- He was captured in Antongil Bay, Madagascar, circa 1729–1730 while working as a pilot
- He was tried and convicted in Saint-Denis, Île Bourbon, July 1730
- He was executed by hanging at Saint-Paul, Île Bourbon, at 5 PM on 7 July 1730
- His body was exhibited on a gibbet by the sea

### Disputed or uncertain:
- His exact birth date (1695 per one French source vs. 1688–1690 per many secondary sources)
- The exact date of the *Nossa Senhora do Cabo* attack (April 8, 20, or 26 — most likely April 26 for the actual boarding)
- Whether Levasseur personally claimed the Flaming Cross of Goa (possible; no corroborating source besides the 1934 book)
- Exactly where Levasseur buried or hid his share of the treasure
- The nature and extent of his piracy after 1721 (probably limited)
- His exact words or actions before and during execution
- The exact location of his burial (unknown; the "grave" in Saint-Paul's cemetery is fake)

### Probable legend (originated in or unsupported before 1934):
- The cryptogram-necklace story and the execution speech ("Mes trésors à qui saura comprendre")
- The 17-line cipher as a genuine document created by Levasseur
- The Flaming Cross of Goa as physically described (7 feet tall, 3 men to carry, encrusted cross from Sé Cathedral)
- The specific claim that the treasure is at Bel Ombre, Mahé
- The Labours of Hercules interpretive framework for the cipher
